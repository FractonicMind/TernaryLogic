# Mandated Ternary: A Feasibility Study Confronting the Voltage Domain Crossing Bottleneck Against a 2025 CMOS Baseline

## Device Physics and Engineered State Requirements

The foundational question of Mandated Ternary (MT) is whether its core concept—three distinct, non-volatile, and stable hardware-enforced states—can be realized in matter. This section evaluates the physical plausibility of instantiating the Ternary Logic (TL) states of Proceed (+1), Epistemic Hold (0), and Refuse (-1) as Low Resistance State (LRS), Intermediate Resistance State (IRS), and High Resistance State (HRS) respectively, within a memristive device. The analysis proceeds by first establishing the universal principles of multi-state memory, then focusing on the specific case of tantalum oxide (TaOx), and finally assessing the comparative advantages and risks against the specified 2025 technology baseline.

The primary challenge is not merely the existence of more than two resistance states, but the engineering of a third state that is sufficiently stable, reliably distinguishable, and programmable to serve as a deterministic enforcement mechanism. While binary memories store one bit per cell, emerging Non-Volatile Memory (NVM) technologies have long explored Multi-Level Cell (MLC) operation to increase data density [[8](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9292/14/17/3456), [21](https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acs.chemrev.4c00670)]. Technologies such as Phase-Change Memory (PCM), Magnetoresistive RAM (MTJ), and various forms of Resistive RAM (RRAM) have demonstrated the ability to create multiple, stable conductance levels [[17](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/25/20/6464), [22](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S3050913025000154)]. RRAM, in particular, is noted for its simple structure and scalability, making it a prime candidate for MLC implementation [[109](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3731454), [120](https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/3731454)]. The underlying physical mechanism often involves the formation and rupture of a nanoscale conductive filament within a dielectric switching layer, typically an oxygen-deficient oxide [[102](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/298763339_3-Bit_Multilevel_Switching_by_Deep_Reset_Phenomenon_in_PtWTaOXPt-ReRAM_Devices), [104](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/4357560_A_unified_physical_model_of_switching_behavior_in_oxide-based_RRAM)]. By controlling the length or number of these filaments, or by manipulating the concentration of oxygen vacancies, it is possible to achieve a range of intermediate resistances between the fully formed (LRS) and fully ruptured (HRS) states [[69](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/7729/10817133/10902144.pdf)].

For the MT architecture, the value proposition extends beyond mere data density; it hinges on the creation of a deliberately engineered, mandatory pause state. The "Epistemic Hold" state is not a logical uncertainty but a stable, power-retaining physical condition that must be enforced deterministically by hardware [[55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886)]. This requires an IRS that is robust against stochastic fluctuations, temperature variations, and read-disturb effects. The engineering of such a state is a significant departure from typical MLC-NVM applications where state drift is often managed through error correction codes rather than inherent stability. The key metric for success at this level is the separation between the resistance distributions of the LRS, IRS, and HRS. If the statistical variability (expressed as the standard deviation over the mean, or σ/μ) of the IRS is too large, its distribution will overlap with those of the adjacent states, rendering reliable discrimination impossible [[108](https://escholarship.org/content/qt0t48p35q/qt0t48p35q_noSplash_e0b41ee48d2eba9913d1772c4759f0c6.pdf)]. An unacceptably high variability, defined as σ/μ exceeding 40%, would constitute a direct failure of this layer, as it would prevent the hardware from correctly identifying the state of the cell [[108](https://escholarship.org/content/qt0t48p35q/qt0t48p35q_noSplash_e0b41ee48d2eba9913d1772c4759f0c6.pdf)].

Tantalum oxide (TaOx) has emerged as a leading material system for this purpose due to its excellent electrical characteristics and compatibility with CMOS processing. Research has demonstrated bilayer TaOx memristor devices capable of supporting ternary logic operations, confirming the physical viability of the concept [[13](https://advanced.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/advs.202104107), [123](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8844464/), [124](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/357096232_Ternary_Logic_with_Stateful_Neural_Networks_Using_a_Bilayered_TaO_X_-Based_Memristor_Exhibiting_Ternary_States)]. A typical device stack for a TaOx-based memristor suitable for integration into a 2025 node process might consist of a bottom electrode (e.g., Ta or W), a TaOx switching layer approximately 5-10 nm thick, and a top electrode (e.g., Pt or TiN) [[54](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Resistance-switching-in-Pt-TaOx-Ta-memristors-a-Schematic-of-the-Pt-TaOx-Ta-device_fig1_319558952), [126](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/A-ternary-state-bilayer-tantalum-oxide-memristor-device-and-its-mechanism-a-The_fig1_357096232)]. The switching mechanism is primarily attributed to the migration of oxygen vacancies under an applied electric field, which leads to the electrochemical formation of a metallic Ta-rich filament for the SET (LRS) transition and its thermal oxidation/rupture for the RESET (HRS) transition [[104](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/4357560_A_unified_physical_model_of_switching_behavior_in_oxide-based_RRAM), [133](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4124699/)]. Recent studies using hard X-ray photoemission spectroscopy (HX-PES) have provided direct evidence of this redox reaction, confirming the stability of the high and low resistance states [[135](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/224392428_Highly_Reliable_TaOx_ReRAM_and_Direct_Evidence_of_Redox_Reaction_Mechanism)].

The engineering of the IRS within this system is a critical point of investigation. Several physical models have been proposed:
1.  **Partial Filament Formation/Reset:** By applying a sub-threshold voltage during reset, the filament may be only partially ruptured, leaving behind a residual conductive path that defines the IRS [[68](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s11671-020-03299-9)].
2.  **Dual-Filament Regime:** At certain switching conditions, the formation of multiple, partially connected filaments can create a distinct, stable intermediate resistance level [[65](http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/6245494/10834603/10969850.pdf)].
3.  **Intermediate Oxidation States:** The IRS could correspond to a specific, stable stoichiometry of the TaOx film, representing a middle ground between the fully reduced (filament-forming) and fully oxidized (HRS) states [[135](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/224392428_Highly_Reliable_TaOx_ReRAM_and_Direct_Evidence_of_Redox_Reaction_Mechanism)].

Among these, the model correlating the IRS with a distinct, stable redox state appears most promising for reliability, as it represents a fundamental energy minimum rather than a transient or stochastic configuration. However, achieving consistent and repeatable formation of this state across billions of cells on a chip remains a formidable process integration challenge. The uniformity and reproducibility of the switching behavior are paramount for industrial-scale manufacturing [[101](https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acsaelm.3c00062)].

A crucial aspect of the MT architecture is its long-term reliability. The 20-year operational horizon is a stringent requirement that necessitates rigorous reliability modeling. Data retention, the ability of a programmed state to remain unchanged over time, is a primary concern. Retention is thermally activated, and its long-term behavior is typically modeled using the Arrhenius equation, which extrapolates results from accelerated aging tests conducted at elevated temperatures [[15](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/317955496_Retention_Model_of_TaOHfOx_and_TaOAlOx_RRAM_with_Self-Rectifying_Switch_Characteristics), [19](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40820-025-02035-1)]. For TaOx-based devices, an activation energy ($E_a$) of 1.0-1.2 eV is commonly reported, indicating good thermal stability [[15](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/317955496_Retention_Model_of_TaOHfOx_and_TaOAlOx_RRAM_with_Self-Rectifying_Switch_Characteristics)]. To validate a 10-year retention specification at 85°C with a 95% lower confidence bound, the device must undergo a Temperature-Humidity-Bias (THB) test according to JEDEC standards (e.g., JESD22-A108) for a period sufficient to gather statistically relevant data points [[105](https://eureka.patsnap.com/article/jedec-reliability-test-standards-jesd22-and-beyond), [106](https://www.ti.com/quality-reliability/reliability/testing.html)]. The measured resistance drift must remain within predefined margins for all three states, with the IRS being the most vulnerable due to its metastable nature. The resistance of the HRS, for instance, has been observed to decrease over time due to neutral species heating, highlighting the need for careful characterization of all states [[66](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel7/8784029/8999500/10416883.pdf), [70](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel7/8784029/10423923/10416883.pdf)].

When compared against the 2025 TSMC N2 CoWoS + ReRAM (1T1R) baseline, the discontinuous advantage of the engineered IRS is clear. While the baseline offers a highly mature, scalable, and reliable binary memory solution, it cannot natively enforce a mandatory, hardware-controlled intermediate state without complex software intervention [[74](https://investor.tsmc.com/static/annualReports/2024/english/pdf/2024_tsmc_ar_e_ch5.pdf), [78](https://investor.tsmc.com/sites/ir/annual-report/2022/2022%20Annual%20Report-E.pdf)]. Software-based approaches to implementing a "pause" state would introduce blocking behavior, polling overhead, and latency jitter, directly violating the non-blocking constraint of the TL framework. The MT architecture, by contrast, provides a novel enforcement primitive: a persistent, non-volatile, and parallelizable state that acts as a physical interlock. This is not an incremental improvement but an architectural shift that enables a new class of deterministic enforcement mechanisms unavailable to conventional binary systems. The value is therefore not in performing ternary arithmetic faster, but in creating a fundamentally more secure and predictable execution environment for critical decisions.

In summary, the device physics layer presents a conditional pass. The physical principles for creating a stable, multi-level resistive state in TaOx are well-established. The core risk lies in the manufacturability and long-term reliability of the specifically engineered IRS. Key kill criteria for this layer include: (1) failure to demonstrate an IRS with a σ/μ ratio below the 40% threshold under all operating conditions; (2) projected retention for the IRS falling below the 10-year specification at 85°C with 95% confidence; and (3) inability to consistently fabricate the required device stack with the necessary uniformity. Success at this layer is a prerequisite for proceeding to the next layers of the architectural evaluation.

## Circuit Primitives and Sensing Margins

Assuming a viable device physics foundation, the next layer of evaluation concerns the practical realization of circuit primitives capable of reliably reading the three distinct resistance states and executing logic upon them. This layer assesses the feasibility of building the sense amplifiers, logic gates, and associated support circuitry that form the computational fabric of the MT architecture. The central challenge is maintaining signal integrity in the face of significant noise sources while satisfying the absolute non-blocking constraint. The discontinuous advantage sought here is not necessarily superior computational throughput compared to binary CMOS, but rather the ability to perform these functions in a manner that is intrinsically tied to the deterministic enforcement properties of the underlying memristive device.

The cornerstone of any multi-level memory system is the sense amplifier, which must be able to discriminate between the LRS (~1-10 kΩ), IRS (~100 kΩ - 1 MΩ), and HRS (~1-10 MΩ) [[55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886)]. Due to the inherent process variation and stochastic nature of filament dynamics in RRAM devices, the true resistance of a given state is not a single value but a statistical distribution [[46](https://advanced.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/aelm.202500635), [69](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/7729/10817133/10902144.pdf)]. Therefore, a simple comparator is insufficient. A more sophisticated architecture employing two reference cells—one nominally biased towards the LRS and another towards the HRS—is required to establish three distinct discrimination thresholds [[138](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/400864488_Design_of_multi-level_ReRAM_resistance_states_for_Column-wise_Weight_Value_Inversion_Colwin_to_reduce_MAC_error_of_ReRAM-based_analog_Computation-in-Memory)]. The sizing and design of these sense amplifiers are critical; they must be large enough to overcome several sources of noise, including random telegraph noise (RTN) from individual defects in the oxide, cycle-to-cycle variability, and read-disturb effects from repeated sub-threshold reads that can inadvertently perturb the cell's state [[46](https://advanced.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/aelm.202500635), [55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886)]. A circuit designer tasked with implementing this layer must be able to size the sense amplifiers based on the statistical noise margins derived from device characterization data. The goal is to ensure that the probability of misreading a state is negligible even after trillions of read operations over a 20-year lifespan.

Beyond simple sensing, the architecture requires logic primitives. Research has explored the design of native ternary logic gates, such as AND, OR, and NOT, using memristors, often leveraging the principle of stateful logic where the output depends on both the input signal and the device's current resistive state [[28](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/9640614), [29](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/9214881/), [31](https://dl.acm.org/doi/fullHtml/10.1145/3611315.3633268)]. For example, a ternary AND gate might be designed such that a high input voltage (representing a '1') combined with an IRS (representing '0') results in a SET operation, driving the cell to LRS ('1'), whereas the same input on an HRS cell (representing '-1') would have no effect [[34](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/6287639/6514899/11165748.pdf)]. While academically interesting, these designs are still in their infancy and have not yet demonstrated a competitive advantage in terms of speed, area, or energy efficiency over equivalent binary CMOS implementations for general-purpose computation. Furthermore, developing a complete set of 16 primitive logic functions for ternary systems adds significant complexity to the design ecosystem [[34](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/6287639/6514899/11165748.pdf), [35](https://dl.acm.org/doi/abs/10.1145/3731245)].

Given these challenges, a more pragmatic approach is the hybrid architecture, where the memristive crossbar array is used exclusively for storing the ternary states and enforcing the "Epistemic Hold" interlock, while surrounding binary CMOS logic handles all other computational tasks [[55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886)]. This strategy leverages the strengths of both technologies: the non-volatility and stateful properties of the memristors for enforcement, and the maturity, predictability, and performance of CMOS for computation. This approach avoids the "emulation tax" of trying to simulate ternary logic in binary hardware. Simulating the TL decision pipeline in software on a binary CMOS processor would require a state machine to poll the status of the ternary cells, introducing unbounded latency and blocking the main execution path—a clear violation of the non-blocking constraint [[55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886)]. In contrast, the MT architecture uses a dedicated, parallel logging path to manage the confirmation process, keeping the primary execution lane free [[55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886)]. This parallelism is the source of its discontinuous advantage.

The discontinuous advantage of the hybrid approach is the hardware-enforced, non-blocking nature of the "Epistemic Hold" state. This is a novel security and reliability primitive that cannot be replicated with software alone. The overhead in terms of area and energy is substantial, involving the memristor crossbar itself, charge pumps for write pulses, and Analog-to-Digital Converters (ADCs) for reading the resistance states. However, this overhead is a necessary cost for enabling a fundamentally safer execution model. The key is to quantify this overhead and determine if it is justified by the architectural benefits.

Verifying the non-blocking constraint is paramount. The entire design philosophy rests on the premise that no execution path can be stalled indefinitely. The parallel logging path for handling the NL=NA interlock must have its own worst-case execution time (WCET) that is provably bounded. The specification calls for a dual-lane timing hard specification: the execution lane WCET must be ≤ 2 ms at the 99.99th percentile, while the logging lane must have a hard ceiling of 300 ms with a jitter (σ/μ) of ≤ 10% [[55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886)]. A circuit designer must be able to perform a full static timing analysis (STA) on both lanes, considering all process, voltage, and temperature (PVT) corners, to prove that these bounds are met. Any path that contains a loop dependent on external events or that lacks a guaranteed exit condition would be considered blocking and would cause this layer to fail.

In conclusion, this layer passes conditionally. The design of non-blocking sensing circuits is feasible, and the hybrid architecture presents a viable path forward. The primary risk is that the overhead introduced by the memristive components (area, energy, timing) erodes the performance benefit gained from parallel execution, potentially making the overall system slower or less efficient than a well-optimized binary CMOS solution for certain workloads. Another risk is that the noise margins are too small, leading to unreliable state sensing and a high bit error rate over the device's lifetime. The critical success factor is the successful co-design of the device and circuit layers to maximize the sensing margin while minimizing the overhead. The discontinuous advantage over the baseline is real and compelling—it is the provision of a deterministic, non-blocking enforcement primitive—but its practical utility depends entirely on whether this overhead can be managed within acceptable limits.

## NL=NA Physical Interlock Architecture and Voltage Domain Crossing Overhead

The "No Log = No Action" (NL=NA) interlock is the heart of the Mandated Ternary (MT) architecture, providing the physical instantiation of the Ternary Logic (TL) "Epistemic Hold" state. This section provides a detailed architectural description of the NL=NA mechanism and performs a deep analysis of its most critical vulnerability: the voltage domain crossing between the low-voltage CMOS logic (0.75V N2) and the high-voltage confirm pulse (1–3V) required to release the hold. The evaluation of this layer determines whether a secure, tamper-evident, and non-blocking enforcement mechanism can be built, and whether the systemic overheads introduced by this mechanism undermine its architectural advantage.

The NL=NA interlock operates on a simple but powerful principle: a memory cell holding an Intermediate Resistance State (IRS)—the physical representation of "Epistemic Hold"—will not transition to the "Proceed" (LRS) state until it receives a valid, authenticated "confirm pulse" on a dedicated hardware wire [[55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886)]. This creates a mandatory, hardware-enforced pause. The execution path of the main logic continues, making the mechanism non-blocking, while a parallel logging path is responsible for generating and verifying the confirm pulse [[55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886)]. This design satisfies Constraint 1 by ensuring that the wait state is not a software-induced stall but a stable physical condition.

To ensure the security of this mechanism, a multi-layered defense-in-depth approach is required. The first line of defense is a window comparator circuit that monitors the incoming confirm pulse. This comparator checks that the pulse voltage falls within a tight tolerance (e.g., ±5%) and arrives within a specified timing window (e.g., ±10%) relative to the expected event [[55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886)]. Pulses outside this window are rejected as invalid. However, a sophisticated attacker could attempt to spoof the pulse by precisely mimicking these parameters. To counter this, a second, more advanced defense mechanism is needed: RC signature spoof detection [[55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886)]. The rise time of the confirm pulse arriving at the interlock circuit should match the expected RC time constant of the dedicated interconnect wire, calculated from its known length and parasitic capacitance. An attacker attempting to tap the wire or inject a pulse from an off-chip source would inevitably alter the effective RC characteristics, resulting in a measurable change in the pulse's rise time. By comparing the measured rise time to a precise threshold, the circuit can detect such tampering attempts [[55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886)]. This method provides strong evidence of physical integrity for the confirm pulse path.

Despite these security measures, the architecture is subject to several attack vectors that must be modeled and mitigated:
*   **Confirm Wire Shorting:** If the confirm pulse wire is shorted to VDD or GND, the interlock will never be released. This is a critical failure mode. The system must be designed to detect this fault (e.g., by monitoring the open-circuit voltage on the wire) and respond by permanently locking the cell in the HRS ("Refuse") state, effectively failing-safe. This action must be logged as a severe hardware fault.
*   **Read Disturb Exploitation:** Repeated sub-threshold reads of the IRS could, in theory, generate localized Joule heating or induce subtle changes in the filament, gradually shifting its resistance towards the LRS over time [[55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886)]. The engineered IRS must be designed to be inherently immune to this effect, likely by choosing a physical configuration (e.g., a stable redox state) that is not easily perturbed by low-energy stimuli.
*   **Analog Fault Injection:** The system must be resilient to external threats like voltage glitches, electromagnetic pulses (EMP), or extreme temperatures. These attacks could potentially force a state transition or corrupt the internal state of the sense amplifier. The design must incorporate robust isolation and error detection to identify and recover from such events, again likely by failing to "Refuse" (HRS).
*   **Degraded Mode Operation:** Upon detecting any of the above attacks or faults, the system's response must be clearly defined. Simply alerting is insufficient. The correct degraded mode is to enter a fail-safe state, which for the NL=NA interlock is to refuse the action by transitioning the cell to HRS and preventing any further execution until a full system reset and re-verification can occur [[55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886)].

The most significant and challenging aspect of the NL=NA architecture is the voltage domain crossing tension. The N2 CMOS logic operates at a nominal VDD of 0.75V, while the TaOx confirm pulse requires a much higher voltage, typically in the range of 1–3V [[55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886), [78](https://investor.tsmc.com/sites/ir/annual-report/2022/2022%20Annual%20Report-E.pdf)]. This mismatch introduces a triad of systemic overheads that must be quantified and evaluated.

First, the **energy overhead** is substantial. Generating a 3V pulse from a 0.75V supply is not trivial. It requires a charge pump circuit. Charge pumps are notorious for their inefficiency and significant area consumption. The energy required to operate this charge pump must be added to the total energy budget of the MT cell. If the energy cost of generating and transmitting the confirm pulse is greater than the energy saved by the subsequent power-down of the execution unit, the entire energy advantage of the architecture collapses. This overhead must be calculated and compared directly against the energy consumption of a comparable binary CMOS implementation performing the same logical function.

Second, the **timing margin degradation** is a critical timing closure challenge. A charge pump has a finite settling time; it takes a non-zero amount of time to build up the high voltage from the low supply. This adds latency to the generation of the confirm pulse. Furthermore, the high-voltage pulse propagating through the interconnect and being interpreted by the low-voltage logic gates of the interlock circuit will introduce additional propagation delays. All of this extra latency must fit within the pre-defined WCET budget of the logging path (≤ 300 ms). Any unexpected delay could push the system out of specification, causing it to fail timing and potentially violating the non-blocking guarantee if recovery actions become too slow.

Third, the **reliability impact** is perhaps the most insidious threat to the 20-year operational lifetime. Exposing transistors designed for a 0.75V maximum rating to transient voltage spikes of up to 3V is a severe stress. This can accelerate numerous degradation mechanisms, including Time-Dependent Dielectric Breakdown (TDDB) in gate oxides and Hot Carrier Injection (HCI), which degrades transistor mobility and lifetime [[78](https://investor.tsmc.com/sites/ir/annual-report/2022/2022%20Annual%20Report-E.pdf)]. The cumulative effect of these stress events over millions or billions of operations could significantly reduce the Mean-Time-To-Failure (MTTF) of the surrounding CMOS logic, threatening the core requirement of 20-year state retention and operational integrity. The impact on device reliability must be modeled and quantified, likely through extensive stress testing and lifetime projection models.

In conclusion, this layer passes conditionally. The conceptual design of the NL=NA interlock is sound and provides the core enforcement primitive that differentiates MT from binary systems. However, its practical viability is critically threatened by the voltage domain crossing overhead. The primary risk is that the combined energy, timing, and reliability costs of interfacing the low-voltage logic with the high-voltage memristive world completely negate the architectural advantage. The key to passing this layer is demonstrating that this overhead can be contained within acceptable limits through careful circuit design, layout, and process selection. If the overhead proves too great, the entire project fails, as the fundamental mechanism is rendered impractical.

## Hardware Root of Trust and Manufacturing Provenance

A trustworthy autonomous system must have an immutable and verifiable chain of trust that begins at the silicon level and extends through its operational lifetime. This section evaluates the proposed Hardware Root of Trust architecture for Mandated Ternary (MT), focusing on the generation of a unique, unclonable identity via a Physical Unclonable Function (PUF) and the establishment of a secure manufacturing provenance chain. The objective is to verify that every decision made by the TL framework can be cryptographically anchored to a specific, authentic piece of hardware, thereby preventing vendor capture and enabling full auditability.

The foundation of the trust chain is a die-level identity generated from post-manufacturing physical entropy, not from factory-programmed values [[50](https://faculty.sist.shanghaitech.edu.cn/faculty/zhoupq/teaching/fall18/Guest-Lecture/Nonvolative-AnChen-2016.pdf), [93](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3591464)]. This is a critical distinction. A system whose identity is burned in by the foundry reduces trust to a single point of failure—the foundry itself—and is vulnerable to vendor capture, where a malicious manufacturer could embed backdoors or override policies [[75](https://investor.tsmc.com/sites/ir/annual-report/2024/2024%20Annual%20Report_E.pdf)]. The MT architecture mandates a PUF that exploits the intrinsic, uncontrollable randomness of the semiconductor fabrication process to create a unique "fingerprint" for each die [[95](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Jiliang-Zhang-2)]. Memristive devices are an exceptionally promising source for such a PUF. The stochastic nature of filament nucleation sites, the precise dimensions of grain boundaries, and the initial distribution of oxygen vacancies are all infinitesimally varied between devices, creating a natural source of entropy [[46](https://advanced.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/aelm.202500635), [47](https://iopscience.iop.org/article/10.1088/2631-7990/ae053a/pdf)]. A memristor-based PUF can be implemented using a small array of identical but mismatched RRAM cells; their relative resistance rankings when first powered up produce a unique, stable bitstring [[45](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9126908/)].

For this PUF to be effective, it must meet two stringent requirements:
1.  **Uniqueness:** The Hamming distance between the PUF responses of any two different dies must be high. A target of >49% is specified to ensure that the identities are practically unique and resistant to cloning attempts [[45](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9126908/)]. If the average Hamming distance is lower, the PUF fails as a reliable identifier, and this constitutes a program-level kill condition.
2.  **Reliability:** The PUF response must be stable and repeatable across the full operating range of temperature (0-125°C) and voltage (±10%). The intra-die bit error rate (BER) must be kept below a very low threshold, specified as <1% [[45](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9126908/)]. High BER would make it impossible to derive a consistent key from the PUF, rendering it useless for cryptographic applications. The PUF circuit must incorporate error correction codes (ECC) to mask these minor variations and produce a clean, reliable output.

Once a reliable PUF is fabricated on the die, the next step is to bind its public key to the physical hardware. This is achieved through a secure provisioning chain managed by the foundry. At wafer sort, before the dies are packaged, the foundry generates a public-private key pair for the PUF. The foundry cryptographically signs the PUF's public key using its own master private key [[63](https://investor.tsmc.com/sites/ir/annual-report/2023/2023_Annual_Report_E.pdf), [75](https://investor.tsmc.com/sites/ir/annual-report/2024/2024%20Annual%20Report_E.pdf)]. This signed attestation is then recorded in a secure database along with detailed manufacturing provenance data: the fab date, process node (N2), wafer lot number, and the precise X-Y coordinates of the die on the wafer [[78](https://investor.tsmc.com/sites/ir/annual-report/2022/2022%20Annual%20Report-E.pdf)]. This act of signing establishes an unbreakable link between the physical entropy of the die (the PUF) and its manufacturing origin. The escrow of the foundry's master private key in a trusted third-party escrow service is also a critical requirement to ensure the longevity of the trust chain, preserving the attestation capability even if the foundry ceases operations [[142](https://www.iso.org/obp/ui/#iso:std:iso-iec:17065:ed-1:v1:en:sec:3)].

This hardware identity and provenance information becomes an integral part of the TL framework's audit trail. When the system makes a decision—whether to "Proceed" or "Refuse"—it logs the event. Crucially, this log entry is then cryptographically signed using the die's private PUF key [[50](https://faculty.sist.shanghaitech.edu.cn/faculty/zhoupq/teaching/fall18/Guest-Lecture/Nonvolative-AnChen-2016.pdf)]. This binding ensures that any future audit can verify that the log entry was indeed generated by this specific, authentic piece of hardware and originated from this specific wafer lot and fabrication run. The complete trust chain is as follows:

```
PUF (Post-manufacturing physical entropy, unique per die)
    |
    v
Foundry Attestation (PUF public key signed by foundry at wafer sort)
    |
    v
Hardware Identity (Fab date, node, wafer lot, die coordinates)
    |
    v
NL=NA Interlock (Confirms the "Epistemic Hold" state is hardware-enforced)
    |
    v
Immutable Log Entry (Cryptographically signed by PUF private key)
    |
    v
Merkle Hash Chain (Ensures the integrity of the entire log history)
    |
    v
TL Framework Attribution (ORCID: 0009-0006-5966-1243)
```

This architecture satisfies the core requirements for a hardware root of trust. The PUF provides unclonable identity, the foundry attestation provides provenance, and the cryptographic signing of log entries provides immutability and authenticity. A hostile reviewer must be able to trace any log entry from its final Merkle root back to the original PUF signature, then to the foundry's attestation, and ultimately to the specific manufacturing parameters recorded at wafer sort. Any break in this chain would represent a catastrophic failure of the trust architecture.

The primary risk at this layer is the physical implementation of the PUF. While memristor-based PUFs show great promise due to their small area, low power, and inherent randomness [[98](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/368161593_A_ReRAM-based_physically_unclonable_function_with_bit_error_rate_05_after_10_years_at_125C_for_40nm_embedded_application), [99](https://intech-files.s3.amazonaws.com/a043Y00000yuj5oQAA/0014829_Authors_Book%20%282024-06-20%2010%3A28%3A37%29.pdf)], they are still an emerging technology. The main challenge is ensuring that the uniqueness and reliability specifications are met at scale and across all operating conditions. Variability in the fabrication process could lead to PUFs with poor Hamming distance, while sensitivity to temperature and voltage could result in a BER that exceeds the 1% limit, requiring excessive ECC and reducing the usable key length. Extensive characterization and testing are required to validate the PUF design. If a manufacturable and reliable memristor PUF cannot be demonstrated, this layer fails, and the entire trust infrastructure collapses.

In summary, this layer passes conditionally. The proposed Hardware Root of Trust architecture is logically sound and aligns with best practices in hardware security. The innovative use of a memristor PUF is well-suited to the overall theme of the MT architecture. The critical success factor is the successful demonstration of a PUF that meets the stringent requirements for uniqueness (>49% Hamming distance) and reliability (<1% BER). Failure to meet these metrics is a program-ending failure.

## Comparative System-Level Emulation Tax and Overall Viability

To determine the ultimate viability of the Mandated Ternary (MT) architecture, it is essential to move beyond individual component-level comparisons and evaluate its performance at the system level. This involves calculating the "emulation tax"—the overhead incurred when implementing a desired functionality in one system versus another. Specifically, we must compare the native MT approach against two alternatives: a purely software-based implementation on a conventional binary CMOS processor and a hybrid model that combines MT's enforcement capabilities with binary computation. This analysis provides a holistic view of MT's discontinuous advantage and identifies the primary bottleneck that governs its overall feasibility.

The core of the MT advantage is not computational speed or density, but the provision of a deterministic, non-blocking hardware enforcement mechanism for the "Epistemic Hold" state. Any comparison must therefore focus on the cost of implementing this specific enforcement behavior. Let's define the scenario: a decision pipeline that must pause execution for authorization verification before proceeding. We will analyze the cost in terms of latency and energy.

**Implementation A: Software Simulation on Binary CMOS**
In this baseline scenario, the ternary state is simulated using software state machines running on a binary CMOS processor. The system would periodically poll the status of a memory location that holds the desired state. If the state is "Hold," the main execution thread enters a tight loop, consuming CPU cycles and stalling progress. This approach is fundamentally flawed because it introduces blocking behavior, directly violating the non-blocking constraint. The latency is unbounded, depending on the polling frequency and scheduler priority. The energy cost is high, as the CPU remains active during the entire hold period. This method represents the problem that MT is designed to solve and is therefore not a viable option for this application domain.

**Implementation B: Native MT Architecture**
Here, the ternary state is stored in a memristive crossbar cell. The execution lane consists of binary CMOS logic that can proceed freely. The enforcement is handled by the parallel logging lane, which includes the NL=NA interlock circuit [[55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886)]. When the cell is in IRS, the interlock holds the state. The logging path runs independently to generate and verify the confirm pulse. This approach is non-blocking by design. The latency is bounded by the WCET of the logging path (e.g., 300 ms max), and the energy cost during the hold period is minimal, as the memristor retains its state without power and the main execution unit is idle but not spinning in a loop. The overhead comes from the memristor crossbar itself, the sense amplifiers, and the charge pump for the confirm pulse. However, this overhead is paid once to enable a fundamentally safer and more predictable execution model.

**Implementation C: Hybrid Approach**
This is a pragmatic version of the native MT architecture. The memristive crossbar is used solely for storing the ternary state and enforcing the interlock. All other computational logic is performed by the surrounding binary CMOS. This avoids the immense complexity of designing a full native ternary logic family. The emulation tax here is the cost of communicating between the binary CMOS and the memristive array: reading the state requires an ADC, and writing requires a DAC and the high-voltage charge pump. The key advantage remains the non-blocking nature of the enforcement.

The critical insight from this comparison is that the discontinuous advantage of MT is not in computation, but in enforcement. The value is the elimination of software-induced blocking stalls in favor of a stable, hardware-enforced pause. The "tax" of implementing this in binary is the unbounded latency and high energy consumption of a busy-wait loop. The "cost" of implementing this in MT is the fixed, bounded overhead of the memristive hardware. For deterministic systems, the latter is almost always preferable.

Let's perform a worked numerical example to quantify this. Assume a decision pipeline where an "Epistemic Hold" of 100 ms duration is required.

*   **Software Simulation Cost (Implementation A):**
    *   **Latency:** Unbounded, but let's assume a polling interval of 1 µs. The average wait time is 50 µs, but the worst case is 100 ms. This violates the WCET constraint.
    *   **Energy:** Assume a 1 GHz CPU with an activity factor of 1 during the hold. Energy = $P \times t = (CV^2f) \times t = (\alpha \times C \times (0.75V)^2 \times 1GHz) \times 0.1s$. Even with a conservative $\alpha C$ of 1 fF, this is $(1 \times 1fF \times 0.5625 V^2 \times 10^9 Hz) \times 0.1s = 56.25 \mu J$. This is the cost of a 100ms block.

*   **Native MT Cost (Implementation B/C):**
    *   **Latency:** Bounded by the logging path WCET, e.g., 300 ms. This satisfies the non-blocking constraint.
    *   **Energy:** The hold state is maintained by the passive memristor. The dominant cost is the charge pump energy to generate the confirm pulse. Assume a pulse energy of 1 nJ. Total energy = 1 nJ. The CPU can be in a deep sleep state, consuming nanoamps.

The energy saving is dramatic: from microjoules to nanojoules. The latency is traded from unbounded to bounded and predictable. This demonstrates the tangible advantage of the hardware-enforced approach.

However, this advantage is entirely contingent on overcoming the previously discussed challenges, particularly the **voltage domain crossing overhead**. The 1 nJ pulse energy is just one component. The larger overheads are the area and power of the charge pump, the timing delay it introduces, and the reliability damage it inflicts on the surrounding CMOS. If the charge pump consumes 10% of the chip's dynamic power and occupies 5% of the area, and its delay forces the entire system clock to run 20% slower, then the net gain is erased. The overhead must be quantified and shown to be acceptable.

The following table summarizes the comparative system-level costs.

| Metric | Implementation A (Software) | Implementation B (Native MT) | Implementation C (Hybrid MT) | Advantage of MT (vs. A) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Latency** | Unbounded (Violates Constraint) | Bounded (e.g., < 300 ms) | Bounded (e.g., < 300 ms) | Yes (Deterministic, non-blocking) |
| **Energy (during Hold)** | High (CPU active, ~µJ) | Very Low (Memristor passive, ~nJ) | Very Low (Memristor passive, ~nJ) | Yes (Orders of magnitude lower) |
| **Area Overhead** | None | Unknown | Significant (Crossbar, ADCs, Pump) | No (Higher area) |
| **Design Complexity** | Low | Very High | High | No (Higher complexity) |
| **Security** | Low (Vulnerable to DoS) | High (Hardware-enforced) | High (Hardware-enforced) | Yes (Tamper-resistant) |

Ultimately, the overall viability of MT hinges on a single, overarching trade-off. The architecture offers a powerful, discontinuous advantage in the form of a deterministic, non-blocking enforcement primitive. This is a compelling feature for safety- and security-critical systems. However, this advantage comes at the cost of increased design complexity, area overhead, and, most critically, the multifaceted overhead of the voltage domain crossing. The primary determinant of MT's success is whether the gains in reliability, security, and energy efficiency from the hardware-enforced hold outweigh the penalties imposed by the inefficient and stressful interface between the low-voltage logic and high-voltage memory worlds. If the voltage domain crossing overhead proves to be the dominant factor, it will render the MT architecture impractical despite its elegant enforcement concept. This is the central, decisive finding of this layered analysis.

## Phase 1 Conclusion: Pass/Fail Determinations and Program Inheritance

This concludes the first phase of the technical evaluation of Mandated Ternary (MT), a hardware architecture for enforcing a three-state decision framework in deterministic autonomous systems. The assessment proceeded layer-by-layer, from fundamental device physics to the system-wide root of trust, adhering strictly to the defined constraints and requiring a discontinuous advantage over the fixed 2025 TSMC N2 CoWoS with embedded ReRAM (1T1R) baseline. This final section synthesizes the findings from each layer, delivers a definitive pass/fail determination, and outlines the precise conditions that will be inherited by Phase 2. It is critical to note that a failure at one layer does not terminate the entire research program, but rather forecloses the investigation path corresponding to that failed layer in Phase 2. Other layers that passed remain available for development.

The core hypothesis of MT—that a third, mandatory hardware-enforced state can be built into matter—was subjected to rigorous scrutiny. The analysis revealed that while the concept is physically plausible, its practical realization is fraught with significant challenges that threaten to undermine its architectural advantage. The most critical finding to emerge from this phase is the systemic overhead associated with the voltage domain crossing between the low-voltage (0.75V) CMOS logic and the high-voltage (1-3V) confirm pulse required to manipulate the memristive states. This overhead manifests as energy waste, timing margin degradation, and accelerated reliability loss, and represents the primary bottleneck for the entire architecture.

The following table presents the pass/fail determination for each evaluated layer, along with the justification and key inherited constraints.

| Layer | Evaluation Focus | Pass/Fail | Justification and Key Findings |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Layer 1: Device Physics** | Can a stable, distinguishable, and reliable Intermediate Resistance State (IRS) be engineered in a memristive device to serve as the physical "Epistemic Hold"? | **Conditional Pass** | The physical principles for multi-level operation in TaOx are established [[13](https://advanced.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/advs.202104107), [123](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8844464/)]. The primary risk is insufficient state separation (high σ/μ) or unacceptably short IRS retention [[108](https://escholarship.org/content/qt0t48p35q/qt0t48p35q_noSplash_e0b41ee48d2eba9913d1772c4759f0c6.pdf)]. |
| **Layer 2: Circuit Primitives** | Can circuits be designed to reliably sense the three states and perform logic, while satisfying the non-blocking constraint? | **Conditional Pass** | Non-blocking sensing is theoretically possible with proper sense amplifier design. The discontinuous advantage is the enforcement mechanism, not the logic itself [[55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886)]. The primary risk is that overhead (area, energy) erodes the performance benefit. |
| **Layer 3: NL=NA Interlock** | Can a secure, tamper-evident, and non-blocking physical interlock be built around the IRS state? | **Conditional Pass** | The core concept of the interlock is sound. ### Summary of Key Falsifiability Conditions (Kill Criteria)

The following ten conditions, had they been met, would have resulted in a definitive failure at their respective layers, terminating that investigation path.

1.  **TaOx IRS Variability:** The standard deviation over the mean (σ/μ) for the engineered Intermediate Resistance State (IRS) exceeded 40% under all specified operating conditions, rendering reliable discrimination from adjacent states impossible [[108](https://escholarship.org/content/qt0t48p35q/qt0t48p35q_noSplash_e0b41ee48d2eba9913d1772c4759f0c6.pdf)].
2.  **Insufficient Retention:** Arrhenius extrapolation of accelerated aging tests (minimum 10 years at 150°C) showed that the 95% lower confidence bound for the IRS resistance drift exceeded the specification limits at 85°C for the 10-year mark [[15](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/317955496_Retention_Model_of_TaOHfOx_and_TaOAlOx_RRAM_with_Self-Rectifying_Switch_Characteristics), [106](https://www.ti.com/quality-reliability/reliability/testing.html)].
3.  **No Discontinuous Advantage:** The native MT architecture demonstrated no quantified, discontinuous advantage over the TSMC N2 CoWoS + ReRAM baseline at any tested layer. Incremental improvements were deemed insufficient justification for the added complexity [[74](https://investor.tsmc.com/static/annualReports/2024/english/pdf/2024_tsmc_ar_e_ch5.pdf), [78](https://investor.tsmc.com/sites/ir/annual-report/2022/2022%20Annual%20Report-E.pdf)].
4.  **Blocking Enforcement Path:** Any proposed enforcement mechanism for the "Epistemic Hold" state was found to introduce unbounded latency or blocking behavior in the primary execution path, violating the absolute non-blocking constraint [[55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886)].
5.  **Failed Non-Blocking Timing Analysis:** A full static timing analysis (STA) proved that the WCET for any enforcement-related path could not be bounded at the 99.99th percentile with a jitter (σ/μ) below 10% across all PVT corners [[55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886)].
6.  **Unachievable Tamper Evidence:** The proposed tamper-evidence mechanisms, such as RC signature spoof detection for the confirm pulse, were found to be non-viable within the BEOL thermal budget or were susceptible to sophisticated analog spoofing attacks [[55](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2633540923019886)].
7.  **PUF Unreliability:** The proposed memristor-based PUF failed to meet the critical reliability criterion: the intra-die bit error rate (BER) exceeded 1% across the specified temperature (0-125°C) and voltage (±10%) ranges [[45](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9126908/)].
8.  **PUF Uniqueness Failure:** The inter-die Hamming distance of the memristor PUF was measured to be below the 49% threshold, rendering the generated identity unreliable and insecure [[45](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9126908/)].
9.  **Voltage Domain Crossing Overhead Dominance:** The combined energy, timing, and reliability overheads of the voltage domain crossing (specifically, the charge pump and high-voltage interfacing) were quantified to be so severe that they eliminated any remaining architectural advantage of the MT design over the binary baseline.
10. **Broken Trust Chain Link:** It was determined that any link in the proposed Hardware Root of Trust chain (PUF -> Foundry Attestation -> Immutable Log) could not be completed within the specified constraints, breaking the end-to-end verifiability of the system [[50](https://faculty.sist.shanghaitech.edu.cn/faculty/zhoupq/teaching/fall18/Guest-Lecture/Nonvolative-AnChen-2016.pdf), [75](https://investor.tsmc.com/sites/ir/annual-report/2024/2024%20Annual%20Report_E.pdf)].

### Phase 2 Inheritance

Based on the conditional passes in Phase 1, the following investigation paths are open for Phase 2. Each path inherits the constraints and assumptions validated in this phase.

*   **Investigation Path 1 (Device Physics):** Phase 2 may proceed to investigate the detailed process integration and yield of the TaOx memristive stack. It may assume the successful demonstration of a physically plausible device capable of forming three distinct resistance states. It must investigate how to minimize σ/μ and maximize the retention of the IRS through materials engineering and programming algorithms. It must also investigate methods to mitigate the reliability impact of the 1-3V pulses on the surrounding CMOS.
*   **Investigation Path 2 (Circuit Primitives):** Phase 2 may proceed to design and simulate the hybrid circuit architecture, including the sense amplifiers, ADCs, and the charge pump. It may assume the existence of a device with a reasonable sensing margin. It must focus on optimizing the trade-offs between sensing accuracy, power consumption, and area to ensure the non-blocking constraint is met.
*   **Investigation Path 3 (NL=NA Interlock):** Phase 2 may proceed to create a detailed physical implementation of the NL=NA interlock circuit, including the window comparator and RC spoof detection circuitry. It must perform a detailed co-simulation of the interlock with the charge pump and the surrounding 0.75V logic to accurately quantify the voltage domain crossing overhead. This path is the most critical, as it addresses the primary risk identified in this report.
*   **Investigation Path 4 (Hardware Root of Trust):** Phase 2 may proceed to design and characterize the memristor PUF and the secure provisioning protocol. It must assume a reliable hardware identity source. It must focus on meeting the stringent uniqueness and reliability specifications under all environmental stresses.

Conversely, any of the ten kill criteria listed above would have terminated the investigation. For example, if Layer 1 had failed due to unmanageable IRS variability, Phase 2 would not be permitted to explore circuit designs based on that device. Similarly, if Layer 3 had failed due to insurmountable voltage domain crossing overhead, Phase 2 would not be allowed to integrate the NL=NA interlock into a full system simulation. This disciplined, pass/fail methodology ensures that resources are not wasted on pursuing dead ends and that only the most promising concepts are advanced to the next stage of development.
