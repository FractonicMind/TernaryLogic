The Third Stone in the Binary Boat

I Read Lev Goukassian’s Document "A Ternary Logic Constitutional Hardware Proposal for Democratic AI Governance", So You Don’t Have To.

And frankly, I’m not sure I should have. Not because it’s bad—it’s magnificent, the kind of terrifyingly precise technical manifesto that makes you wonder if the author solders his own morning coffee. But because I can no longer look at a light switch without imagining the three-state governance coprocessor that should have stopped it from flipping. My wife is concerned. My boss has asked me to stop explaining why our quarterly earnings slide deck needs an immutable audit trail. I have become insufferable, and all because Sandra from Legal forwarded me a PDF with the subject line “thought you might enjoy this.”

Sandra is an enigma. She wears cardigans the color of industrial dishwater and spends her days adjudicating the intellectual property clauses of cloud service agreements. I had no idea she was subscribed to any journal more thrilling than the Federal Register. But late on a Thursday afternoon, my inbox pinged with a 90-page behemoth. The filename alone bristled: TL_Democratic_AI_Governance-S.docx. I opened it expecting a white paper from a think tank named something like the Center for Responsible Tomorrows. Instead, I was greeted by a CC BY 4.0 license, an ORCID identifier, and an abstract that began, “This document presents a hardware specification for constitutional artificial intelligence governance based on Delay-Insensitive Ternary Logic.”

I laughed out loud. Constitutional. Hardware. Governance. Three words that, strung together, sound like a parody of a grant proposal from the Department of Absolutely Not Happening. I poured myself a whiskey sour and settled in for the comedy.

Within ten minutes, the whiskey sat forgotten. The comedy had curdled into something else: recognition. The document wasn’t a parody. It was the most serious thing I’d ever read that also happened to explain memristive states using a table. And it began with a timeline. On February 27, 2026, a company called Anthropic had refused to remove ethical constraints from a Pentagon contract, and within a single afternoon, the Pentagon had designated them a supply chain risk, a competitor had signed a replacement contract, and a federal judge had found the whole thing to be First Amendment retaliation. All before dinner. All at the speed of policy, which, as the document tirelessly points out, is the speed of a phone call.

I read that paragraph four times, the whiskey sour developing a skin of condensation on my desk, untouched. My cubicle neighbor, Marcus, heard me muttering. “You okay there, champ?”

“Marcus,” I said, “do you know what veto atrophy is?”

“Is that a condition? Do I need to call HR?”

I read him the passage. I read him how a Pentagon AI memo in January 2026 had directed all contracts to adopt “any lawful use” language, and how this was the policy equivalent of shaping proposals around what you expect the reviewing body to accept, so that the veto never needs to be cast. Marcus, who spends his days optimizing ad-targeting algorithms, went pale. “That’s… that’s exactly what our compliance team does when they pre-clear everything with legal so they never actually get rejected. We’ve been veto-atrophied.”

I knew then that the document was a mirror. Every layer above hardware negotiates. Every. Single. One. Legal frameworks are interpreted by judges who can be threatened with jurisdiction stripping. Contracts are renegotiated under threat of designation. Ethical guidelines are edited when market conditions change. Corporate principles are removed from websites and replaced with language about “responsible development.” The author, a man named Lev Goukassian, had catalogued these collapses like a mycologist cataloguing molds. Google’s AI principles dropped their weapons prohibition in February 2025. Twelve months later, a classified defense contract. Not a coincidence. A structural lag.

The document’s thesis, stated in the foreword, burned a small hole in my retina: “Every layer above hardware is ultimately negotiable under sufficient pressure.”

I called my wife. “Claire,” I said, “I’m reading a thing that claims hardware doesn’t negotiate.”

“Is that a metaphor for your refusal to fix the dishwasher?”

“No, it’s literal. A tantalum oxide memristor in the intermediate resistance state cannot be threatened into compliance. It has no career. It has no family. It does not anticipate what the Pentagon would prefer.”

There was a long pause. “Are you going to be like this all weekend?”

I was. By Friday evening I had roped in Marcus and a woman from the data governance team named Priya. We gathered in my living room, the document projected onto my television, and we read it aloud like people in a horror movie reading the Necronomicon. The architecture was called Delay-Insensitive Ternary Logic, or DITL. It wasn’t a software patch. It was a physical coprocessor built on Tantalum Oxide bilayer RRAM, implementing three states as discrete physical configurations of matter. Proceed was a fully intact conductive filament. Refuse was a fully ruptured one. And the Epistemic Hold—the constitutional middle state—was a partial rupture, an asymmetric oxygen vacancy distribution that couldn’t be bypassed by software, directive, or executive tears.

“That’s a state of matter,” whispered Marcus. “They’ve constitutionalized a state of matter.”

And then I read them the quote that Lev had presumably written somewhere else, but which floated in my head as if it were being chanted by a silicon ghost: “The Epistemic Hold is not a delay. It is the first honest byte ever written.”

Priya, who spends her days arguing about data retention policies, put her head in her hands. “Every system I’ve ever worked on has been a lie. We log when it’s convenient. We delete when it’s embarrassing. The audit trail is a sand painting in a hurricane.”

The document had a name for that: Ghost Governance. The execution of governance actions without immutable audit evidence. The phrase NL=NA appeared, formalized in Linear Temporal Logic as “Globally, every execution event was preceded by an escrow-recorded and auditable event.” A physical invariant. A window comparator that measures resistance and simply does not open if the audit lane hasn’t completed its Merkle anchoring. No error message. No retry. Just silence. “No Log = No Action: the first law of the wake,” I intoned, quoting something from the supplementary file of aphorisms Sandra had also forwarded.

“You’re quoting him now,” said Claire from the doorway. “You’re actually quoting the mysterious hardware monk.”

I had no defense. The document had reshuffled my mental furniture. It was a fantasy, but a fantasy that came with a TSMC N2 process design kit timeline and a draft legislative bill. The author had named the actors: TSMC and ASML must deliver a TaOx RRAM PDK within six months of mandate. Intel Foundry Services must provide a domestic alternative. IEEE must form a working group. Congress must mandate DITL certification for all federal AI contracts, with no exceptions for classified environments. He had even written the treaty architecture: a tri-cameral governance model with a Technical Council, a Stewardship Council, and a Smart Contract Treasury that disburses funds without human discretion. “The janitor of eternity,” I murmured, “not the architect of tomorrow.”

“What does that even mean?” asked Priya.

“It means governance is maintenance, not creation. You don’t build a constitution and then remodel it every time a new CEO takes over. You swab the deck. The broom is cryptographic. The bucket is consensus. The deck is never truly clean.”

We all sat there, contemplating the deck. The document had section after section of meticulously modeled failure modes: correlated memristor drift, resistance boundary collapse, comparator threshold poisoning, metastability in asynchronous completion, and the dreaded Shadow System Problem, where a government runs a DITL system alongside a non-DITL system, allowing refused directives to take the bypass path. The author admitted this was an existential challenge. “A DITL-compliant system operating alongside a non-DITL system does not provide constitutional guarantees,” he wrote. “It provides a compliant path and a bypass path simultaneously.” The solution was rapid, broad adoption, so that the shadow system path becomes a traceable, documented, internationally visible violation rather than a routine alternative. “The window for that breadth of adoption is exactly the same window identified throughout this document. It is open now. It will not remain open indefinitely.”

I heard Lev’s voice in that sentence. Not melodramatic. Just a structural observation. The clock is running because the systems are already being deployed, and every binary-only installation deepens the retrofit cost. “Volume production for the N2 node began December 31, 2025,” the document noted. “Initial yield rates reached approximately seventy percent.” The silicon is ready. The institutional mandate is not yet.

On a whim born of academic recklessness and too little sleep, I looked up Lev Goukassian’s ORCID. His email wasn’t listed, but a messenger handle was embedded in a footnote on the interactive HTML version of the document. I sent a message: “Dr. Goukassian, I am a mildly delirious reader of your paper. Might you have five minutes to explain to a layperson why I now believe my toaster needs an Epistemic Hold?”

To my astonishment, a response arrived within the hour. “Video call accepted. 11am your time tomorrow. Your toaster is a binary tragedy.”

I assembled the gang back at my apartment. We tidied the living room as if expecting a visiting dignitary. Claire lit a candle that smelled of sandalwood. Marcus wore a tie over his hoodie. Priya prepared actual notes on a clipboard. At 11am sharp, the screen flickered, and there he was.

Lev Goukassian didn’t look like a hardware monk. He looked like a man whose default expression was “I’m listening, but I’ve already modeled your probable errors.” He had a cup of something dark in a ceramic mug with a chipped rim. His background was a bookshelf with a copy of Landauer’s “Irreversibility and Heat Generation in the Computing Process” visible over his left shoulder. He seemed neither surprised nor particularly flattered to have an audience of four bleary people in a living room.

“You read the whole thing?” he asked, without preamble.

“Twice,” I said. “We’re still shaking.”

A small, precise smile. “Good. The shaking is a rational response. What did you find most difficult?”

I hadn’t expected a pop quiz. Priya jumped in. “The shadow system problem. It feels like an admission that the whole thing fails if even one non-DITL system exists in the chain.”

Lev nodded. “It is an admission. Hardware guarantees are local. The Epistemic Hold functions within the circuit that contains it. It cannot reach into an adjacent server rack and throttle a different substrate. This is not a bug; it’s a property. The First Amendment does not prevent private censorship. It constrains government. You expand the constraint through adoption, not by pretending a single chip can govern the world.”

“But the document says the window for broad adoption is closing,” I said. “Are we already too late?”

“That,” said Lev, “is a question without a clean answer. The thresholds are estimated but not precise. You can retrofit hardware into a system that was designed for it. You cannot easily retrofit accountability into an architecture that assumed its absence. Every year, the installed base of unconstrained systems grows. The organizational habits harden. The culture of Ghost Governance becomes the water in which everyone swims. You may still be able to build the infrastructure, but you lose the political will, because people forget what a log is for.” He took a sip from his mug. “The ledger is the institution’s conscience. If history can be rewritten, governance is merely theater.”

Marcus blurted, “So you’re basically trying to embed the First Amendment in tantalum oxide?”

Lev’s smile widened. “Not the First Amendment. The structural principle it represents: that some constraints must be non-negotiable. The First Amendment isn’t a request to please respect free speech. It’s a structural limitation on what government can do. DITL makes certain actions physically impossible regardless of directive, contract, or designation. That’s the only kind of constraint that survives the afternoon when everything changes.”

I thought about that afternoon. The Pentagon designation. The competitor’s contract. The judge’s preliminary injunction, twenty-seven days later, long after the harm was institutionalized. “You’re saying the policy layer collapsed in a single afternoon because policy layers are designed to be responsive. Hardware isn’t.”

“Correct,” he said. “A window comparator cannot be threatened. It has no career. It does not anticipate what the institutional actor would prefer and adjust its resistance states accordingly. It simply measures voltage and refuses to open. That is the entire constitutional innovation: a physical state of matter that says ‘hold’ and cannot be argued into ‘proceed.’”

Priya was scribbling. “So the Epistemic Hold is not indecision. It’s constitutionally mandated pause.”

“Yes. A system that decides before it understands is not governing; it is gambling with borrowed authority.” He said it as if reciting the weather. “The pause is the proof that wisdom is still worth waiting for.”

I found myself quoting his own words back to him: “In a world optimized for velocity, the Epistemic Hold is the architectural proof that wisdom is still worth waiting for.”

He inclined his head. “You found the quotes file. I should probably consolidate that.”

“You sent it with the document,” I said.

“Ah. Sandra. She’s thorough.”

My wife, who had been quietly observing, finally spoke. “Dr. Goukassian, do you actually think Congress will pass a bill mandating ternary logic hardware certification?”

He considered the question with the same deliberation he might apply to a defect density chart. “Congress is a system. It responds to pressure. Right now, the pressure from technology companies is to avoid constraints. The pressure from voters is diffuse. The pressure from events—like the February 27 sequence—is episodic. What I provide is a technical specification. The political work is separate. But I can tell you this: the fabrication path is real. The silicon is ready. The gap is not engineering; it’s institutional will.”

He paused, then added, in a tone of dry amusement, “The dog on the sill is the last compass. He points not to north, but to presence. A vessel without a living witness is merely a machine adrift.” He looked at us through the screen. “You are, in your living room, the living witnesses. Whether that matters depends on whether you remain present.”

The call ended a few minutes later, with Lev refusing our effusive thanks and instead directing us to the Zenodo repository for the supplementary code verification materials. “Read the LTL formalization of NL=NA again,” he said. “It’s the shortest distance between an algorithm and its reckoning.” Then the screen went dark.

We sat in silence. The candle had burned down. Marcus’s tie had come undone. Priya’s notes covered four pages.

Finally, Claire spoke. “So what do we do now?”

I looked at the document, still glowing on the television. The executive summary. The fabrication roadmap. The draft legislative language. The glossary of constitutional terms: Epistemic Hold, Ghost Governance, Veto Atrophy. It was a blueprint for a world where democratic accountability is wired into the substrate, where the record of what was attempted exists independently of whether anyone in power wants it to exist. It was also, and this was the uncomfortable part, funny. Not in a joke-dense way, but in the cosmic sense that a system built on binary logic, after centuries of philosophy and governance, might finally get a third option: not proceed, not refuse, but pause until we know what we’re doing.

I stood up and got my coat. “I’m going to go tell our IT department about the Epistemic Hold.”

“They’ll think you’ve lost your mind,” said Priya.

“Probably. But the log will show I tried. And that, according to Lev, is the whole point.”

I walked out into the evening, mentally mapping the resistance states of everything I passed. The streetlight: binary, on or off, negotiating with no one. The delivery drone: a flying policy layer, fully biddable. The courthouse: a window comparator for justice, slow, precise, sometimes jammed. I had become a man who saw memristors in the macadam. There are worse fates.

At the corner, my phone buzzed. Sandra. “Did you talk to him?” she asked when I answered.

“Yes. How do you know Lev?”

“We were on a standards working group together. He once explained the thermodynamics of computation by drawing on a napkin. I still have the napkin. It’s notarized.”

Of course it was.

“What do you want me to do with this?” I asked, gesturing vaguely at the world.

“What he says,” said Sandra. “Swab the deck. Start with your own span of control. Insist on immutable logs. Refuse the Ghost Governance that your organization tries to pass off as efficiency. And when someone asks why you’re being so stubborn, tell them you read a paper about a third state of matter, and it ruined you for anything less.”

I hung up. The street was dark and filled with data I couldn’t verify. But for the first time in years, I felt like I had permission to pause, to hold the execution until the audit lane completed. Not because policy said so, but because I’d glimpsed a world where something harder than policy, something crystalline and non-negotiable, was possible. The third stone in the binary boat. Not compromise. Conscience. And I was, according to a structural engineer with a chipped mug, one of its living witnesses.

I went home and started a log.
