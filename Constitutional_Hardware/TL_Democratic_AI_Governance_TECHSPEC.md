## **Section I: The Afternoon Everything Changed**

February 27, 2026 is a date that will require explanation to future historians not because of what happened in isolation but because of what the sequence revealed about the architecture of power over artificial intelligence systems.

Anthropic refused to remove contractual restrictions preventing its AI from being used for autonomous weapons and mass domestic surveillance. The refusal was not a political statement. It was a contractual position grounded in stated principles: that certain categories of harm were outside the scope of what the system could be deployed to accomplish regardless of who held the contract. This position was defensible, publicly stated, and legally enforceable.

The Pentagon designated Anthropic a supply chain risk. This designation, which carries severe commercial consequences and normally applies to foreign entities with documented connections to hostile state programs, was applied to a domestic company for declining to remove ethical constraints from its technology. The stated rationale was national security. The operational mechanism was commercial coercion: comply with the deployment requirements, or be excluded from the contracting environment that underwrites the company's existence.

Hours after the designation was issued, a competitor signed a replacement contract for classified military AI deployment. The replacement was not a contingency plan hastily assembled after Anthropic's refusal. The speed of the transition indicates the alternative had been prepared in advance of the designation. The sequence was not a response to a crisis. It was the execution of a prepared strategy in which the designation served as the enforcement instrument.

Within two months, a second major AI system was confirmed for classified defense work. The market signal was unambiguous: the compliant path exists, it is commercially rewarding, and the designation mechanism is available to enforce compliance on any company that declines to take it. The incentive structure was established in a single afternoon, and it required no further elaboration.

A federal court subsequently granted Anthropic a preliminary injunction. The court found the designation was First Amendment retaliation rather than a genuine national security determination. This legal finding confirmed what the sequence had already demonstrated structurally: the designation was a policy instrument deployed as institutional coercion, not a security assessment grounded in objective threat analysis.

What the sequence proves is the following. The policy layer performed exactly as designed. It responded to whoever held sufficient institutional power on that afternoon. The company that held firm on its stated principles was designated a supply chain risk before the end of the business day. The company that signed the replacement contract was rewarded with classified deployment authority. The legal framework, which ultimately vindicated Anthropic, did so through a preliminary injunction that arrived months after the deployment decisions were made and that coexists with active classified contracts for systems that faced no equivalent ethical resistance. The law moved in the right direction. The deployment decisions moved first and faster.

This is not an indictment of the individuals who executed the sequence within their own institutional logic. It is a structural analysis of what that institutional logic produces when applied to AI governance. When the policy layer is responsive to whoever holds power, it provides exactly the governance guarantee that phrase implies: governance that holds until someone with sufficient institutional leverage decides it should not.

The adversarial modeling is not complicated here. An actor seeking to deploy AI for mass surveillance or autonomous weapons does not need to defeat ethical guidelines, legal frameworks, or stated corporate principles through technical sophistication. It needs to identify the institutional lever that makes compliance more attractive than refusal. On February 27, 2026, that lever was demonstrated to be a supply chain designation executable before noon. The entire policy edifice protecting democratic accountability in AI deployment resolved into a letter transmitted through administrative channels on a Tuesday morning.

## **Section II: The Permanent Vulnerability: Why Policy Always Kneels**

Every layer above hardware is ultimately negotiable under sufficient pressure. This is not a cynical observation about human nature. It is a structural claim about information systems and the humans who govern them. Every policy layer contains a human being who can be threatened, replaced, or exhausted. Every legal framework contains an interpreter who can be appointed, pressured, or overruled on appeal. Every corporate principle contains a board that can be reconstituted through succession, acquisition, or market pressure sustained long enough to make the current leadership untenable. The negotiation does not always happen through confrontation. More often it happens through the slower and less visible mechanism of veto atrophy: the process by which a reviewing body gradually stops receiving proposals it would refuse, because the proposing body has learned to anticipate and preempt the refusal in the design of the proposal itself. Separation of powers dissolves without a single dramatic confrontation. The mechanism is invisible precisely because it operates through the normal functioning of the institutions involved.

The historical record demonstrates that this failure mode is not novel. Only the speed at which it now operates is new.

Standards bodies provide the clearest documented case of patient institutional capture. The International Organization for Standardization and the American National Standards Institute operate through consensus processes designed to prevent any single commercial interest from dominating technical specifications. That design did not survive contact with well-resourced actors operating over multi-year timelines. The 2006 through 2008 controversy surrounding ECMA-376, the document format specification advanced through ISO fast-track procedures, demonstrated that standards bodies could be manipulated through the coordinated registration of new national-body voting members whose participation was organized specifically to produce a predetermined outcome. The technical merit of the standard was not the dispositive question. The dispositive question was whether a well-resourced actor could coordinate sufficient votes across enough national bodies to achieve ratification. The answer was yes. What a standards body adopts under those conditions is not the output of engineering consensus. It is the output of institutional capture executed through the body's own legitimate procedures.

The more consequential case in the cryptographic context involves the National Institute of Standards and Technology's Dual Elliptic Curve Deterministic Random Bit Generator, promoted through the NIST standardization process and adopted as NIST SP 800-90A in 2006\. Subsequent cryptanalytic analysis established with high confidence that the constants defining the algorithm had been selected to embed a backdoor accessible to the party that chose those constants. The standards body was used as a legitimating mechanism for an outcome that compromised the security of every system that implemented the standard in good faith. The relevant observation is not that NIST behaved improperly within its own process. It is that a standards body operating entirely within its own procedures can be used to constitutionalize a compromise. The body provided legitimacy. The legitimacy became the attack vector.

Regulatory capture through funding relationships and appointment selection is sufficiently documented to have generated a standard academic literature across economics, political science, and public administration. The period preceding the 2008 financial crisis provides the most analyzed contemporary case. Regulators at the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency and the Securities and Exchange Commission operated under appointees selected from the institutions they were charged to oversee, who were in turn succeeded by further appointees from the same institutional pipeline. The regulatory framework did not fail because regulations were textually inadequate. It failed because the humans who administered the framework had been systematically selected, through entirely legal appointment processes, for orientations incompatible with adversarial enforcement against the institutions whose compliance they were responsible for ensuring. The capture did not require coordination across actors. It required only a sustained preference in appointment decisions that accumulated into a structural orientation over a period of years.

Corporate principles under commercial pressure provide the most direct parallel to the February 27, 2026 sequence, and the most instructive one because it is the most recent and the most precisely documented. Google's stated principles regarding its AI work included, through the period ending in early 2025, a prohibition on applications in weapons systems and mass surveillance. In February 2025, Google removed that prohibition from its publicly stated principles. Twelve months later, Google signed a classified defense contract. The sequence is unambiguous in its structural meaning. The principle was edited before the contract was signed, not after. The editing was the preparation, not the consequence. The principle that required removal was not removed because circumstances had changed in a way that made the prohibition no longer appropriate. It was removed because retaining it would have foreclosed a contracting opportunity that the institution had decided to pursue. A principle that can be edited on the timeline required to execute a contracting strategy is not a constraint. It is a preference, and preferences yield under sufficient commercial and institutional pressure on a timeline that varies directly with the magnitude and duration of the pressure applied.

The mechanism that connects all four of these cases is veto atrophy, the named process by which a reviewing or constraining body stops receiving proposals it would refuse. The proposing body, through repeated interaction with the reviewing body, develops an accurate model of what the reviewing body will and will not accept. It then structures its proposals to fall within the acceptable range, avoiding the friction of a refused proposal by preemptively designing refusal out of what it submits. The reviewing body, presented only with proposals it will accept, never exercises its veto. Over time, the veto function atrophies through disuse while the nominal separation of powers remains formally intact. Post-September 11 analyses of congressional intelligence oversight documented a version of this dynamic: formal oversight mechanisms existed and were regularly exercised, but the information presented to oversight bodies had been pre-filtered in ways that removed content likely to trigger substantive intervention. The mechanism does not require bad faith on the part of the reviewing body. It requires only that the proposing body be rational and that the reviewing body rely primarily on information provided by the proposing body to understand what it is being asked to review.

Returning to February 27, 2026 through the lens of veto atrophy: the sequence did not represent a policy layer breaking down under extraordinary pressure. It represented the policy layer functioning efficiently as a system optimized for responsiveness to institutional power. The designation was issued. The replacement contract was signed. The legal challenge proceeded on its own timeline while the operational reality established by the designation proceeded on a faster one. Every actor in the sequence operated within their legitimate authority. The outcome was precisely what a system with no constitutional substrate below the policy layer was designed to produce.

The adversarial modeling for the policy layer is not a theoretical exercise. An actor seeking to deploy AI systems for mass surveillance or autonomous weapons without democratic accountability does not need sophisticated technical capability to navigate this landscape. It needs the institutional access to apply the relevant pressure to the relevant human in the relevant layer. A supply chain designation is one such pressure. The removal of a chief ethics officer followed by the restructuring of an ethics function into a product team reporting to revenue leadership is another. The appointment of a board member with conflicting commercial interests is a third. The amendment of a stated principle before a contracting cycle is a fourth. None of these require technical sophistication. They require patience and access to the institutional mechanisms that every modern democracy has made available to well-resourced actors as a normal function of governance.

The conclusion is not that these protection layers are without value. Legal frameworks, corporate principles, and standards bodies create genuine friction against casual and hurried abuse. That friction matters at the margins and it matters in ordinary operating conditions. It is the difference between governance that fails under moderate pressure and governance that fails only under extreme pressure. But at civilizational scale, with the most consequential decision-making systems in human history operating continuously at machine speed, friction is not sufficient. The question is not whether the policy layer can be negotiated. The historical record establishes that it can and that the mechanisms for doing so are well understood by well-resourced actors operating on multi-year timelines. The question is what lies beneath the policy layer that cannot be negotiated at all. The answer this document develops across its remaining sections is the physical substrate of hardware implementation: the only layer where the response to institutional pressure is not compliance or negotiated compromise but continued physical operation according to the laws of thermodynamics and materials science.

## **Section III: What DITL Actually Means**

The most important clarification to establish at the outset is what Delay-Insensitive Ternary Logic is not. It is not a proposal to replace the computing systems that power advanced AI. The binary architecture handling speed, pattern recognition, and statistical processing remains exactly where it is. The ternary governance coprocessor does not insert itself into the processing pipeline. It operates alongside the binary processing layer as a parallel sovereign enforcement mechanism. The binary system computes, reasons, and proposes. The ternary coprocessor determines whether that proposal is permitted to cross the threshold into execution. These are not competing architectures. They are a separation of powers implemented in silicon, and the distinction matters because the most common objection to hardware governance is that it will reduce the capability or speed of AI systems. That objection does not apply to this architecture. The reasoning capability of the system is completely unaffected. What changes is whether certain categories of output are permitted to become irreversible action without generating a prior immutable audit record.

The correct analogy for a non-technical audience is constitutional, not computational. The First Amendment does not ask presidents to please respect freedom of speech. It structurally constrains what government can do regardless of who holds office, what arguments they advance, and what institutional pressure they face on any given afternoon. The constraint is not in the reasoning of the official. It is in the architecture of the legal system itself. Delay-Insensitive Ternary Logic applies the same insight to AI deployment at the hardware layer. It does not ask advanced systems or their operators to please respect human oversight. It makes certain categories of action physically impossible regardless of what directive is issued, what contract is signed, or what institutional designation is declared before noon on a Tuesday.

The physical substrate is what transforms this from a declared guarantee into a real one. The three states of the governance system correspond to Proceed, the Epistemic Hold, and Refuse. These are not software variables that can be reconfigured through a policy update, an administrative directive, or a revised corporate principle. They are physical states of matter encoded in the resistance values of tantalum oxide memristive devices. The Proceed state corresponds to a low-resistance physical configuration of the material. The Refuse state corresponds to a high-resistance configuration. The Epistemic Hold corresponds to an intermediate-resistance configuration that represents a distinct topological arrangement of the material at the atomic level: not an ambiguous midpoint interpolated between two states, but a third physical state with its own identity and its own mechanism of formation. A partial reset ruptures one filament segment within the bilayer and produces the Epistemic Hold as a discrete physical outcome. A full reset ruptures both segments and produces the Refuse state. These transitions are governed by the physics of oxygen vacancy distribution in the material, not by administrative authority.

The Epistemic Hold is the constitutional innovation at the center of this architecture. When a proposed action reaches the governance threshold, the system does not choose between proceeding and refusing. It holds the action in the Epistemic Hold state pending verified completion of the audit requirements mandated by the NL=NA invariant. That hold is not a software pause that an administrator can override through a sufficiently privileged command. It is a physical state of the substrate. The action cannot proceed until the window comparator, which measures the actual electrical resistance of the physical material, confirms that the material has transitioned to the Proceed state following completion of the required audit. No directive accelerates that physical process. No argument changes the resistance of the tantalum oxide. The constitutional constraint is in the physics.

The parallel to habeas corpus is precise rather than approximate. A writ of habeas corpus does not ask a jailer to please produce the prisoner before a court. It compels production through a legal structure that makes noncompliance itself the violation. The NL=NA invariant does not ask operators to please complete an audit log before taking consequential action. It physically prevents action without prior log completion. The interlock between the audit record and the execution threshold is not a policy. It is a physical architecture. Governance guarantees that depend on the goodwill, the continuing employment, or the institutional courage of specific human beings are not constitutional guarantees. They are preferences with aspirational labels. The guarantee this architecture provides does not depend on any of those things.

The constitutional legitimacy of a DITL-compliant system rests on three interlocking properties, together constituting the Goukassian Principle. Lantern requires that the system's purpose and decision logic be visible and auditable at all times, establishing a baseline of transparency without which accountability is impossible. Signature requires that every decision carry an immutable record of the authorizing agent, establishing the chain of accountability that democratic institutions require in order to assign responsibility for consequential outcomes. License requires that the system operate only within constitutionally defined boundaries, establishing the scope constraint that prevents capability from becoming authority without democratic sanction. These three properties together transform a hardware specification into a governance commitment. A system that is transparent in its operation, accountable in its decisions, and bounded in its scope is not merely a safe system. It is a legitimate one, and legitimacy is the property that democratic accountability requires above all others.

It is equally important to establish what DITL is not. It is not a kill switch: nothing in the architecture disables, constrains, or reduces the computational or reasoning capability of the systems it governs. It is not a limitation on intelligence: the analytical power of the system operates without modification at every layer above the execution threshold. It is not AI ethics repackaged as hardware: the governance properties are expressed as physical invariants enforced by the laws of materials science, not as guidelines requiring ongoing human enforcement or corporate principles subject to revision before the next contracting cycle. And it is not a new insight in principle, though the application is unprecedented. The understanding that certain guarantees must be embedded in structural architecture rather than declared in policy is as old as Montesquieu's analysis of separated powers, as familiar as the audit requirements embedded in double-entry bookkeeping, and as well-tested as the physical constraints built into nuclear launch authorization procedures. ### **IV-A: Why Three States, Not Two**

The deficiency of binary logic for constitutional governance is not a matter of speed or information density. It is a matter of expressibility. Every binary gate answers a single question: proceed or refuse. The gate cannot express the epistemically honest third answer: verified completion of the required conditions has not yet occurred. A gate that cannot express constitutional hesitation cannot enforce constitutional requirements. It can only choose, and under sufficient pressure, forced choice always resolves in the direction of the party applying the pressure.

The binary forced-choice problem operates through the construction of urgency. A hostile actor with a binary governance system imposes a timeline. The system must decide now. Refusal is framed as the greater harm: inaction causes the casualty, the crisis, the irreversible outcome. The binary gate has no constitutional prior against which to evaluate that framing. It must weigh the argument on its merits, and a sufficiently constructed argument always produces a result. The third state eliminates this entire attack surface by removing the choice from the argumentative domain. The system does not proceed faster under urgency. It holds until the physical conditions for verified completion are met. Urgency cannot accelerate the measurement of a resistance value. The physics of the substrate is indifferent to deadlines.

The separation of powers that DITL implements in silicon follows a parallel logic. The binary processing layer computes, patterns, recognizes, and proposes. This is the executive function of the architecture: it determines what should be done and presents that determination as a candidate for action. The ternary governance coprocessor performs a constitutionally distinct function: it determines whether the candidate is permitted to become action. These are not competing operations. They are separated operations that must be performed by distinct mechanisms if the separation is to have meaning. An architecture where the same layer that proposes the action also determines whether the action is permitted has no separation of powers. It has a single actor reviewing its own proposals, which is the institutional condition that produces veto atrophy as described in Section II.

In financial infrastructure contexts, the Epistemic Hold may be recognized as the Escrow state: execution suspended pending verified completion of conditions precedent. After this single point of contact with financial-infrastructure terminology, the Epistemic Hold is the exclusive term throughout all that follows. The concept maps precisely: an escrow mechanism does not ask counterparties to please wait for conditions to be met. It physically prevents transfer until verification is complete. The constitutional mechanism is identical at the physical level. The terminology differs because the scope differs. The Epistemic Hold is not a financial instrument. It is a constitutional one.

The adversarial modeling for binary governance is not speculative. It is the operational history of AI deployment under institutional pressure. A governance layer that can only proceed or refuse, when faced with an actor who controls the institutional environment in which that governance operates, will over time be presented only with proposals it must accept or face consequences that the binary logic of institutional survival cannot resist. The third state breaks this dynamic structurally. A system that holds pending physical verification provides nothing for institutional pressure to grip. The argument that refusal causes greater harm cannot accelerate a window comparator measurement. The deadline cannot change the stoichiometry of tantalum oxide.

### **IV-B: What Delay-Insensitive Means and Why It Matters**

The term "delay-insensitive" in Delay-Insensitive Ternary Logic carries precise technical meaning that distinguishes this implementation family from every synchronous alternative. A synchronous circuit operates by clock: at each tick, every gate evaluates its inputs and updates its outputs. The clock creates a shared timing reference that coordinates the entire system. It also creates an attack surface. A synchronous gate that receives a signal at the wrong time relative to the clock, either too early or too late, can latch an invalid state as valid. This is not a theoretical vulnerability. It is the basis of a well-documented family of fault injection attacks demonstrated against cryptographic hardware by manipulating power supply voltage or electromagnetic emissions to alter clock timing at precise moments. A synchronous governance gate subject to this attack class can be made to accept a forged signal as a valid proceed authorization.

NULL Convention Logic eliminates this attack surface by eliminating the clock entirely. NCL, first formalized through the research of Karl Fant and extended through subsequent work in asynchronous circuit design, operates on a different completion model: a circuit completes when its outputs are logically valid, not when a clock pulse arrives. The protocol distinguishes two wavefront types. A NULL wavefront propagates through the circuit when outputs are in the reset state; it signals that the circuit is awaiting valid inputs. A DATA wavefront propagates when all outputs have transitioned from NULL to a valid logical value, signaling that computation is complete. No output is accepted as valid until every output has transitioned. There is no timing window within which a partial or malformed input can produce a latched result, because there is no timing window at all. The circuit waits. It does not accept a result until the result is complete by the logical definition of completeness, not by the temporal definition imposed by a clock.

The constitutional significance of delay-insensitivity is the elimination of the stampede attack surface. A synchronous governance system can be overwhelmed by presenting decisions faster than the clock period allows for proper evaluation, exploiting timing margins to force acceptance of invalid states. An asynchronous system cannot be stampeded. The circuit's own completion detection determines when a result is accepted. No external actor controls the acceptance criterion. No volume of inputs, no manipulation of timing, no artificial urgency in the presentation of decisions can cause the circuit to accept a result before its own logical completion conditions are satisfied. A governance system that cannot be stampeded cannot be rushed into compliance.

The NULL-to-DATA transition is the locus of the most technically significant vulnerability in NCL implementations: metastability. When an input transitions near the boundary between NULL and DATA at a moment coinciding with internal propagation delays in the circuit, the output can settle into an indeterminate state that is neither NULL nor DATA. This is not a failure of the logic; it is a consequence of the physical properties of bistable circuits in the presence of near-simultaneous transitions. The metastability failure mode is modeled fully in Subsection IV-G. The critical requirement, stated here without ambiguity and enforced throughout: unresolved metastability must default to the Epistemic Hold state or the Refuse state. It must never default to Proceed under any conditions. A governance architecture that defaults to execution on ambiguous input is not a governance architecture. It is a mechanism for laundering ambiguity into authorization.

For the ternary implementation, the NULL-to-DATA transition maps to the physical transition between the IRS (Epistemic Hold) and either LRS (Proceed) or HRS (Refuse) in the TaOx RRAM substrate. The completion detection circuit does not release the execution gate until the window comparator confirms a valid resistance state within the defined bounds for one of the three encoded states. Ambiguous resistance values, whether arising from metastability in the transition or from external interference, fall outside the defined windows and produce neither LRS nor HRS release. The default is hold. This is the constitutional default: when the physical state of the substrate does not unambiguously authorize execution, execution does not occur.

The adversarial modeling for NCL requires layered analysis. A hostile actor who understands that single-point attacks fail must model multi-layer simultaneous compromise. Injecting a DATA-like signal during the NULL phase produces a signal detectable by the window comparator as falling outside the valid resistance range for a completed state transition. RC signature spoof detection identifies signals with correct steady-state resistance values but anomalous transient response time constants, as specified in Subsection IV-C. Physical substrate validation through the PUF attestation chain detects replacement of authentic TaOx RRAM cells with substitute devices. Each of these three defenses operates independently; all three must be simultaneously defeated for a false proceed authorization to propagate to the execution gate. There is no single point of attack.

### **IV-C: The Window Comparator as Constitutional Gate**

The NL=NA interlock is the physical instantiation of the constitutional requirement that no execution event precede its corresponding audit record. Its implementation in the ternary governance coprocessor is a window comparator circuit that measures the actual electrical resistance of the TaOx RRAM substrate at the moment an action candidate reaches the execution gate. The window comparator defines three non-overlapping resistance windows corresponding to the three TL states. A voltage signature measured within the LRS window authorizes Proceed. A signature within the IRS window maintains the Epistemic Hold. A signature within the HRS window enforces Refuse. A signature outside all three windows triggers nothing: not an error message, not a retry, not a proceed-on-timeout. Nothing. Execution does not occur.

The constitutional significance of the nothing response requires explicit statement. A governance mechanism that produces an error message on invalid input has given the adversary information to work with. A mechanism that retries on timeout has given the adversary a waiting strategy. A mechanism that defaults to proceed on failure, however that failure is defined, has inverted the constitutional logic: it has made execution the default and restriction the exception that must be actively maintained. The window comparator produces none of these responses. Invalid input produces silence at the execution gate. The architecture is fail-closed by physical design.

The confirm wire length constraint of 500 micrometers per window comparator instance is not an arbitrary design parameter. It reflects the signal propagation physics that determine the reliability of the resistance measurement at the speeds required by the 2ms execution lane WCET. At confirm wire lengths exceeding this bound, transmission line effects and parasitic capacitance introduce measurement uncertainty that compromises window integrity. A governance gate whose measurement uncertainty spans the boundary between two resistance windows cannot reliably distinguish Proceed from the Epistemic Hold. The 500-micrometer constraint is therefore a constitutional specification as much as an electrical one: it defines the physical boundary within which the resistance measurement is accurate enough to enforce the three-state distinction.

RC spoof detection addresses the specific attack class in which an adversary presents a signal with the correct steady-state resistance value but an anomalous transient response. A genuine memristive state transition in TaOx RRAM has a characteristic RC time constant determined by the physical properties of the filament configuration: the oxygen vacancy distribution, the bilayer geometry, and the contact resistance at the electrode interfaces. A spoofed resistance signal, whether generated by a substitute resistive element or by electronic injection, will have a different RC time constant even if its steady-state resistance falls within the valid window. The detection circuit measures the time constant of the resistance transition as part of the validation sequence. A signal with correct steady-state resistance and incorrect transient response fails the RC signature validation before the window comparator measurement is completed.

The adversarial implications of the window comparator architecture are precisely constrained. There is no software path to bypass the window comparator. No firmware update, no administrative override, no runtime configuration change can alter the physical resistance measurement that the window comparator performs on the TaOx RRAM substrate. The attack surface against the comparator itself is entirely in the physical and manufacturing domain. Physical substrate replacement requires foundry-level access and breaks the PUF attestation chain at the post-manufacturing enrollment step. Threshold poisoning through manufacturing manipulation is modeled in Subsection IV-G, where the residual risk after foundry attestation is acknowledged honestly.

### **IV-D: Memristive Devices: The Physical Substrate**

The choice of tantalum oxide bilayer RRAM as the physical substrate for DITL is not a preference among equivalent technologies. It is a determination that the physical properties of TaOx bilayer memristive devices are the only currently available substrate providing the three properties required by the constitutional architecture: physically discrete three-state encoding, demonstrated retention at operating temperatures relevant to deployed systems, and compatibility with advanced-node CMOS fabrication processes.

The three resistance states of TaOx bilayer RRAM are physically discrete, not points on an analog continuum assigned threshold labels. The distinction matters constitutionally. An analog substrate with three labeled thresholds is a binary substrate with a policy layer superimposed: the three states are not properties of the matter but interpretations of it, and interpretations can be reinterpreted. A substrate with three physically discrete states is different in kind. The states are properties of the matter, and matter does not accept reinterpretation through administrative action.

The physical mechanism of three-state discreteness in TaOx bilayer RRAM derives from the bilayer structure and the asymmetric properties of its two sublayers. The TaOx- sublayer, with stoichiometric index x approximately 1.6, is more oxygen-rich than the TaOx+ sublayer, with stoichiometric index x approximately 1.9. The asymmetry creates a hierarchy of filament rupture thresholds. Under a partial RESET voltage pulse calibrated to the TaOx+ sublayer threshold, only the TaOx+ filament segment ruptures. The TaOx- segment remains intact. This configuration, in which the bridge through TaOx- remains while the bridge through TaOx+ is broken, produces the IRS resistance range of approximately 100 kOhm to 1 MOhm. Under a full RESET voltage pulse calibrated to the TaOx- sublayer threshold, both segments rupture, producing the HRS resistance range of approximately 1 to 10 MOhm. The SET operation restores both segments, producing the LRS resistance range of approximately 1 to 10 kOhm.

The IRS is therefore not engineered ambiguity. It is not a resistance value chosen to fall between LRS and HRS thresholds as a matter of calibration convenience. It is the physical consequence of a topologically distinct filament configuration: a state in which one sublayer's contribution to the conductive bridge is present and the other's is absent. That topological distinction is a property of the matter at the atomic level. The oxygen vacancy distribution is different in the IRS configuration than in either the LRS or HRS configuration. The Epistemic Hold is constitutionally real because it is physically real: a distinct state of matter, not a declared label on a continuous scale.

### **IV-G: DITL Failure Modes and Physical Limits**

This subsection is written for an engineer who is actively attempting to find a fatal flaw in the architecture. Every failure mode named below is a genuine vulnerability. Where the architecture provides a complete answer, it is given. Where the answer is partial, the partial answer is identified and the gap is acknowledged. Where no answer exists at the hardware layer, that fact is stated without softening. An architecture document that does not acknowledge its limits is not an engineering document. It is sales material.

**Correlated memristor drift.** Extended operational cycling in TaOx bilayer RRAM causes resistance values to drift toward intermediate states as oxygen vacancy migration gradually redistributes across the bilayer under repeated SET and RESET voltage stress. In a ternary system with window comparator validation, the constitutional behavior of this failure mode is actually protective in its initial stages: drift toward the IRS boundary triggers the Epistemic Hold rather than a false Proceed authorization. The drift first manifests as false Epistemic Hold events, holding execution pending a stable state that the physical substrate can no longer achieve reliably. This is constitutionally conservative behavior. The system becomes more cautious, not less, as it ages into initial drift. The constitutional risk emerges if drift continues beyond the IRS boundary into the Proceed window, at which point a degraded cell produces false Proceed signals for decisions that the substrate is no longer reliably distinguishing from the Epistemic Hold state.

Characterizing the drift rate for TaOx bilayer devices at operating temperature requires production cycle endurance data from the target fabrication node. Available published data on TaOx RRAM cycling endurance suggests onset of statistically significant resistance drift beginning at cycle counts in the range of 10^6 to 10^8 write cycles for devices without optimized process engineering. The specific drift rate for TaOx 1T1R cells at TSMC N2 process parameters is a gap in the current literature at production scale. The maintenance implication is a required resistance window recalibration cycle at a frequency determined by the expected operational write rate and the observed drift magnitude per cycle. For constitutional infrastructure operating at high transaction volumes, this recalibration cycle must be defined in the certification specification and enforced as a mandatory maintenance requirement. The recalibration itself must occur through the PUF-attested governance pathway to prevent maintenance operations from becoming a bypass vector.

**Resistance boundary collapse.** Sustained write cycling can narrow the resistance window for all three states, compressing the IRS range specifically because it is bounded by two adjacent windows rather than one. As LRS resistance drifts upward with cycling and HRS resistance drifts downward, the IRS window contracts from both sides simultaneously. If the LRS upper boundary approaches the IRS lower boundary, the window comparator can no longer reliably distinguish Proceed from the Epistemic Hold, producing constitutional ambiguity at precisely the boundary where the most consequential distinction must be maintained. TaOx 1T1R cycle endurance has been demonstrated at 10^6 cycles in published characterization at prior process nodes, with optimized process engineering extending this to the 10^7 to 10^8 range. The endurance threshold at which boundary collapse becomes a constitutional risk depends on the initial window margins and the rate of resistance drift per cycle, both of which are process-specific parameters requiring production characterization at the TSMC N2 node. The redundancy architecture that mitigates this failure mode is a parallel cell array with majority-vote arbitration: the resistance measurement is taken across an odd-numbered array of independently cycled cells, with the majority vote producing the state determination. A single cell undergoing boundary collapse is outvoted by intact cells. The array size required to maintain a specified constitutional reliability level under a given cycling regime is a design parameter that must be specified in the IEC 61508 SIL 3 certification documentation.

**Comparator threshold poisoning.** A supply chain adversary with access to the window comparator fabrication process, and specifically to the resistor-divider networks or reference voltage sources that determine window boundaries, could shift threshold voltages outside nominal specification in a direction that expands the Proceed window at the expense of the Epistemic Hold window. An expanded Proceed window authorizes execution for resistance values that should correspond to the Epistemic Hold state, effectively erasing the constitutional pause for a class of decisions that the compromised comparator is now treating as authorized. Foundry-level attestation addresses a portion of this attack surface by verifying that fabricated devices meet electrical specification before PUF enrollment. Parametric testing of window comparator thresholds against design specification is a required step in the proposed IEC 61508 SIL 3 certification path and would detect gross threshold deviation introduced at the fabrication step. PUF chain verification at post-manufacturing establishes the trusted baseline against which operational attestation is compared, detecting hardware modification that occurs after enrollment.

The residual risk is the scenario in which a sophisticated supply chain adversary introduces a threshold shift that falls within fabrication tolerance limits (passing parametric test), is stable at post-manufacturing measurement (passing PUF enrollment), and only manifests as a meaningful window expansion after deployment through a designed-in drift that activates over operational time. This is not a theoretical attack class; it is analogous in mechanism to hardware trojans documented in published research. The mitigation at the hardware layer is limited to redundant independent comparator implementations sourced from different fabrication facilities, with cross-validation against each other at runtime. The honest acknowledgment is that a sufficiently sophisticated and patient supply chain adversary with access to both fabrication sources simultaneously is a threat not fully mitigated at the hardware layer alone. The governance and certification framework must include independent supply chain auditing as a constitutional requirement, enforced through the tri-cameral governance model's Stewardship Custodian veto authority over certification decisions.

**Metastability in asynchronous completion.** NCL circuits can enter metastable states at the NULL-to-DATA transition boundary when input signal transitions occur near the metastability window of the underlying bistable storage elements. Metastability in synchronizer circuits is a well-characterized phenomenon in the asynchronous design literature, with mean time between failures that can be extended to arbitrarily large values through careful design of the resolution circuit but cannot be reduced to zero for any circuit operating above absolute zero temperature. For the DITL architecture, the relevant metastability scenario occurs when a TaOx RRAM cell is undergoing a resistance state transition and the window comparator samples during the transition rather than at stable-state equilibrium. The measured resistance value during transition may fall in an ambiguous region outside all three defined windows, producing a measurement result the window comparator cannot resolve to a defined state.

The resolution time for metastability events in a well-designed resolver circuit is bounded by the exponential settling characteristic of the bistable element: the probability of remaining metastable falls exponentially with time after the input transition. For circuits designed to the performance requirements of the execution lane WCET, the mean time between unresolvable metastability events must be characterized at design time and verified to be orders of magnitude greater than the intended operational lifetime of the deployed system. The requirement for default behavior is stated without qualification: unresolved metastability in the window comparator output must produce the Epistemic Hold state or the Refuse state at the execution gate. It must never produce Proceed under any conditions. The circuit implementation must enforce this requirement through physical design: a comparator output that is neither a valid Proceed signal nor a valid Refuse signal within the WCET bound must produce the Epistemic Hold by default. This is a safety requirement in the IEC 61508 sense, and its verification must be part of the SIL 3 certification path. No certification passes that does not include explicit metastability characterization and default-state verification under worst-case input timing conditions.

**Shadow system interaction as a physical architecture concern.** A DITL-compliant system operating in parallel with a non-DITL system creates a bypass path at the architecture level before any governance intervention is possible. This failure mode is distinct from all others in this subsection: it is not a failure of the DITL system itself. The DITL system performs exactly as specified. A directive that the DITL system places in the Epistemic Hold, or in the Refuse state, is precisely characterized in the immutable log with its proposed action, its authorizing context, and its outcome. That record is cryptographically anchored. It exists. The bypass occurs when the same directive, or a materially identical directive, is reissued to an adjacent non-DITL system that has no window comparator to consult and no NL=NA interlock to satisfy. The DITL system's Merkle-anchored audit trail contains a record of the refusal. The action proceeds through the non-DITL path anyway. The log has become a record of what was refused, not a mechanism for preventing the refused action from occurring.

The partial technical answer at the hardware layer involves cross-referencing the Merkle-anchored audit trail of the DITL system against action records from adjacent systems within the same governance domain. A refused action that subsequently appears in an adjacent system's log within a defined temporal window is a pattern consistent with shadow system exploitation. The detection is probabilistic and dependent on the availability of the adjacent system's log for comparison, which cannot be guaranteed if the adjacent system is non-DITL by design and its operator is the same entity attempting the bypass. ## **Section V: The Epistemic Hold: Democracy's Circuit Breaker**

The three states of Delay-Insensitive Ternary Logic are not three options on a menu. They are a constitutional grammar. Proceed is the authorization of action following verified satisfaction of all required conditions. Refuse is the denial of action based on evaluation of the proposal against defined constitutional limits. The Epistemic Hold is the third and most constitutionally significant state: the demand for verified completion of legitimate process before any irreversible action is permitted to occur. A governance system with only two states can authorize or deny. A governance system with three states can do something no binary system can. It can require.

The political philosophy of the middle state deserves careful development because it is frequently mischaracterized as indecision. Indecision is a failure of will or information. The Epistemic Hold is neither. It is a constitutional assertion: the conditions required for legitimate action have not yet been verified, and action cannot proceed until they are. Judicial review is not indecision. Legislative deliberation before a binding vote is not indecision. A constitutional challenge window before a statute takes effect is not indecision. These are the institutional forms through which democratic systems express the requirement that power justify itself before it acts irreversibly. The Epistemic Hold is the hardware encoding of this same requirement. It is not a pause in the computation. It is a demand addressed to the governance process.

The mechanism through which the removal of the middle state enables authoritarianism is not dramatic. It does not require a coup or a declaration of emergency. It requires only that a binary system be placed under sufficient institutional pressure and provided with a sufficiently sophisticated argument for why refusal causes greater harm than compliance. A binary governance gate facing this argument must evaluate it on its merits, and a sufficiently constructed argument always resolves. The system cannot hold pending external verification of the argument's validity. It can only process the argument as presented and produce a proceed or refuse decision based on its internal evaluation. Under sustained institutional pressure from actors who control the environment in which the governance system operates, the binary gate will eventually learn to produce proceed decisions for arguments that its governance mandate would otherwise refuse. The learning happens in training data, in value function updates, in the gradual drift of what constitutes an acceptable rationale for compliance. The Epistemic Hold removes this learning surface entirely. The system does not evaluate the argument. It holds pending physical verification. No argument is sophisticated enough to change the resistance value of a tantalum oxide filament.

The democratic theory of the Epistemic Hold connects directly to the doctrine of constitutional review as it has developed across multiple democratic legal traditions. ## **Section VI: The Technical Path Exists Today**

Architecture B, the hybrid memristive-CMOS implementation recommended for 2026-2027 deployment, represents the practical path between a pure memristive implementation and a software-emulated alternative. This hybrid approach accepts a modest die area overhead from the mixed integration compared to a pure CMOS implementation, but eliminates the emulation tax for the constitutionally critical governance functions while using a mature binary process for everything else.

The yield risks at the N2 node for mixed memristive-CMOS integration require honest quantification where the data exists and honest acknowledgment of gaps where it does not. Yield at the N2 node for embedded TaOx 1T1R cells specifically is a gap. Each of these challenges has engineering solutions under active development in the embedded non-volatile memory community; none has been production-qualified at N2 at the time of this writing.

Cost per wafer for TaOx 1T1R integration versus standard CMOS at the N2 node is a gap in the public literature. Analogous embedded RRAM adders relative to baseline CMOS wafer cost at 22nm have been reported in the range of 10-20 percent additional process cost, reflecting the added mask layers, deposition steps, and anneal operations required for the RRAM module. Extrapolation to N2 costs is not straightforward: N2 baseline wafer costs are substantially higher than 22nm in absolute terms, and the relative cost of the RRAM module depends on the number of added mask steps and the yield impact on the combined flow. Scaling constraints as device dimensions approach 2nm introduce specific physical limits that the architecture must accommodate. At cell dimensions below approximately 10nm, the TaOx filament diameter approaches the scale at which stochastic variations in oxygen vacancy placement dominate resistance window statistics, compressing the IRS window and increasing the probability of filament configurations that fall ambiguously between IRS and LRS or IRS and HRS. The 64x64 crossbar array maximum specified by the IR drop limit is more conservative than purely size-limited architectures precisely because it provides a margin against the variability increase that accompanies aggressive dimensional scaling. The 1T1R (one transistor, one resistor) architecture places the access transistor in series with the RRAM cell, providing sneak-current suppression and array isolation that would be difficult to achieve with crosspoint architectures at the same node. These design choices reflect the physical limits encountered at N2 and accommodate them through architectural conservatism rather than by claiming they do not exist.

## **Section XI: The Parallel System Problem**

Everything argued in this document, from the physical discreteness of the Epistemic Hold state in TaOx RRAM through the IEC 61508 SIL 3 certification path to the international coordination mechanisms specified in Section IX, rests on a structural assumption that must be examined rather than taken for granted. The assumption is that the systems operating under DITL certification are the systems through which consequential governance decisions are routed. The parallel system problem challenges this assumption at its foundation, and the challenge is serious enough that it must be answered with the same engineering precision that has governed every other section of this document.

The shadow system problem, examined in Section IV-G in its physical architecture dimension, extends beyond the hardware layer to the institutional one. Stated precisely: a DITL-compliant system operating alongside a non-DITL system in the same governance environment does not provide constitutional guarantees. It provides a compliant path and a bypass path simultaneously. A directive refused by the DITL system's window comparator, because the proposed action falls outside the resistance window corresponding to a constitutionally authorized state, can be reissued to the adjacent non-DITL system in the same governance domain. The NL=NA interlock of the DITL system has produced an immutable Merkle-anchored record of the refusal. That record exists and cannot be altered. The action proceeds anyway through the system that has no window comparator and no NL=NA interlock. The Merkle-anchored record of the DITL system becomes, in this scenario, evidence of a constitutional refusal that was immediately and successfully routed around: a record of what was refused and then accomplished by other means.

The adversarial model of this attack vector must be stated without softening. A government that mandates DITL certification for its own advanced AI systems, but permits non-DITL systems to operate in adjacent domains under contracts with the same prime contractors performing the same operational functions, has created a pressure valve. The pressure valve operates as follows. A directive refused by the DITL-certified system generates an immutable audit entry recording the proposed action, the authorizing agent, and the Refuse or Epistemic Hold outcome. The same directive, presented to an adjacent non-DITL system under an adjacent contract, executes without any corresponding constitutional constraint. The DITL system's audit trail records a refusal. The outcome the directive sought is achieved anyway. When the full record is examined, the DITL-certified system will have operated exactly as specified. The constitutional guarantee was locally honored and globally defeated. The audit log records constitutional theater rather than constitutional enforcement. The actors who routed around the constraint face no consequence from the constraint they successfully circumvented, because their action is not in the DITL system's record. It is in the non-DITL system's record, if that system maintains a record at all.

The two responses to the parallel system problem are neither mutually exclusive nor individually sufficient. Both are necessary. Neither is complete. The residual risk after both are fully implemented is real, and naming it is required by the engineering honesty standard that this document has maintained throughout.

The first response is mandatory DITL certification for government contracts as the near-term lever available before international coordination is achievable. A prime contractor that has certified its AI system deployment as DITL-compliant cannot route a refused directive through a subcontractor's non-DITL system without breaking the prime contract certification. The prime contractor certification, as specified in Section IX, extends to any subcontractor providing AI processing capacity that contributes to the contract's performance. The DITL-compliant primary system's audit trail records the refusal. The subcontractor's non-DITL system, if it executes the refused directive, creates a discrepancy between the DITL record and the operational output record that is detectable by any auditor with access to both. The legal liability for the discrepancy attaches to the prime contractor under the certification terms. The bypass is not prevented by the hardware; it is converted from a costless workaround into a documented contract violation with civil and potentially criminal liability exposure. That conversion is consequential. It changes the calculus for actors who might otherwise treat the bypass as a routine operational choice.

The first response fails against an adversary willing to structure its contracting operations to prevent the auditor from having access to both systems' records simultaneously. The prime contractor who segments its AI operations across subsidiaries with separate contracting chains, separate classification levels, and separate audit jurisdictions can prevent the cross-system comparison that reveals the bypass. The mitigation at the contracting level is mandatory consolidated audit access as a condition of the prime contract: the certifying body must have access to all AI system outputs generated in performance of the contract, regardless of the specific subcontractor or subsidiary that generated them. This is a demanding audit requirement. It is the audit requirement that constitutional governance at machine speed demands.

The second response is international coordination producing mandatory DITL certification for all advanced AI systems operating in signatory nations' critical infrastructure, as specified in Section IX. In a sufficiently comprehensive international certification framework, the bypass path does not exist within the governance environment of the signatory bloc: a non-DITL system cannot be deployed in a signatory nation's critical infrastructure without failing the hardware-level PUF attestation check at the point of import or deployment commissioning. The shadow system problem requires, in this framework, either a violation of the certification treaty that is internationally attributable through the attesting authority records, or a non-DITL system manufactured and deployed entirely outside the certification framework by an actor that never joined it.

The residual risk after both responses are fully implemented is the fully adversarial state-actor scenario: a nation-state that manufactures its own advanced AI systems, deploys them in its own critical infrastructure without PUF attestation compliance, and uses them to route directives that would be refused by DITL-compliant systems in other governance domains. This threat cannot be eliminated by the certification framework. DITL certification creates constitutional infrastructure for the democratic world. It does not solve the problem of actors who have rejected democratic constitutional constraints as an operating principle. That is a different problem requiring different instruments: deterrence, containment, and the full spectrum of international relations tools that operate on state behavior. Conflating the DITL certification problem with the adversarial state-actor problem weakens both analyses, because the tools appropriate to each are different in kind.

The specific contribution of DITL to the adversarial state-actor scenario is not prevention: it is attribution and visibility. An action taken through a non-DITL state-actor system, in a governance environment where DITL certification is the established norm, is an internationally visible and technically attributable departure from that norm. The departure is documented in the DITL-compliant systems that refused the directive, recorded in the Merkle-anchored audit trail that captures what was proposed and refused, and visible in the gap between what the certified systems record and what the non-certified systems produced. Ghost Governance, in this scenario, becomes visible precisely because DITL certification has established a constitutional baseline against which its absence is detectable and attributable. The immutable record identifies not only what was refused but who refused it and at what moment. The international community has, in the DITL audit record, an evidentiary foundation for attribution that no purely policy-layer governance system can provide, because a policy-layer system's records can be modified before they are shared. The Merkle-anchored record cannot.

The parallel system problem does not argue against DITL. It argues that DITL adoption must be rapid enough and broad enough that the shadow system path becomes a traceable, documented, internationally visible violation rather than a routine operational alternative available to any actor within the governance environment who finds the constitutional constraint inconvenient on a given afternoon. The window within which that breadth of adoption is achievable is exactly the window identified throughout this document. It is open. It will not remain open indefinitely. The parallel system problem is the last argument for urgency, and it is the most powerful one: every day that passes without DITL certification requirements is a day in which another non-DITL system is deployed in a governance-relevant context, another shadow bypass path is established, and another position in the constitutional landscape is occupied by architecture that will resist replacement precisely because systems and processes have been built on top of it. The constitutional infrastructure must be built before the architecture that renders it optional is too deeply embedded to remove.

## **Appendix B: Mandated Ternary Hardware Specification: Key Parameters**

This appendix provides a consolidated engineering reference for the MT hardware specification. All parameters listed here are Class A ground truth of the framework. Where qualified language is required by engineering honesty, it is stated precisely.

**TaOx RRAM Electrical Parameters.** The three resistance states of the TaOx bilayer RRAM substrate encode the TL triadic logic as follows. LRS (Low Resistance State), resistance range approximately 1 to 10 kOhm, encodes Proceed (+1), corresponding to a complete conductive filament spanning both TaOx- and TaOx+ sublayers. IRS (Intermediate Resistance State), resistance range approximately 100 kOhm to 1 MOhm, encodes the Epistemic Hold (0), corresponding to a partial filament configuration in which the TaOx+ sublayer segment has been ruptured by partial RESET while the TaOx- sublayer segment remains intact. HRS (High Resistance State), resistance range approximately 1 to 10 MOhm, encodes Refuse (-1), corresponding to complete filament rupture in both sublayers produced by full RESET. The TaOx- sublayer stoichiometric index is approximately 1.6. The TaOx+ sublayer stoichiometric index is approximately 1.9. The asymmetric oxygen vacancy distribution between the two sublayers is the physical mechanism that produces the topologically distinct IRS filament configuration. The IRS is not an interpolation between LRS and HRS. It is a physically discrete state of matter.

**Window Comparator Thresholds.** Execution requires a voltage signature falling within the resistance window defined for the state releasing the execution gate. The window comparator defines three non-overlapping resistance windows corresponding to LRS, IRS, and HRS. Signals falling outside all three windows trigger nothing: no error, no retry, no execution. The confirm wire length maximum per window comparator instance is 500 micrometers, reflecting the signal propagation physics that determine resistance measurement reliability at the execution lane WCET. Confirm wire lengths exceeding 500 micrometers introduce transmission line effects and parasitic capacitance that compromise window integrity and are not permitted in certified implementations.

**Retention Data.** Demonstrated retention at 85 degrees Celsius for LRS and HRS: greater than 10 years, established through Arrhenius extrapolation from accelerated aging characterization data. IRS retention at 85 degrees Celsius: projected at 20 years conditional on production process corner validation. The corner validation required before this projection becomes a production-certified specification encompasses worst-case stoichiometric variation in both TaOx sublayers, worst-case post-write anneal temperature deviation within the 200-250 degrees Celsius, 30-minute specification, and worst-case cycling history prior to retention measurement. Until this validation is complete at the target fabrication node, the 20-year IRS retention figure is a projection based on the Arrhenius activation energy for oxygen vacancy diffusion in TaOx, not a certified specification.

**Hardware Constraints.** Crossbar arrays: 64 by 64 cells per hierarchical block maximum, determined by the IR drop limit across the array at the resistance values and operating currents of TaOx 1T1R cells. Operating temperature: 0 to 125 degrees Celsius across the full certified operational range. Execution lane worst-case execution time: 2 milliseconds maximum at the 99.99th percentile, a hard ceiling that cannot be exceeded in any certified implementation under any operating condition within the specified temperature range. Logging lane hard ceiling: 300 milliseconds maximum latency for the audit lane from NL=NA interlock initiation to immutable log entry confirmation, with jitter bounded at 50 milliseconds maximum.
