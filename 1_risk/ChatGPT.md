This is a serious governance design. You are correctly targeting the real failure mode of constitutional systems: not frontal assault, but normalization drift through procedural exhaustion and “temporary exceptions.” The amendment is intellectually coherent with TL’s fail-closed philosophy. But adversarially, it introduces a different class of risk: constitutional ossification and procedural weaponization. 

1. Single veto risk

This is the largest structural risk.

You are explicitly choosing “false negative” over “false positive.” Meaning: better to block necessary change forever than allow one catastrophic survivability breach. That is philosophically consistent with NL=NA fail-closed logic. 

But operationally, unanimity creates:

* permanent freeze risk,
* hostage leverage,
* cognitive capture by one member,
* and martyr dynamics.

One stubborn but honest member becomes a constitutional deadman switch.

That may be acceptable for survivability-class changes if the framework truly treats them as quasi-sacred constitutional axioms. But then you must explicitly state:

“Survivability-class provisions are presumed immutable under normal governance conditions.”

Right now the amendment implicitly says this. It should say it directly.

Otherwise future operators will assume survivability amendments are difficult but still realistically achievable. They are not. Under this design they are effectively nuclear launch codes.

Real danger:
A legitimate flaw may later be discovered in one of the protected primitives themselves. Then one psychologically rigid member can freeze constitutional repair indefinitely.

You need one escape valve.

Not a bypass. A civilizational deadlock breaker.

Example:
After 720 days and 3 failed unanimous rounds, proposal escalates to “constitutional dissolution review,” where:

* both chambers dissolve automatically,
* full reconstitution election occurs,
* then unanimous vote required again from entirely new chambers.

Extremely painful. Still fail-closed. But not permanently frozen.

2. Survivability-class definition

This is currently under-specified and vulnerable to procedural laundering.

Biggest problem:
You rely on proposer self-classification.

That creates two attack paths:

Under-classification attack:
A member intentionally frames survivability changes as “implementation details.”

Example:
Changing:

* schema constraints,
* enum permissiveness,
* threshold semantics,
* required-field logic,
* validation defaults,
* interpretation metadata,
* “temporary compatibility layers.”

All can silently mutate constitutional behavior without touching named survivability primitives directly.

Over-classification attack:
A hostile actor labels ordinary governance proposals survivability-class purely to force proposer recusal or create paralysis.

You need an independent classification trigger.

Recommendation:
A proposal becomes survivability-class if ANY ONE of the following occurs:

* proposer declares it,
* 33% of either chamber declares it,
* automated constitutional diff engine flags protected artifacts,
* external auditor report flags constitutional impact.

Most important:
You need machine-verifiable constitutional boundary definitions.

Otherwise survivability-class becomes interpretive politics.

Given your schema-centric architecture, constitutional hashing is the obvious answer:

* protected schema hashes,
* protected endpoint signatures,
* protected enum cardinalities,
* protected required-field sets,
* protected const constraints.

If hash delta touches protected constitutional surfaces, survivability-class automatically activates.

That would fit TL beautifully.

3. Advocacy disqualification

This clause is dangerously broad.

Right now:
discussion itself can become self-destructive.

That creates chilling effects and informational suppression.

Members will avoid exploring dangerous constitutional questions because exploratory dialogue itself risks removal.

You unintentionally incentivize silence over transparency.

Worse:
The clause can become a political intimidation mechanism.

Example:
“Are you advocating this?”
“No, I’m analyzing implications.”
“That sounded supportive.”

Now governance becomes linguistic forensics.

You should distinguish:

* analytical discussion,
* exploratory modeling,
* formal advocacy,
* formal sponsorship.

Only sponsorship should trigger automatic recusal.

Maybe:
“A member becomes recusable only upon co-authorship, sponsorship, or formal endorsement entered into chamber record.”

Otherwise people stop thinking out loud. That kills epistemic integrity.

Ironically, it weakens Pillar I logic.

4. The 360-day window

The dual-window design is one of the strongest parts of the amendment.

You are introducing temporal friction against emotional governance. Excellent instinct.

Most constitutional failures occur during:

* panic,
* fatigue,
* crisis compression,
* wartime logic,
* “temporary emergency” rhetoric.

The second vote separated by time is extremely strong.

But 360 days creates operational risks:

* member death,
* incapacity,
* geopolitical disruption,
* institution collapse,
* regulatory deadlines,
* litigation clocks.

The bigger issue:
membership freeze between votes.

That can become pathological.

Example:
One member becomes comatose after vote one.
Now unanimity becomes impossible forever.

You need incapacity doctrine.

Otherwise biology becomes veto authority.

You need explicit rules for:

* death,
* permanent incapacity,
* disappearance,
* sanctions,
* imprisonment,
* communication loss,
* verified coercion.

Without this, adversaries target people, not systems.

5. Proposer return

The personal cost is probably directionally correct.

You are designing a “constitutional sacrifice mechanism.”

That is psychologically powerful.

It ensures survivability proposals are existentially serious.

But permanent discretionary exclusion creates two problems:

* chamber retaliation,
* ideological purification.

A member could become permanently exiled for responsibly raising a legitimate issue that fails.

That discourages necessary truth-telling.

Better:
Automatic restoration after failed proposal unless:

* misconduct,
* deception,
* hidden conflict,
* procedural violation,
  is independently established.

Otherwise you create a priesthood where members fear touching foundational questions.

Fear produces hidden governance, not safer governance.

Attack sequence:

Another variant:
proposal flooding.

Even failed survivability proposals consume:

* time,

* chamber stability,

You need proposal rate limiting.

Maybe:

Otherwise constitutional warfare becomes attritional.

7. Emergency gap

This is the hardest problem in the entire amendment.

Because every emergency bypass eventually becomes the real constitution.

History proves this relentlessly.

Your instinct to avoid emergency bypasses is correct.

But zero emergency path creates another risk:
external legal override.

Example:
A nation-state legally compels immediate modification under sanctions threat.

Now operators face:

* constitutional violation,
* or criminal liability.

You need a “containment mode,” not a bypass mode.

Meaning:
During existential emergency:

* system may degrade to forced State 0,
* operations narrow,
* actuation ceilings tighten,
* but survivability primitives themselves remain unchanged.

In other words:
Emergency powers may reduce capability, never expand it.

That is the key distinction.

Never allow emergency powers to authorize new Proceed pathways.

Only:

* Hold,
* Refuse,
* quarantine,
* rate limiting,
* segmentation,
* temporary suspension.

That preserves fail-closed integrity.
