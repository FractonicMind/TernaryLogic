# **L'Architecture de la Révolution: Le Jour où la Banque de France a Découvert la Vérité**

## *Or: How Six French Bureaucrats Accidentally Started a Financial Revolution Over Coffee and Cigarettes*

### A Comedy in One Furious Act

---

I am Jean-Baptiste Moreau, Directeur de la Stabilité Systémique at the Banque de France, and I can state with absolute certainty that Tuesday, December 9th, 2025, was the day our conference room achieved philosophical enlightenment, revolutionary fervor, and near-total chaos—simultaneously, naturellement.

The meeting was scheduled for 14h00 precisely. In France, we are punctual about our unpunctuality. By 14h17, everyone had arrived, which meant we were practically early.

The Salle des Délibérations—our main conference room—is a masterpiece of Second Empire excess: gilt moldings, crystal chandeliers, oil paintings of stern men who'd never heard of cryptocurrency. The mahogany table could seat twenty but held only six of us today, plus one unexpected guest who would arrive shortly and change everything.

To my left sat **Marguerite Dubois**, Governor of the Banque de France. Fifty-three, silver hair pulled back with mathematical precision, three espressos already consumed, eyes sharp enough to audit your soul. She'd survived four administrations by being brilliant, ruthless, and occasionally correct.

To my right: **Philippe Renard**, our liaison from the Ministère de l'Économie. Nervous energy incarnate. Currently on his second phone call of the meeting, speaking rapidly into his mobile while making apologetic gestures. The Élysée was nervous about something. The Élysée was always nervous about something.

Across the table sat **Dr. Claire Beaumont**, Chair of the Autorité des Marchés Financiers. Late forties, designer suit, expression suggesting she'd seen every financial crime imaginable and been bored by most. She was here because crypto was now "systemic," whatever that meant this week.

Next to her: **Antoine Lefebvre**, representing the Fédération Bancaire Française—essentially, the voice of every major bank that would resist any change whatsoever while claiming to embrace innovation. Silver hair, expensive watch, smile that never reached his eyes.

And at the head of the table, our unexpected guest: **Professor Michel Lavigne**, philosopher and occasional government consultant. Nobody remembered inviting him. He'd simply appeared, carrying a leather satchel and an expression of profound contemplation. This was not unusual. Michel appeared at approximately thirty percent of important meetings, cited Foucault extensively, and was occasionally right about things in ways nobody could explain.

"Bon," Marguerite said, crushing her cigarette in the crystal ashtray despite the 2008 smoking ban. Nobody enforced it in this room. Rules were for other people. "Jean-Baptiste. You called this emergency session. Enlighten us."

I cleared my throat. "We were supposed to review the quarterly stability metrics for—"

"MERDE!" Philippe shouted into his phone, then: "Non, non, not you, Minister, I was—yes, I understand—yes, the President is concerned—yes, we're addressing it now—" He covered the phone. "The Élysée wants updates every thirty minutes."

"Updates on what?" Claire asked, examining her nails.

"Nobody knows. Something about digital currency architecture. The Minister is convinced the Chinese are ahead of us."

"The Chinese are always ahead of us," Antoine muttered. "Until they're not. Then we pretend we were ahead all along."

"Gentlemen, lady," Michel interjected, his voice carrying the weight of continental philosophy and three decades of tenure, "before we discuss metrics, I must ask: have any of you considered the *ontological status* of financial truth in our post-digital age?"

Silence.

"No?" he continued, undeterred. "Then we shall discover it together."

The door burst open.

A young staffer—I later learned her name was Isabelle, though I prefer to think of her as "The Harbinger of Our Philosophical Awakening"—stumbled in carrying four identical leather binders. Her face was flushed, her hair escaping its professional arrangement.

"I am so sorry, I am so sorry, there was confusion at Document Services, they said third floor but there are two third floors if you count the mezzanine, and the elevator, it stopped between—"

"The binder, mademoiselle," Marguerite said, extending one elegant hand.

Isabelle frantically sorted through her stack, selected one, and handed it to the Governor with a small bow that would have been appropriate for royalty. Then she fled like Cinderella at midnight, leaving one shoe behind. (Not literally, but spiritually.)

Marguerite opened the binder. Her expression did not change, which was our first warning that something was terribly wrong. When Marguerite's expression doesn't change, entire markets have been known to tremble.

She read the title page. Then she read it again. Then she pushed it to me without a word.

I looked down:

**THE ARCHITECTURE OF ASSURED GOVERNANCE**    
**Ternary Logic as a Sovereign, Evidentiary Triadic Framework for Global Economic Systems**

Technical Standard & Research Monograph    
Version 1.0 (Release Candidate)    
December 2025

**Author: Lev Goukassian**

"This," I said weakly, "is not our quarterly stability report."

"Non?" Marguerite said, in that tone that suggested she knew perfectly well it wasn't but was curious how I would handle the situation.

"It appears to be... a technical specification for something called Ternary Logic."

"Ternary," Philippe repeated, phone finally down. "As in trois?"

"Oui."

"Three what?"

"Three... states. For economic decisions. Instead of oui and non, it adds... peut-être."

**"PEUT-ÊTRE?"** Marguerite's voice could have shattered the chandelier. "Our entire financial system already *lives* in peut-être! We don't need an architecture for maybe, we need an architecture for CERTAINTY!"

"Actually," Michel said, leaning forward with the expression of a man who'd just spotted Sartre at a café, "the introduction of a third state represents a profound epistemological breakthrough. The binary yes/no paradigm is a Western philosophical trap dating back to Aristotelian logic. This framework appears to embrace—dare I say—*Hegelian synthesis*."

Everyone stared at him.

"Please," Claire said carefully, "do not make this about Hegel."

"Everything is about Hegel!" Michel declared. "Thesis: yes. Antithesis: no. Synthesis: the Epistemic Hold! It's dialectical materialism for financial systems!"

"It's American," Antoine said darkly, pointing at the author's name. "This Goukassian. American?"

"Armenian-American, I think," I said, scanning the credentials page.

"So American," Antoine confirmed. "This is Anglo-Saxon imperialism disguised as innovation. They tried this with Basel III, they tried it with IFRS, now they try it with—what did you call it?"

"Ternary Logic."

"*Logique ternaire*," Michel corrected, making it sound sacred. "And I must point out, Antoine, that the French philosophical tradition—from Descartes through Derrida—has always questioned binary reductionism. Perhaps this American has simply codified what French intellectuals intuited centuries ago."

"So we were right first," Antoine said. "As usual."

"Exactement."

Marguerite stubbed out her cigarette and immediately lit another. "Jean-Baptiste. Read. Aloud. From the beginning. If we're going to have a philosophical crisis, we'll have it properly."

I began reading.

## **PILIER UN: L'ARRÊT ÉPISTÉMIQUE (The Epistemic Hold)**

"The first pillar," I read, my voice taking on the careful cadence of a man translating heresy, "is called the Epistemic Hold. Also known as the Sacred Zero."

"When truth is uncertain, the system enters a mandatory pause state. All economic action freezes until—"

"FREEZES?" Philippe was back on his phone. "Minister, yes, I'm reading it now—no, I don't know what it means—yes, freeze is the correct translation—"

"Not the entire economy," I clarified. "Individual transactions. If data quality fails, if the model cannot converge, if there's ambiguity in regulatory compliance, the system refuses to proceed until human oversight resolves the uncertainty."

Claire had pulled the binder toward her, scanning rapidly. "This is elegant. Look—it triggers on quantifiable thresholds. Data latency above 5%, model variance exceeding 10%, sanctions screening inconclusive. It's not arbitrary. It's *mathematical*."

"But this pause," Antoine said slowly, "this *Arrêt Épistémique*—it creates a permanent log? Of the uncertainty?"

"Oui."

"Detailing exactly what triggered it? Who must resolve it? What threshold was violated?"

"Oui."

"So if someone attempts market manipulation, and the system detects the pattern—"

"It freezes them mid-action and creates permanent evidence of the attempt," Claire finished, her eyes gleaming. "This is prosecutorial gold."

Michel was reading over her shoulder now, nodding vigorously. "You see? This is the Cartesian moment of radical doubt! *Cogito ergo pauso*—I think, therefore I pause! The system achieves consciousness of its own uncertainty!"

"Michel," Marguerite said tiredly, "it's a database trigger, not a philosophical awakening."

"Is there a difference?" he countered. "When code enforces ethical mandates, does it not achieve a form of moral agency?"

"Non."

"I disagree."

"You always disagree."

"That's because I'm always correct."

Philippe hung up. "The Minister wants to know if this threatens French sovereignty."

"How would a database trigger threaten sovereignty?" I asked.

"Because it's American? Or because it works? I'm unclear."

"Let me continue reading," I said. "Pillar Two: The Immutable Ledger."

"To anyone who checks."

"Anyone?"

The room absorbed this. The implications hung in the air like Marguerite's cigarette smoke.

"That's horrifying," Antoine said.

"For some people," Claire agreed.

"For *many* people."

"Yes, but which people, Antoine? The corrupt people or the honest people?"

"I refuse to answer on grounds that this binder might be recording me."

Marguerite had moved ahead, reading rapidly, her cigarette burning forgotten in the ashtray. When she finally looked up, her expression had changed. Still controlled, still precise, but something had shifted.

"Messieurs, dame," she said quietly. "This is not a technical specification."

"Non?" Philippe asked nervously.

"Non. This is a *constitutional framework* for economic governance. Written in code. Made executable. Made *inevitable*."

She turned the binder to show us Section IV. "Because it operates on what it calls the 'Dual-Lane Latency Architecture.' High-speed execution happens in sub-milliseconds. The subsequent anchoring to public blockchains happens in 300-500 milliseconds. The system maintains competitive speed while ensuring sovereign-grade auditability."

"Public blockchains?" Antoine's voice rose. "As in Bitcoin? Ethereum?"

"Oui."

"So our transactions would be... published?"

"Not the transactions themselves. Cryptographic proof-hashes. The governance claims. The fact that we executed correctly. Not the content, but the *integrity*."

Claire was already pages ahead. "Oh my God. Look at the GDPR compliance section. It's specifically architected for European privacy law. Pseudonymization-before-hashing. The proof-hash contains no personal data. The identity linkage is stored separately, off-chain, and can be deleted for Right to Erasure requests."

"So it's... legal?" Philippe asked hopefully.

"It's not just legal, it *solves* the blockchain-privacy paradox that Brussels has been fighting for years. The governance history persists. The personal connection can be forgotten. It's architecturally brilliant."

Michel had gone very quiet. This was unusual and therefore concerning. When he finally spoke, his voice had the quality of a man discovering scripture.

"Mes amis," he said softly, "I must tell you something. This 'Goukassian Vow' that underlies the entire framework—*Pause when truth is uncertain. Refuse when harm is clear. Proceed where truth is.*"

"Oui?" Marguerite prompted.

"This is not American philosophy. This is French Enlightenment thinking. Descartes: systematic doubt. Rousseau: the general will refusing harm. Voltaire: proceed only with verified truth. This man—this Armenian-American—has encoded the French Revolution into financial software."

Silence.

Then Philippe: "So we invented it?"

"Spiritually, yes."

"Intellectually?"

"Absolument."

"But an American documented it?"

"A minor technicality."

"So France should claim it?"

"France should *lead* it," Michel declared. "We understand what this represents in ways the Anglo-Saxons never could. This is not merely technology. This is *philosophy made executable*. This is Enlightenment principles given computational form. This is—"

"This is making my head hurt," Antoine muttered.

Marguerite stubbed out her cigarette. "Continue reading, Jean-Baptiste. I want to know exactly what we're dealing with before I call the Minister."

"So it's a bouncer," Antoine said. "For transactions."

"Michel, it's a database trigger."

"A database trigger *with morals*."

Claire was reading the implementation details. "The Signature is the system's binding declaration—proceed, refuse, or hold. The License is computational permission granted only after the Signature is validated. So: check integrity, declare intent, receive permission. It's a three-step constitutional process for every action."

"And if someone tries to skip a step?" Philippe asked.

"Architecturally impossible. The system won't execute without all three. No Lanterne test, no Signature. No Signature, no License. No License, no action. It's a logical chain that cannot be broken."

Marguerite was reading ahead now, occasionally muttering in rapid French. Her cigarette had burned to ash in the tray. She didn't light another, which meant she was either very impressed or planning violence.

"The legal implications," she said finally, "are staggering. Listen to this: 'Corporate liability shifts from proving intent to proving architectural compliance. If your system granted a License without a valid Signature and Lanterne check, that's automatically negligence. Architecturally defined negligence.'"

"Meaning?" Antoine asked warily.

"Meaning you can't argue your way out. Either the log exists and is valid, or you violated the standard. It's mathematical proof of negligence. No grey area. No negotiation. No plea bargaining."

"That's terrifying."

"For negligent people, yes."

"For *everyone*! What if the system makes an error?"

"That's why there's the Sacred Zero," Claire said. "If the system is uncertain, it pauses. It doesn't execute and hope. It stops, logs the uncertainty, and demands resolution."

Philippe was back on his phone. "Minister—yes—I'm trying to explain—it's French philosophy implemented in American code—no, I'm not being sarcastic—yes, really—"

Philippe hung up. "The Minister says Brussels is having an emergency meeting about something related to this. Nobody knows what."

## **THE REVOLUTIONARY FERVOR**

It was Michel who finally said it. We'd been reading for ninety minutes, passing the binder around, arguing about implications, chain-smoking our way through philosophical crises. The afternoon sun slanted through the high windows, painting everything gold.

He stood at the window, hands behind his back, staring out at the streets of Paris.

"Mes amis," he said softly, "we are holding a revolution."

"It's a technical document," Antoine said weakly.

"Non. It is a blueprint for a new social contract. Rousseau's *Du Contrat Social* was philosophy. The Declaration of the Rights of Man was aspiration. This—" he turned, eyes blazing, "—this is *implementation*."

"Of everything we claimed to believe! Liberty: the architecture makes you free by preventing chains of fraud. Equality: the same rules enforce for everyone, rich or poor, powerful or weak. Fraternity: shared verification, mutual accountability, collective truth."

"Michel," Marguerite said carefully, "you're having an episode."

"I'm having an *awakening*! France has spent two centuries trying to balance individual freedom with collective security. We've failed repeatedly because humans are fallible! But this framework—it enforces both simultaneously! Proceed where truth is—freedom of action! Refuse when harm is clear—collective protection! Pause when uncertain—prudent humility!"

Claire was nodding slowly. "He's right. Not about the revolution—well, maybe about the revolution—but about the balance. The Anglo-Saxons always privilege individual freedom over collective welfare. The Chinese privilege collective control over individual rights. This framework somehow achieves both."

"How?" Antoine demanded.

"By making proof the arbiter. You're free to act if you can prove you're acting correctly. The collective is protected because false proof is architecturally impossible. It's not surveillance—you're not watched. It's not trust—you're not believed. It's verification."

"*Vérification*," Michel repeated. "The French word is more beautiful. From *verus*—true, and *facere*—to make. To make truth! That's what this does! It makes truth computationally!"

Marguerite had gone very quiet. She'd lit another cigarette but wasn't smoking it, just watching it burn.

"There's a section here," she said finally, "called Strategic Recommendations. It's addressed to central banks."

"Read it," Philippe said, phone finally silent.

She did. The room grew quieter with each paragraph:

Silence.

Then Marguerite, very quietly: "What if France led?"

"Led what?" Antoine asked nervously.

"This. All of it. What if we were the first major economy to implement Ternary Logic as official standard?"

"The banks would revolt," Antoine said immediately.

"The Élysée," Marguerite interrupted, "is desperate to prove France is still relevant in the digital age. We've been eclipsed by American tech companies, Chinese infrastructure projects, German industrial might. What if we became the global leaders in verified economic governance?"

"An American documented it," Michel said firmly. "But France understands it. The philosophical foundations are French Enlightenment thinking. The revolutionary implications are inherently Gallic. We're not copying Americans. We're reclaiming our own intellectual heritage that somehow got lost in translation."

"That's ridiculous."

"That's *politics*. And it's effective."

Claire was reading the attack vectors section. "There are failure modes documented here. Custodian capture. Technical backdoors. Quantum computing threats. Semantic drift. The author doesn't claim it's perfect."

"That's *revolutionary*. It means we could actually launch a credible digital euro. Before the ECB. Before Germany. Before anyone. Paris as the center of verified monetary policy."

We build the first completely transparent, cryptographically secured monetary system! The world watches! They copy us! France becomes the model!"

Marguerite had pulled out her phone. She dialed a number from memory. When someone answered, she spoke rapidly in French:

"Claude? Yes, it's Marguerite. I need an emergency authorization for a pilot program. No, not standard. Something... unprecedented. I'll brief you in one hour. Have the technical team ready. And Claude? This stays quiet until we're ready to announce. Absolutely quiet. Yes. Thank you."

"I authorized a pilot implementation of Ternary Logic for the Banque de France's internal systems. We start with our own operations. Prove it works. Then we expand to commercial banks. Then we propose it for the digital euro."

"Of a revolutionary framework that will expose—"

Marguerite smiled. It was not a reassuring expression. "Tell them: yes. We'll need full technical support, legal authorization, and political cover. But yes. We can do this."

"And if it fails?"

"Then we fail dramatically, as France always has. But if it succeeds—*when* it succeeds—we'll have built something that changes the world."

"Of course I'm crying! I'm French! We cry at beauty! And this—" he gestured at the binder, "—this is beautiful!"

"It's a database architecture."

"What we'd forgotten," Antoine said slowly, "is that principles without enforcement are just poetry."

"Exactly! And now we have enforcement! *Architecture as law*! Code that cannot be bent or broken or ignored! It's what Napoleon tried to do with the Civil Code, but better—because code doesn't get tired, doesn't take bribes, doesn't compromise!"

Philippe was reading the governance section. "The document proposes three-way distributed governance. Technical Council, Stewardship Custodians, Smart Contract Treasury. No single point of failure. No single point of capture."

"Very French," Marguerite observed. "We've never trusted centralized authority. Not really. Not since 1789."

"So we build it distributed," Claire said. "The Technical Council can be international—best cryptographers from France, Switzerland, Singapore. The Stewardship Custodians rotate through European institutions. The Smart Contract Treasury is truly autonomous."

"Who controls it?" Antoine asked.

"Nobody," Claire said simply. "That's the point. It enforces the rules. Period. No administrator access. No emergency overrides. No political pressure can change it."

"That's terrifying."

"That's *freedom*," Michel declared. "Freedom from the tyranny of human discretion! The law applies equally because the code cannot discriminate!"

Marguerite stubbed out her cigarette. "Antoine. You've been arguing against this for an hour. But I notice you haven't left the room."

He looked uncomfortable. "I'm... considering."

"Considering what?"

"Whether transparent banking is actually better for banks than opaque banking."

"And?"

"If everyone is transparent, then transparency becomes the competitive advantage. The most trustworthy banks attract the most capital. Currently, trust is performed through marketing. This would make it mathematically provable."

"So you support it?"

"I'm saying the banking federation would need extensive consultation, detailed cost-benefit analysis, phased implementation plans, and—"

"How long?"

"Five to seven years minimum."

"You have three months."

"That's impossible!"

"Then we'll do it without the federation. The Banque de France will implement internally, prove the concept, then offer it to commercial banks as a competitive advantage. Those who adopt early will be the most trusted institutions in Europe. Those who delay will be seen as having something to hide."

Antoine paled. "That's blackmail."

"That's *economics*. Market forces. Very capitalist. I thought you'd approve."

"I approve of capitalism when it works in my favor."

"It will," Claire said. "Once you realize that verified integrity is the ultimate competitive moat. Nobody can undercut you on trust when you have cryptographic proof."

## **THE TECHNICAL REALITY SETS IN**

I cleared my throat. Everyone turned to look at me. "If we're actually doing this, we need to discuss implementation realities."

"Such as?" Marguerite prompted.

"The document specifies hardware requirements. Something called DITL—Delay-Insensitive Ternary Logic. Custom processors designed for side-channel attack resistance."

"Do we have those?" Philippe asked.

"Non."

"Can we build them?"

"Not quickly. But there's a transitional mode. We can run TL on existing hardware using secure enclaves. Intel SGX, AMD SEV. Not sovereign-grade security, but sufficient for pilot programs."

"How long to implement?"

"The core Decision Log and Immutable Ledger? Two weeks for basic functionality. The Anchoring to public blockchains? Another week. The Hybrid Shield with anti-manipulation checks? That's more complex. Maybe six weeks total for a working prototype."

"And the governance structure?"

"That's political, not technical. We need to appoint the Technical Council, select the Stewardship Custodians, deploy the Smart Contract Treasury. That's months of work."

Marguerite waved dismissively. "We start technical implementation immediately. Governance can catch up. Better to have working code and scramble for oversight than perfect governance for vaporware."

"That's backwards," Antoine protested.

"That's French. We build the barricades first, write the constitution later."

Michel laughed. "She's right! 1789—we stormed the Bastille in July, declared the Rights of Man in August! Action first, theory second!"

"That revolution ended with Napoleon," Antoine reminded him.

"And the Napoleonic Code, which governed Europe for two centuries! I'll take those odds!"

Claire was making notes. "For the pilot, we need target systems. What do we implement first?"

"The payment settlement system," I said immediately. "It's critical infrastructure, high volume, currently opaque. If we can prove TL works there, everything else follows."

"Risk?"

"If it fails, we freeze the French payment system and possibly trigger a financial crisis."

"So standard Tuesday in French banking."

"Essentially."

Marguerite pulled out a legal pad, began writing rapidly. "Jean-Baptiste, you lead technical implementation. Assemble a team. Poach from wherever necessary. Budget is unlimited."

"Unlimited?"

"This is either the future of French banking or the end of my career. Either way, I'm committed. Spare no expense."

"Claire, you coordinate with the AMF. We need regulatory flexibility for the pilot. Creative interpretation of existing rules. Can you manage that?"

"Can I creatively interpret regulations?" Claire smiled. "I'm French. It's our national sport."

"Philippe, you manage political liaison. Keep the Ministry informed but not involved. They can take credit after it works. Before that, they're simply 'aware of ongoing innovation initiatives.'"

"Plausible deniability?"

"Essential deniability."

"Antoine, you're my inside channel to the banking federation. They don't need to adopt immediately. They just need to not actively sabotage us."

"I can't promise that."

"Try."

"And you, Governor?" I asked.

"I," she said, lighting another cigarette, "will explain to the President of France why we're betting the credibility of French banking on a framework invented by a dying Armenian-American and delivered to us by accident."

"When you put it that way—"

"It sounds insane. Yes. Which is why I'm not putting it that way. I'm putting it as: France leads the global transition to verified economic governance, reclaiming our position as the intellectual center of Europe while solving the CBDC problem, the Basel III inefficiency, and the trust crisis in financial markets."

"That sounds better."

"It's the same thing. Just French."

## **THE PHILOSOPHER'S FINAL WORD**

Michel had been reading the attack vectors section. "There's something we haven't discussed."

"What?" Marguerite asked warily.

"The document acknowledges failure modes. Ways the system could be corrupted or captured. It's remarkably honest about limitations."

"The 51% Custodian Attack. If someone captures a majority of the Stewardship Custodians, they can systematically approve harmful actions while maintaining technical compliance."

"And the mitigation?"

"Randomized rotation, whistleblower protections, algorithmic auditing. But it acknowledges that if the entire ecosystem is corrupted, no architecture can fix it."

"So it's not perfect," Antoine said.

"Nothing is perfect," Michel replied. "But listen to this conclusion: 'TL doesn't prevent human corruption—it makes human corruption non-repudiable.' That's the genius! It doesn't promise utopia! It promises *evidence*!"

"Evidence that could hang half the French financial sector," Antoine muttered.

"Then perhaps half the French financial sector needs hanging," Claire said coolly.

"Easy for you to say. You're a regulator."

"I'm a regulator who's tired of investigating crimes where the evidence has been conveniently destroyed. This would make my job possible instead of theatrical."

Philippe hung up his phone. "The Minister says the President is interested. Very interested. He's inviting the Governor to the Élysée for dinner tonight. Private. Just the two of them."

Marguerite stood, gathering papers. "Then I need to prepare. Jean-Baptiste, send me the executive summary. Twenty pages maximum. Claire, send me the regulatory framework. Philippe, send me the political risk assessment. Michel—"

"Oui?"

"Send me the philosophical justification. Make it inspirational. The President likes to think of himself as a visionary."

"He shall have vision!"

"Not too much vision. Grounded vision. Practical vision."

"Practically revolutionary vision!"

"Perfect."

She moved toward the door, then paused, looking back at the leather binder still sitting on the conference table. The late afternoon sun illuminated it like a holy relic.

"Mes amis," she said quietly, "I think we just accidentally started something important."

"A revolution?" Philippe asked.

"A better word might be: renaissance. A rebirth of trust through verification. Of liberty through code. Of equality through mathematics."

"You're starting to sound like Michel," Antoine observed.

"Perhaps Michel was right all along."

"Don't tell him that. His ego is insufferable enough."

"I heard that," Michel said cheerfully.

Marguerite smiled—a real smile this time, not the controlled expression of a central banker but the genuine warmth of someone who'd just glimpsed possibility.

"One more thing," she said. "Jean-Baptiste, find this Goukassian. Reach out. Personally, not officially. Tell him France is interested. Tell him we want to discuss implementation. Tell him—"

She paused, considering.

"Tell him the French Revolution never really ended. We've just been waiting for the right architecture to finish what we started."
