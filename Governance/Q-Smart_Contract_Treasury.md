# The Treasury's Paradox: Autonomous Execution, Unresolved Dependencies, and the Hardware Imperative

## The Autonomous Fiduciary: A Self-Executing Financial Entity

The Smart Contract Treasury, as envisioned in the Ternary Logic (`TL`) framework, represents a deliberate architectural departure from traditional decentralized autonomous organization (DAO) treasuries [[24](https://offshoreincorporate.com/how-where-to-set-up-a-dao/)]. While many DAOs grant their governing bodies direct or indirect control over treasury funds for allocation and spending, the `TL` Treasury is designed as a purely reactive entity—a self-executing fiduciary with no vote rights whatsoever [[3](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12598492/), [74](https://arxiv.org/html/2410.13095v1)]. This design choice is not an omission but a core constitutional principle, intended to strictly delineate its role as the "janitor of eternity" rather than an architect of tomorrow [[4](https://www.sec.gov/files/ctf-written-pre-whitepaper-06022025.pdf), [31](https://hackernoon.com/the-day-the-house-entered-epistemic-hold-a-story-of-ternary-logic-congress-and-credible-evidence)]. Its sole function is execution, not deliberation; it collects revenue and disburses funds based on pre-encoded rules, operating autonomously once those rules are set [[101](https://www.sec.gov/files/ctf-memo-molecule-ag-cahill-gordon-reindel-llp-030226.pdf)]. This structure aims to minimize human discretion, reduce the risk of bribery or corruption, and prevent the direct capture of the treasury's assets by any single governance body [[75](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352673425000241)]. The system's integrity hinges on the premise that while the Treasury controls nothing, it executes perfectly what is sanctioned by the other two chambers of governance.

The operational mechanics of the Treasury are straightforward yet powerful. It automatically collects network fees, creating a pool of community-owned funds [[3](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12598492/), [109](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.09608v1)]. Disbursements are triggered autonomously upon the on-chain verification of predefined milestones [[31](https://hackernoon.com/the-day-the-house-entered-epistemic-hold-a-story-of-ternary-logic-congress-and-credible-evidence)]. These milestones are not arbitrary; they are codified into the Treasury's logic and represent verifiable events, such as the successful deployment of a new protocol version or the publication of an external audit report on a decentralized storage system like IPFS [[101](https://www.sec.gov/files/ctf-memo-molecule-ag-cahill-gordon-reindel-llp-030226.pdf)]. Crucially, no human approval is required for these disbursements once the rules are encoded; the smart contract executes them automatically, embodying the principle of autonomous organizing [[47](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/6570650/6780646/10891558.pdf)]. This automation is a cornerstone of its design, intended to ensure predictable and trust-minimized fund management. However, the autonomy of the Treasury is entirely conditional upon the rules it is given. The power to define these rules is deliberately segregated among the three bodies of the tri-cameral government, creating a robust three-way financial constraint that prevents unilateral action [[33](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/frank-mangone_fusaka-peerdas-activity-7402120438803402752-6Jg8)].

This constraint operates through a rigorous, multi-layered governance process known as "Joint-Approval." The Technical Council, acting as the proposers of change, initiates any modification to the Treasury's disbursement rules via a Type 3 proposal [[33](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/frank-mangone_fusaka-peerdas-activity-7402120438803402752-6Jg8)]. Such proposals are significant and include changes to disbursement rules, major cryptographic upgrades, or modifications to the core contracts themselves [[33](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/frank-mangone_fusaka-peerdas-activity-7402120438803402752-6Jg8)]. To proceed, this proposal must first pass an internal supermajority vote (>75%) within the Technical Council [[33](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/frank-mangone_fusaka-peerdas-activity-7402120438803402752-6Jg8)]. If it passes, it is then automatically and non-censorably forwarded to the Stewardship Custodians for review [[33](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/frank-mangone_fusaka-peerdas-activity-7402120438803402752-6Jg8)]. The Custodians, acting as the constitution's ethical guardians, then have the exclusive right to approve or veto the proposal. For the rule change to take effect, the Custodians must also achieve a ≥75% supermajority in favor of the proposal [[33](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/frank-mangone_fusaka-peerdas-activity-7402120438803402752-6Jg8)]. Only after both the Technical Council and the Stewardship Custodians have independently passed the same proposal with their respective supermajorities are the new rules encoded into the Treasury contract [[33](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/frank-mangone_fusaka-peerdas-activity-7402120438803402752-6Jg8)]. Once encoded, the Treasury begins executing them autonomously until a future Joint-Approved update replaces them. This dual-supermajority requirement creates a formidable barrier against malicious or ill-conceived changes, ensuring that any alteration to the system's financial operations has been vetted by both technical experts and ethical overseers [[33](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/frank-mangone_fusaka-peerdas-activity-7402120438803402752-6Jg8)].

This intricate relationship between the three bodies effectively partitions authority. The Technical Council controls the "what"—it proposes the specific conditions under which funds can be spent. The Stewardship Custodians control the "why"—they determine whether those spending conditions align with the immutable constitutional mandates [[33](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/frank-mangone_fusaka-peerdas-activity-7402120438803402752-6Jg8)]. The Treasury itself controls the "how"—it simply executes the final, jointly-sanctioned rules [[33](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/frank-mangone_fusaka-peerdas-activity-7402120438803402752-6Jg8)]. No single body can access the treasury unilaterally, and no two bodies can collude to bypass the third, as each plays an indispensable role in the rule-encoding process [[33](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/frank-mangone_fusaka-peerdas-activity-7402120438803402752-6Jg8)]. This structure is a masterful embodiment of the janitor/architect boundary. The Treasury never touches the foundation of the constitution; it only enforces its financial directives. It cannot propose changes to the Eight Pillars or the Three Mandates, nor can it make discretionary decisions about fund allocation [[33](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/frank-mangone_fusaka-peerdas-activity-7402120438803402752-6Jg8)]. Its power is entirely derived from the authority granted to it by the other two governance bodies. This strict adherence to its designated role ensures that the Treasury remains a tool of governance rather than a governing body itself, successfully avoiding the accumulation of emergent architectural authority despite its significant financial power [[4](https://www.sec.gov/files/ctf-written-pre-whitepaper-06022025.pdf)]. The system is designed so that the Treasury is the lock, and the Joint-Approval process provides the keys held by separate, non-colluding parties.

| Governance Body | Role in Treasury Management | Voting Power / Authority Level | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Technical Council** | Proposes changes to the parameters of Treasury disbursement. | Type 3 (≥75% Internal Supermajority). | As the body responsible for technical upgrades, it is best suited to propose changes to the execution logic of the Treasury contract itself. |
| **Stewardship Custodians** | Approves or vetoes proposed changes to Treasury disbursement rules for constitutional and ethical alignment. | Type 3 (≥75% Supermajority); holds a binding, non-overrideable veto. | As the ultimate guardians of the Immutable Mandates, they possess the final say on whether any financial directive is permissible under the constitution. |
| **Smart Contract Treasury** | Executes the disbursement rules autonomously upon verified milestone completion. | No Vote Rights. | Designed as a pure executor, its lack of voting rights prevents it from participating in or subverting the governance process, keeping it firmly in its "janitorial" role. |

This carefully partitioned authority is the central pillar of the Treasury's design. By giving the Treasury no voice in its own creation or modification, the `TL` architecture attempts to build a financial engine that is both highly automated and politically neutral. It is a vault with no human door, where funds move only when the complex interplay of code, governance, and cross-chain anchors agrees [[31](https://hackernoon.com/the-day-the-house-entered-epistemic-hold-a-story-of-ternary-logic-congress-and-credible-evidence)]. However, this elegant separation of powers introduces a critical dependency on external systems for verifying the very milestones it is programmed to execute. The Treasury's autonomy is contingent on a data feed—often an oracle—that connects its deterministic world to the probabilistic real world, and this connection represents its most significant architectural vulnerability.

## The Oracle Problem: Verifying Milestones Without a Centralized Authority

The core promise of the Smart Contract Treasury—to operate autonomously and trust-minimally—is fundamentally undermined by its dependence on an external data source to verify milestones. The instruction to research both oracle-free approaches and trusted oracle models highlights this central tension. A smart contract cannot directly perceive or interact with the outside world; it can only act on information written to the blockchain [[6](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/arjunvirsingh_predictionmarkets-activity-7416403636219449344-ILjQ)]. Therefore, for the Treasury to know if a milestone like "protocol upgrade vX.Y is deployed" has occurred, it must receive this information from an off-chain entity. This necessity introduces the "oracle problem," a well-documented single point of failure in decentralized finance (DeFi) and blockchain applications [[6](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/arjunvirsingh_predictionmarkets-activity-7416403636219449344-ILjQ), [61](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/pavondunbar_413-million-gone-in-one-transaction-activity-7420626080945999872-Jv3T)]. Any solution to this problem carries inherent trade-offs between decentralization, security, and cost, and the chosen path will determine the true nature of the Treasury's autonomy.

A naive implementation might rely on a single, trusted oracle service. This approach is immediately disqualified by the "No God Mode" kill criterion, which prohibits any mechanism granting a single entity ultimate unilateral control over the system [[59](https://www.scribd.com/document/663428669/CP-Harmony-Endpoint-AdminGuide), [60](https://zenodo.org/records/19133974/files/%F0%9F%9F%AACENTEL%E2%84%A2%EF%B8%8F_Dreams,%20Behavioral%20Grit,%20Lies,%20Signal%20Purity%20&%20Signal%20Drift_%20Inside%20the%20CENTEL%20Intelligence%20Adjacency%20Lab%20(Final%20v2).pdf?download=1)]. Even if the oracle is run by a reputable company like Chainlink, its use still concentrates immense power in a single point of failure [[98](https://www.gemini.com/cryptopedia/glossary)]. An adversary who compromises this oracle can manipulate the data it feeds to the Treasury, tricking it into disbursing funds based on false evidence. Real-world DeFi exploits repeatedly demonstrate the catastrophic consequences of oracle manipulation. For example, attackers have used flash loans to temporarily inflate asset prices reported by oracles, causing protocols to liquidate positions at incorrect valuations and siphoning off funds [[168](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.06348v2), [169](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/understanding-oracle-manipulation-attacks-hamed-gholami-sz9nf)]. The $8.7 million loss in the Polter Finance attack was a direct result of such a price manipulation exploit [[52](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/dmeshkov_fantom-transaction-hash-txhash-details-activity-7270761537588375554-IxPj)]. Audits of real-world asset (RWA) protocols consistently identify oracle-related risks as a critical attack vector, proving that combining on-chain logic with off-chain assets dramatically expands the attack surface [[84](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/quillaudits_top-10-rwa-attack-vectors-every-developer-activity-7422281408821399552-dVqW)]. Thus, relying on a single, trusted oracle would render the Treasury's purported autonomy meaningless and create a direct violation of its core constitutional principles.

A more sophisticated approach involves using a decentralized oracle network (DON), such as Chainlink's, to aggregate data from multiple independent sources [[48](https://www.hkma.gov.hk/media/eng/doc/key-functions/banking-stability/DLT_Research_Paper.pdf)]. This method improves security by making it harder for a single attacker to manipulate the consensus value. However, it does not eliminate the problem. The DON itself becomes a complex, high-value target. Attackers can engage in coordinated identity oracle attacks, poisoning the data streams from multiple nodes simultaneously [[51](https://www.slowmist.com/report/2024-Blockchain-Security-and-AML-Annual-Report(EN).pdf)]. Furthermore, the economic incentives for attacking a DON that governs a multi-million-dollar treasury are substantial. Attackers can use flash loan attacks to gain temporary control over enough voting power to influence the oracle's output, a technique that has been shown to be effective against various protocols [[27](https://dev.to/ohmygod/cross-chain-governance-attacks-how-flash-loaned-voting-power-becomes-the-next-nine-figure-exploit-1bk), [80](https://www.eba.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2025-01/5fe168a2-e5a6-41a1-a1b4-87a35ecebb5c/Joint%20Report%20on%20recent%20developments%20in%20crypto-assets%20%28Art%20142%20MiCAR%29.pdf)]. The complexity of these networks also introduces new vectors for error or manipulation, as demonstrated by numerous security advisories and audits [[36](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/2082889/000121390025080461/ea0254517-s1_bitwise.htm), [56](https://innovariatech.com/blog/owasp-smart-contract-top-10-2026-every-vulnerability-explained-with-real-exploits)]. While better than a single oracle, a DON still places the system's integrity at the mercy of the honesty and security of a third-party data aggregation service, which is antithetical to the goal of a truly autonomous and self-sovereign fiduciary.

Given these challenges, the most viable path forward lies in oracle-free verification methods. These approaches aim to prove that a milestone has been met using on-chain data and cryptographic proofs, thereby eliminating the need for an external, potentially compromised data provider. Several promising techniques exist. One method involves using zero-knowledge proofs (ZKPs) to allow a prover to demonstrate knowledge of certain information without revealing the information itself [[54](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.18535v2)]. For instance, a node operator could generate a ZKP showing that they have correctly executed a piece of code and updated their state accordingly, without having to reveal the entire state. This proof could then be submitted to the Treasury contract for verification [[64](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/382797463_A_Survey_on_the_Applications_of_Zero-Knowledge_Proofs)]. Another approach is to leverage commitments made on-chain beforehand. For example, before a protocol upgrade, the new code's hash could be committed to a Merkle tree stored on the blockchain. When the upgrade is ready, the new code and the corresponding Merkle proof can be submitted, allowing the Treasury to verify that the running code matches the pre-committed hash [[32](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.00243v1?ref=decipherclub.com), [43](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.00243v1)]. Similarly, milestones related to physical-world events could be tied to on-chain records, such as construction payment automation verified through robotic reality capture technologies linked to a blockchain [[100](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/354377034_Construction_payment_automation_using_blockchain-enabled_smart_contracts_and_robotic_reality_capture_technologies)]. While these oracle-free methods are technically sound in principle, they often require significant engineering overhead and may not be feasible for all types of milestones, especially those requiring real-time data like asset prices [[7](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.04583v1)].

A more radical and definitive solution, though currently theoretical, is to integrate hardware-enforced verification. This approach moves beyond software-based proofs and leverages physically secure hardware to generate attestations. Trusted Execution Environments (TEEs) like Intel SGX or ARM TrustZone can run code in an isolated enclave, protecting it from the host operating system and other processes [[16](https://openreview.net/forum?id=RHhMyaBgyw&referrer=%5Bthe%20profile%20of%20Chen-Nee%20Chuah%5D(%2Fprofile%3Fid%3D~Chen-Nee_Chuah1))]. A verifier could submit a task to a TEE, receive back a hardware-backed proof (a remote attestation quote) that the task was executed correctly, and then use this proof on-chain [[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.12095v2)]. This shifts the root of trust from a decentralized but potentially corruptible network of data providers to a physically secure hardware component. The Atomic Auditability paper goes even further, proposing Delay-Insensitive Ternary Logic (DITL) circuits that implement a hardware-enforced Escrow state [[18](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/391530298_Hardware-Enabled_Mechanisms_for_Verifying_Responsible_AI_Development)]. In this model, a computation cannot proceed until its inputs and outputs are physically recorded in a tamper-proof state, providing a hardware-level guarantee of auditability that is impossible to forge [[102](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.04712v1)]. Until such hardware solutions are widely available and integrated, however, the Treasury remains exposed. The current design, which relies on the vague concept of "verifiable on-chain milestones," is incomplete without specifying the precise mechanism for achieving this verification in a way that is consistent with the "No God Mode" constraint. Without a robust, constitutionally mandated solution to the oracle problem, the Treasury's autonomy remains an illusion, a procedural promise rather than a guaranteed property.

## Economic Attack Vectors: Funding Starvation and Zombie Governance

Beyond the architectural vulnerability of the oracle problem, the Smart Contract Treasury presents two distinct and potent economic attack surfaces that threaten the long-term viability of the Ternary Logic protocol. These threats do not necessarily require exploiting a technical flaw in the treasury's code but instead seek to manipulate the governance process or exploit the system's response to catastrophic failures. The first is a form of funding starvation, a denial-of-service attack targeting the protocol's financial health. The second is the "zombie governance" scenario, where the treasury continues to operate uncontrollably during a Layer 1 compromise, posing a severe risk of asset drain. Both vectors expose the limitations of a software-only governance model and underscore the need for robust emergency procedures or hardware-level enforcement.

The primary economic attack vector is funding starvation. The treasury is designed to be perpetually funded by collecting network fees, but its ability to spend those funds is entirely dependent on the Joint-Approval process to encode new disbursement rules [[78](https://www.mdpi.com/1911-8074/17/5/179), [79](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/rego.12659)]. TL's governance architecture requires a high threshold for passing these rules: a ≥75% supermajority internally from the Technical Council, followed by another ≥75% supermajority from the Stewardship Custodians [[33](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/frank-mangone_fusaka-peerdas-activity-7402120438803402752-6Jg8)]. While this double-supermajority hurdle provides strong protection against malicious changes, it also makes the system vulnerable to governance paralysis. An adversary, or a group of adversaries, could systematically work to prevent any new disbursement rule from being passed. They could do this through various means, including quorum griefing, where they ensure a quorum is never reached; social engineering to turn Custodian members against a necessary proposal; or outright bribery of council members. The result would be a frozen treasury: the protocol continues to collect revenue, but it cannot pay for essential operational costs like developer salaries, server hosting, or security audits. This effectively starves the project of the resources needed to maintain and evolve, leading to a gradual decline and eventual collapse [[109](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.09608v1)]. This is a known risk in on-chain governance, where proposal spam and low voter participation can lead to gridlock [[27](https://dev.to/ohmygod/cross-chain-governance-attacks-how-flash-loaned-voting-power-becomes-the-next-nine-figure-exploit-1bk)]. TL's high thresholds exacerbate this risk, making it a credible threat that the architecture must account for. Without a clear, constitutionally defined path to emergency rule changes, the treasury's funds become a liability rather than an asset.

The second, and perhaps more insidious, economic threat is the zombie governance scenario. This situation arises when the underlying Layer 1 infrastructure supporting the protocol fails catastrophically—for example, due to a 51% attack on all anchor chains, a widespread consensus failure, or a confirmed network compromise [[38](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/extropy-security-bytes-w1-w2-2026-extropy-io-ltd-rcnqf), [40](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/ffo2.70037)]. In such a state, the "No Switch Off" mandate, which constitutionally forbids pause, suspend, or kill functions, requires the Treasury contract to continue operating autonomously according to its last set of encoded rules [[31](https://hackernoon.com/the-day-the-house-entered-epistemic-hold-a-story-of-ternary-logic-congress-and-credible-evidence)]. From an on-chain perspective, the treasury is functioning perfectly; it sees no reason to halt execution. However, an adversary who has gained control of the network's consensus layer could now exploit this autonomy. They could force the treasury to disburse funds uncontrollably, draining the protocol's assets to their own benefit. The attacker would be shielded by the very features meant to protect the system: the lack of a centralized kill switch and the inability to distinguish between legitimate autonomous operation and malicious exploitation orchestrated by a compromised network layer. Nearly 80% of projects hit by such exploits never recover, highlighting the existential danger of this scenario [[38](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/extropy-security-bytes-w1-w2-2026-extropy-io-ltd-rcnqf)]. There is no built-in circuit-breaker condition or emergency procedure specified in the provided documents to halt disbursement in this case without introducing a God Mode override, which would violate the constitutional design [[31](https://hackernoon.com/the-day-the-house-entered-epistemic-hold-a-story-of-ternary-logic-congress-and-credible-evidence)]. This exposes a critical gap: the software can guarantee autonomous execution, but it cannot guarantee that the execution is happening in a safe environment. The treasury, in this context, becomes a "zombie"—it persists and acts, but its actions are no longer aligned with the will of the protocol or the safety of its assets [[39](https://www.jstor.org/stable/48539070), [41](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/mitchsharp_portfoliomanagement-programmanagement-aitransformation-activity-7446940100363452417-cCkC)].

These two attack vectors—the strategic freezing of funds and the catastrophic draining of assets—demonstrate that the Treasury's financial power, even without voting rights, creates significant economic risks. Control over a treasury, as seen in studies of DAO dynamics, is a key driver of value and influence, and thus a prime target for adversaries [[74](https://arxiv.org/html/2410.13095v1), [75](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352673425000241)]. The current software architecture offers no clear defense against either of these scenarios. Mitigating funding starvation would require designing a lower-threshold process for emergency budget approvals, perhaps triggered by a Custodian report of systemic failure. Preventing zombie governance would necessitate a hardware-level circuit-breaker or some other mechanism for detecting and responding to network-wide compromise, a problem that is notoriously difficult to solve in a decentralized system. Without such mechanisms, the treasury's design, while elegant in theory, proves fragile in practice. It creates a powerful financial instrument whose security depends on the continued proper functioning of a complex and adversarial ecosystem, a precarious position for a system aspiring to perpetual, autonomous operation.

## The Core Honest Tension: Software Promises Versus Hardware Reality

The entire architectural framework of Ternary Logic, from its governance bodies to its financial instruments, is founded upon a stark and unavoidable truth encapsulated in the "Core Honest Tension": any software can be modified by humans, advanced AI systems, or state-level adversaries with sufficient access [[164](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/zama-ai_zama-confidential-blockchain-protocol-litepaper-activity-7399745592157052928-ym5T)]. This is not a theoretical risk but an architectural fact. The promises of security, immutability, and autonomy encoded in smart contracts are only as strong as the hardware upon which they run. The sole path to genuine, inescapable enforcement of the Immutable Mandates—"No Spy," "No Weapon," and "No Switch Off"—is the Delay-Insensitive Ternary Logic (DITL) hardware architecture [[18](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/391530298_Hardware-Enabled_Mechanisms_for_Verifying_Responsible_AI_Development)]. As of Q2 2026, DITL remains a theoretical or prototype-level technology, with no indication of mass production or deployment in silicon [[164](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/zama-ai_zama-confidential-blockchain-protocol-litepaper-activity-7399745592157052928-ym5T)]. This status creates a profound disconnect: the constitution is written as if it were etched in stone, while the actual enforcement mechanism is, for the foreseeable future, a piece of parchment. The Smart Contract Treasury, like the rest of the system, exists in this transitional state, its guarantees being procedural rather than physical.

In the current software-only reality, the Treasury's autonomy is a facade. Its rules are enforced by the blockchain's consensus mechanism, but that mechanism itself is vulnerable. A 51% attack can rewrite history, MEV extraction can front-run governance votes, and a state actor could shut down the entire network [[29](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/355492521_Attacking_the_DeFi_Ecosystem_with_Flash_Loans_for_Fun_and_Profit), [126](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2106.10740)]. The "No Switch Off" mandate, which omits functions like `pause()` or `kill()`, is a classic example of this tension. On paper, it seems like a strong guarantee. However, an attacker with control over the network can achieve the same effect through economic exhaustion, forcing operators offline, or executing a contract self-destruct, which permanently removes the contract and its state [[162](https://investor.tsmc.com/sites/ir/annual-report/2024/2024%20Annual%20Report_E.pdf)]. The Goukassian Principle—"No execution signal may propagate unless the system has entered, recorded, and exited a physically enforced escrow state"—is the formal invariant that eliminates the "commit-audit gap" [[164](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/zama-ai_zama-confidential-blockchain-protocol-litepaper-activity-7399745592157052928-ym5T)]. In a binary system, there is an unprotected window between the moment a transaction is committed to the chain and the moment its effects are durably recorded in the Decision Log. This is the window in which "ghost fills" can occur in trading systems, or in which a governance action can execute without its corresponding audit evidence being permanently secured [[164](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/zama-ai_zama-confidential-blockchain-protocol-litepaper-activity-7399745592157052928-ym5T)]. In the `TL` software model, a proposal can be voted on and its logic executed, but the act of recording that vote and its justification in the immutable Decision Log may happen afterward. This creates a vulnerability where an attacker could manipulate the state during the commit phase and then have that manipulated state "ratified" by a subsequent, seemingly legitimate vote. The Epistemic Hold, which is the hardware-enforced NULL state in DITL, is the physical manifestation of this security boundary. Without it, the Decision Log is just another piece of mutable data on a mutable chain.

The transition from this fragile software layer to a robust hardware floor would transform the Treasury's capabilities. With deployed DITL hardware, the Escrow state becomes a physical voltage level (NULL / half-Vdd) that is tamper-proof and cannot be overridden by software, firmware, or OS-level access [[18](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/391530298_Hardware-Enabled_Mechanisms_for_Verifying_Responsible_AI_Development)]. Atomic Auditability, where execution and audit evidence share the same physical commit boundary, would mean that a disbursement request cannot be processed until the corresponding entry in the Decision Log has been physically secured in the Escrow state [[102](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.04712v1)]. This would eliminate the "commit-audit gap" entirely, preventing "ghost governance" where an action is executed before its evidence is immutably recorded. It would also provide a physical guarantee of the "No Switch Off" mandate, as the hardware would lack a kill circuit altogether. The Treasury's autonomous execution would no longer be a programmable feature but a physical law. This shift redefines the system's security model. Instead of trusting the honesty of the software and the participants in the network, one would trust the physical properties of the hardware itself. This is the essence of moving from a "trustless" system, which is a misnomer, to a "confidence machine" that builds institutional trust through cryptographic verification and immutable record-keeping that resists tampering over decades [[47](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/6570650/6780646/10891558.pdf), [164](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/zama-ai_zama-confidential-blockchain-protocol-litepaper-activity-7399745592157052928-ym5T)].

Therefore, the Smart Contract Treasury's design must be understood as a provisional holding pattern. It is a beautifully engineered coordination layer for a system that is ultimately destined for physical enforcement. Its current guarantees are meaningful within the bounds of a functional blockchain network but evaporate in the face of a determined, resourceful adversary capable of compromising that network. The "Janitor of Eternity" metaphor is apt: the janitor maintains the building, but the building's walls and foundation are what truly keep the elements out. Until the foundation of DITL hardware is built, the janitor is just cleaning the inside of a house that is already collapsing. The `Governance.md` document must be updated to reflect this reality, moving away from presenting the software architecture as a finished, self-contained system and toward framing it as a transitional, interim solution designed to prepare the system for its eventual physical instantiation. The current language, which treats the constitution as the final arbiter of truth, is misleading and dangerous. The truth is held in the hardware, and the software is merely its servant. The Treasury's viability as a minimum viable model that doesn't depend on software alone is therefore zero without DITL. It is only a viable transitional model because it prepares the ground for the day when hardware enforcement can finally give its promises real teeth.

## Constitutional Integrity and Long-Term Viability

Assessing the Smart Contract Treasury's place within the Ternary Logic constitutional framework requires a careful examination of its adherence to the "janitor-not-architect" principle and its resilience against long-term threats. The Treasury's design, with its complete absence of vote rights, successfully keeps it within its prescribed role [[33](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/frank-mangone_fusaka-peerdas-activity-7402120438803402752-6Jg8)]. It is a tool, not a master. It cannot propose changes to the system's rules, nor can it alter its own programming. Its authority is derived entirely from the Joint-Approval process involving the Technical Council and Stewardship Custodians. This structure correctly avoids a [CONSTITUTIONAL VIOLATION], as the Treasury never accumulates the kind of emergent architectural authority that would allow it to redesign the system. However, its financial power, even without a vote, creates a significant and unavoidable attack surface. The question of whether this constitutes a [CONSTITUTIONAL VIOLATION] is nuanced: the Treasury itself does not violate the constitution, but its existence makes the entire protocol more vulnerable, and its design contains critical flaws that leave it exposed.

The most significant threat to the Treasury's long-term viability is not a direct attack on its code, but the erosion of its foundational assumptions over time. The first assumption is the continued security and reliability of its oracle dependencies. As AI-assisted adversaries become more sophisticated, the risk of synthetic evidence generation and automated exploitation of procedural gaps increases . An AI could be trained to find subtle flaws in the logic used by an oracle or to generate convincing fake data that passes verification checks. The second assumption is the stability of the underlying blockchain infrastructure. The "any one anchor survives" liveness guarantee is predicated on the assumption that at least one anchor chain will remain uncompromised and accessible [[31](https://hackernoon.com/the-day-the-house-entered-epistemic-hold-a-story-of-ternary-logic-congress-and-credible-evidence)]. However, an adversary could launch simultaneous attacks on all five anchor chains, or more realistically, attack the cross-chain relay infrastructure to partition the network, making it appear as though the system has failed when in fact it is just inaccessible [[27](https://dev.to/ohmygod/cross-chain-governance-attacks-how-flash-loaned-voting-power-becomes-the-next-nine-figure-exploit-1bk)]. The third assumption is the continued competence and integrity of the governance bodies. Over a multi-decade timeframe, member fatigue, attrition, and normative drift could weaken the effectiveness of the Joint-Approval process, making it easier for adversaries to game the system [[75](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352673425000241)].

To enhance the Treasury's long-term survivability, several measures could be taken, all of which stay within the janitorial role:
1.  **Formalize Oracle Security Standards:** The constitution should mandate the use of specific, audited oracle architectures. This could involve requiring a combination of on-chain commitments and off-chain proofs, or mandating the use of a particular decentralized oracle network with proven security track record [[48](https://www.hkma.gov.hk/media/eng/doc/key-functions/banking-stability/DLT_Research_Paper.pdf)].
2.  **Implement Emergency Budget Mechanisms:** A constitutionally-defined process should be created for rapid, emergency disbursement of funds to cover critical operational needs (e.g., paying for server hosting) if the standard Joint-Approval process is paralyzed. This could be triggered by a Custodian report of systemic failure and require a lower quorum threshold, balancing speed with security [[27](https://dev.to/ohmygod/cross-chain-governance-attacks-how-flash-loaned-voting-power-becomes-the-next-nine-figure-exploit-1bk)].
3.  **Develop a Network Failure Detection Protocol:** The Treasury contract should be designed to monitor its own liveness. If it detects a prolonged period with no new blocks or significant delays in transaction confirmation across all anchor chains, it could enter a "time-bound epistemic hold," pausing disbursements until a quorum of Custodians confirms that the network has recovered [[31](https://hackernoon.com/the-day-the-house-entered-epistemic-hold-a-story-of-ternary-logic-congress-and-credible-evidence)].
4.  **Promote Oracle-Free Milestone Verification:** The Technical Council should prioritize proposals that replace oracle-dependent milestones with on-chain verifiable ones, such as those using zero-knowledge proofs or hardware-backed attestations [[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.12095v2), [54](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.18535v2)]. This would gradually de-risk the system's core functionality.

Ultimately, none of these software-based patches can fully resolve the fundamental issues. The oracle problem and the zombie governance scenario will persist as long as the system relies on a software-only execution environment. The honest conclusion is that the Treasury's current design is a transitional model. Its constitutional integrity is sound in its current form, but its practical viability is limited. The ultimate safeguard against all these long-term threats, including the APGT ceiling of quantum decryption and AI-driven attacks, is the eventual deployment of DITL hardware [[164](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/zama-ai_zama-confidential-blockchain-protocol-litepaper-activity-7399745592157052928-ym5T)]. The hardware floor would provide a physical enforcement layer that is resistant to the very attacks that threaten the software layer. Until then, the Treasury remains a powerful but fragile institution, its autonomy conditional on a complex and adversarial ecosystem it was designed to transcend.

## Synthesis and Verdict: A Minimum Viable Model Under Scrutiny

The Smart Contract Treasury of Ternary Logic is a bold and intellectually coherent experiment in separating financial execution from political deliberation. Its architecture, with the explicit goal of creating an autonomous fiduciary with no vote rights, successfully navigates the perilous waters between utility and centralization. By placing its financial power under the strict, joint control of the Technical Council and Stewardship Custodians, it avoids the accumulation of emergent architectural authority and adheres to the constitutional principle of the "janitor of eternity" [[31](https://hackernoon.com/the-day-the-house-entered-epistemic-hold-a-story-of-ternary-logic-congress-and-credible-evidence), [33](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/frank-mangone_fusaka-peerdas-activity-7402120438803402752-6Jg8)]. However, a comprehensive analysis reveals that as a minimum viable model that does not depend on software alone, its viability is severely compromised by fundamental flaws in its design.

The verdict on the Treasury architecture is one of cautious appraisal. Its conceptual foundation is robust, but its practical implementation is fragile. The most critical weakness is the unresolved oracle problem. The Treasury's promise of autonomous, trust-minimized execution is nullified by its dependency on external data sources to verify milestones. This reintroduces a "God Mode" vector, either through a single trusted oracle or the complex, high-value target of a decentralized oracle network, directly contradicting a core constitutional tenet [[6](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/arjunvirsingh_predictionmarkets-activity-7416403636219449344-ILjQ), [61](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/pavondunbar_413-million-gone-in-one-transaction-activity-7420626080945999872-Jv3T)]. While oracle-free and hardware-backed verification methods offer a path to resolving this, they are not part of the current software-only architecture. Until a constitutionally mandated, auditable solution is implemented, the Treasury's autonomy remains a procedural ideal, not a guaranteed property.

Furthermore, the design is susceptible to two potent economic attack vectors. The first is funding starvation, where an adversary can paralyze the protocol by preventing the Joint-Approval process from encoding new disbursement rules, leveraging the system's own high supermajority requirements against it [[27](https://dev.to/ohmygod/cross-chain-governance-attacks-how-flash-loaned-voting-power-becomes-the-next-nine-figure-exploit-1bk), [79](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/rego.12659)]. The second is the zombie governance scenario, where the "No Switch Off" mandate leads to uncontrollable fund disbursements during a Layer 1 compromise, creating a severe risk of asset drain [[38](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/extropy-security-bytes-w1-w2-2026-extropy-io-ltd-rcnqf), [40](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/ffo2.70037)]. These vulnerabilities highlight that control over protocol funding, even without a vote, creates a powerful and attractive target for adversaries. The current software-only model is brittle in the face of adversaries who can compromise the underlying network infrastructure.

The "Core Honest Tension" is the central theme of this analysis. The Treasury's guarantees are entirely contingent on the security of the software and the hardware it runs on. Without deployed DITL hardware, its promises are not physically enforced. The DITL hardware floor is not merely an enhancement; it is the prerequisite for transforming the Treasury from a well-designed software protocol into a physically guaranteed, autonomous fiduciary. The hardware would provide a physical Escrow state, eliminating the commit-audit gap and closing the loopholes that allow for ghost governance and other exploits [[102](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.04712v1), [164](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/zama-ai_zama-confidential-blockchain-protocol-litepaper-activity-7399745592157052928-ym5T)]. Until that day, the Treasury serves as a transitional model, a coordination layer that prepares the ground for the day when physical enforcement can give its promises real, unbreakable teeth.

In summary, the Ternary Logic Smart Contract Treasury is a compelling but incomplete model. Its architectural integrity is intact, and it successfully avoids becoming an architect. However, its viability as a standalone, minimum-viable system is questionable due to its oracle dependency and economic vulnerabilities. The current design is a blueprint for a future state, not a description of a present reality. The system's survival depends on either implementing robust, constitutionally-compliant solutions to its current weaknesses or, more fundamentally, on the timely deployment of the DITL hardware that forms its intended foundation.
