# SECTION 11: POST-QUANTUM CRYPTOGRAPHIC MIGRATION

## 11.0 Overview

The cryptographic infrastructure of Ternary Logic was designed to satisfy the security requirements of the present. The post-quantum migration protocol specified in this Section ensures that it continues to satisfy those requirements through the 2032 to 2040 vulnerability window and beyond. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

The migration challenge is not merely technical. It is constitutional. The Immutable Ledger contains a complete historical record of every Decision Log, every Permission Token, and every governance determination since framework deployment. That record is anchored to public blockchains through ECDSA signatures. If quantum computers achieve sufficient capability to break ECDSA through Shor's algorithm, those historical anchors become forgeable. A regulator examining a ten-year-old governance record cannot verify that the record is authentic if the cryptographic signatures that authenticated it can be retroactively forged. The governance history of the framework - the entire evidentiary foundation on which its constitutional claims rest - becomes unreliable. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

This is not a future threat to future records. Harvest-now-decrypt-later attacks are already underway. Nation-state intelligence services are systematically collecting encrypted communications and blockchain signatures today, with the expectation that quantum computers will eventually enable retroactive forgery of any ECDSA-signed record collected before the migration. Every day that TL operates without the hybrid signature protocol is a day that its historical governance record accumulates quantum-vulnerable anchors.

Section 11 specifies the complete migration architecture: the algorithm selection rationale, the hybrid signature protocol that preserves Merkle chain continuity during the transition, the automatic trigger system that prevents institutional delay from creating dangerous exposure, the multi-chain anchoring redundancy during quantum transition, and the constitutional agility protocol that embeds post-quantum readiness into the framework's operational schema from initial deployment. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

## 11.1 The Quantum Threat Landscape

Quantum computing threatens the mathematical foundations of public-key cryptography through two algorithms: Shor's algorithm for integer factorization and discrete logarithm problems, and Grover's algorithm for unstructured search. The threat profile of each differs substantially for TL's cryptographic infrastructure. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

### 11.1.1 Hash Function Resilience

SHA-256 and BLAKE3-256, the hash algorithms used for Merkle tree construction and Decision Log anchoring, retain post-quantum security against Grover's algorithm. A quantum computer running Grover's algorithm can search an unsorted database of size N in approximately the square root of N steps, reducing the effective security of a 256-bit hash from 256 bits to 128 bits. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

The 128-bit quantum security margin is computationally infeasible under any optimistic estimate of near-term quantum computing progress. Even the most aggressive projections for fault-tolerant quantum computing do not place a 128-bit Grover search within the operational horizon of the next two decades. Doubling hash output lengths to SHA-512 or BLAKE3-512 would restore the full 256-bit quantum security margin, but this is not required for the current deployment horizon and would increase ledger storage requirements and Merkle proof sizes proportionally. The hash algorithms do not require immediate replacement. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

### 11.1.2 ECDSA Vulnerability

ECDSA, the elliptic curve digital signature algorithm used for Permission Token signatures, chamber authentication, and blockchain transaction signing, does not share this resilience. Shor's algorithm solves the discrete logarithm problem on which ECDSA security rests in polynomial time on a sufficiently large fault-tolerant quantum computer. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

The security reduction is total, not partial. Whereas Grover's algorithm reduces hash security by a square root factor, Shor's algorithm reduces ECDSA security to zero once sufficient quantum hardware exists. An attacker with a cryptographically relevant quantum computer does not merely have an advantage over ECDSA verification - the attacker can derive the private signing key from any observed public key, forge arbitrary signatures on any message, and retroactively authenticate any historical record as if it had been signed by the legitimate private key holder. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

For TL's Immutable Ledger, this means that ECDSA-signed anchors on Bitcoin, Ethereum, and Polygon become forgeable. A state actor or well-funded adversary who has harvested the public keys from historical anchor transactions - which are permanently visible on all three chains - can retroactively produce signatures for any alternate transaction content, enabling the fabrication of a fraudulent governance history that cannot be distinguished from the authentic one through signature verification alone.

### 11.1.3 The Harvest-Now-Decrypt-Later Threat

The harvest-now-decrypt-later attack is already operationally active. Nation-state intelligence services with the resources and the motivation are collecting all publicly accessible ECDSA-signed data today - including blockchain transactions - with the expectation that sufficiently capable quantum hardware will become available within the decade. The collection cost is negligible. The storage cost is decreasing. The economic calculation favors collection now regardless of the uncertainty about quantum timeline. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

This means that the vulnerability window is not bounded by the date quantum computers become available. It is bounded by the date the hybrid signature protocol is activated. Every ECDSA anchor generated before hybrid activation is permanently quantum-vulnerable regardless of when quantum hardware actually arrives. An institution that delays hybrid activation by one year generates approximately thirty-one million seconds of additional quantum-vulnerable anchor accumulation. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

The automatic trigger system specified in Section 11.4 addresses this by removing institutional discretion from the activation decision. The Stewardship Custodians configure threshold parameters that trigger hybrid activation automatically when quantum readiness indicators cross defined values, preventing the economic and operational pressures that would otherwise cause institutions to defer activation.

### 11.1.4 The Vulnerability Window: 2032 to 2040

The vulnerability window for ECDSA is estimated at 2032 to 2040. This estimate represents the period between the plausible emergence of cryptographically relevant quantum computers - defined as fault-tolerant systems with sufficient logical qubits and sufficiently low error rates to run Shor's algorithm against 256-bit elliptic curves - and the completion of global migration to post-quantum algorithms. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

NSA CNSA 2.0 sets 2030 as the mandatory migration deadline for National Security Systems - a deadline set deliberately in advance of the estimated vulnerability window to ensure completion before exposure. The UK National Cyber Security Centre has characterized post-quantum cryptography migration as a global-scale change to IT and operational technology systems, noting that hardware roots of trust implementing the NIST post-quantum standards were expected to become available during 2025. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

The 2030 NSA deadline implies that TL deployments should initiate hybrid signature mode no later than 2028, providing a two-year buffer for full migration before the NSA's projected exposure onset. The automatic trigger system is designed to enforce this timeline regardless of institutional preference.

## 11.2 NIST Post-Quantum Algorithm Standards

NIST finalized three post-quantum cryptographic standards in August 2024. These standards represent the outcome of an eight-year international evaluation process that assessed over sixty candidate algorithms across three submission rounds for security strength, implementation efficiency, and resistance to both classical and quantum attacks. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

### 11.2.1 FIPS 203: ML-KEM-1024

FIPS 203 specifies Module-Lattice-Based Key-Encapsulation Mechanism, derived from the CRYSTALS-Kyber submission. ML-KEM-1024 is the highest security parameter set, providing security equivalent to AES-256 against both classical and quantum attacks. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

ML-KEM-1024 is used in TL for key encapsulation operations during the EKR ceremony: establishing the shared secret from which the ephemeral session key is derived. The module lattice security assumption means that breaking ML-KEM-1024 requires solving the Module Learning With Errors problem, for which no efficient quantum algorithm is known. The algorithm produces public keys of approximately 1568 bytes and ciphertexts of approximately 1568 bytes - larger than ECDH key exchanges but well within the bandwidth capacity of authenticated chamber communication channels. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

### 11.2.2 FIPS 204: ML-DSA (Dilithium)

FIPS 204 specifies Module-Lattice-Based Digital Signature Standard, derived from CRYSTALS-Dilithium. ML-DSA is the primary post-quantum digital signature standard and the direct replacement for ECDSA in TL's Permission Token signing, chamber authentication, and blockchain anchor transactions. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

ML-DSA at the highest security parameter set produces signatures of approximately 4627 bytes - significantly larger than the 64-byte ECDSA signature. This size increase has implications for ledger storage and blockchain transaction fees. The Merkle tree structure accommodates the larger signature sizes because each leaf hash is computed over the complete Decision Log including the full Dilithium signature, so the ledger's proof efficiency is independent of the signature size within the leaf. Blockchain anchor transactions carrying Dilithium signatures incur higher fees than ECDSA transactions, but the anchor frequency - one transaction per three-hundred-second Slow Lane cycle rather than per individual proposal - limits the fee impact to an operationally manageable level. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

### 11.2.3 FIPS 205: SLH-DSA-SHAKE-128s

FIPS 205 specifies Stateless Hash-Based Digital Signature Standard, derived from SPHINCS+. SLH-DSA provides a hash-based fallback signature option for deployments requiring long-term security without state management requirements. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

Hash-based signatures derive their security from the collision resistance of the underlying hash function rather than from algebraic hardness assumptions. Their security is therefore reducible to SHA-256 or SHAKE-128 security, which retains the 128-bit quantum security margin described in Section 11.1.1. This makes SLH-DSA conservative by design: it makes fewer mathematical assumptions than ML-DSA and is more auditable because its security proof reduces to a single well-understood primitive.

SLH-DSA's primary operational limitation is signature size: the SHAKE-128s parameter set produces signatures of approximately 7856 bytes. In TL, SLH-DSA-SHAKE-128s is designated as the fallback for Stewardship Custodian chamber signatures in the highest-value governance operations where the conservative security proof justifies the storage overhead. It is not the default replacement for ECDSA in routine Permission Token operations. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

The API Signature Block reserves slots for both ML-KEM-1024 and SLH-DSA-SHAKE-128s in addition to the primary ML-DSA slot, aligned with the NIST standards finalized in August 2024. These slots are present in the schema from initial deployment, ensuring forward compatibility without requiring a schema update at migration time.

## 11.3 The Hybrid Signature Protocol

The central technical challenge of post-quantum migration is preserving the Merkle chain continuity of the Immutable Ledger across the algorithm transition. A naive migration - replacing ECDSA signatures with Dilithium signatures on a specific cutover date - creates a ledger fork where pre-migration records are verifiable only with ECDSA and post-migration records are verifiable only with Dilithium. External auditors must maintain two verification stacks indefinitely. The cryptographic binding between pre-migration and post-migration records weakens. The constitutional chain of custody becomes ambiguous at the transition point. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

The hybrid signature protocol resolves this by requiring that every Permission Token and chamber signature carry both an ECDSA signature and a Dilithium signature throughout the defined transition window.

### 11.3.1 Hybrid Leaf Structure

During the transition window, every Decision Log leaf in the Merkle tree carries a dual signature payload. The leaf hash is computed over the complete leaf content including both the ECDSA signature bytes and the Dilithium signature bytes. This creates a cryptographic link that is valid under either algorithm independently. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

The Governance Lane verifies both signatures before issuing Proceed during the transition window. A Decision Proposal that carries a valid ECDSA signature but lacks a valid Dilithium signature receives Epistemic Hold: the evidentiary documentation is incomplete because the constitutional schema requires both signatures during the hybrid period. A proposal that carries a valid Dilithium signature but lacks a valid ECDSA signature similarly receives Epistemic Hold. Both signatures are required as long as the hybrid protocol is active.

This dual verification requirement is not a performance bottleneck in practice. ECDSA and Dilithium verification can proceed in parallel within the Governance Lane's evaluation pipeline. The total verification latency is bounded by the slower of the two operations, not their sum.

### 11.3.2 Merkle Chain Continuity

After the transition completes and the Stewardship Custodians vote through the supermajority process to retire ECDSA, the Merkle tree continues to incorporate the hybrid leaves from the transition period. External auditors can verify historical records using whichever algorithm they trust. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

The dual-signature leaf structure means that neither algorithm can be retroactively removed without breaking the hash chain. A quantum attacker who derives the ECDSA private key from a historical public key and forges an alternate ECDSA signature cannot substitute this forged signature for the authentic one, because the Merkle leaf hash was computed over both signatures simultaneously. The forged ECDSA signature, computed over different leaf content than the original, produces a different hash. The hash chain breaks. The forgery is detected. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

This chain continuity guarantee is the core constitutional property of the hybrid protocol: historical governance records remain verifiable by auditors who trust only classical cryptography, by auditors who trust only post-quantum cryptography, and by auditors who require both. No single algorithm's compromise can retroactively corrupt the governance history.

### 11.3.3 Chamber Key Migration

The Tri-Cameral chambers hold the signing keys that authenticate every Permission Token. During the hybrid protocol, each chamber must maintain both its existing ECDSA key pair and its new Dilithium key pair simultaneously. The ECDSA key pairs are not immediately retired - they continue to sign every Token during the transition window in parallel with the new Dilithium signatures.

Key migration ceremonies for chamber signing keys follow the same physical presence and Shamir quorum requirements as other sensitive key operations. The new Dilithium key pairs are generated in hardware security modules under multi-party ceremony protocols. The enrollment of the new Dilithium public keys is anchored to the Immutable Ledger before the hybrid protocol is activated, so that the chain of custody for the post-quantum keys is itself verifiable from the moment of their introduction. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

ECDSA key retirement occurs through a Stewardship Custodian supermajority vote anchored to the Immutable Ledger after the Technical Council certifies that the Dilithium implementation has been validated against the NIST CAVP test vectors and that no operational dependency on ECDSA verification remains in any deployed system component. Retired ECDSA keys are cryptographically shredded through the standard HKDF-SHA3-256 destruction protocol used for all key lifecycle terminations.

## 11.4 Automatic Migration Triggers

The automatic trigger system prevents institutional delay from extending the quantum-vulnerable ECDSA-only exposure window beyond the constitutionally safe timeline. Unlike discretionary governance decisions that require Stewardship Custodian deliberation and supermajority approval, the migration trigger system activates automatically when quantitative threshold parameters embedded in the constitutional schema are crossed. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

### 11.4.1 Quantum Readiness Parameter

The Stewardship Custodians configure and maintain the Quantum Readiness Parameter - a composite score computed from four input streams.

**NIST Certification Stream.** The parameter tracks the status of NIST post-quantum algorithm standardization. When NIST finalizes a post-quantum digital signature standard suitable for TL's Permission Token signing use case, this stream advances the parameter score. NIST finalized FIPS 203, 204, and 205 in August 2024, advancing this stream to its maximum contribution.

**Academic Cryptanalysis Stream.** The parameter tracks peer-reviewed cryptanalysis of both the current ECDSA implementation and the candidate post-quantum algorithms. New attacks on ECDSA that reduce its effective security below defined thresholds advance the parameter score toward trigger activation. Successful cryptanalysis of a candidate post-quantum algorithm that reduces its security below acceptable levels would reduce that algorithm's suitability score and could require substitution with an alternative.

**Industry Deployment Stream.** The parameter tracks the commercial availability of hardware security modules that implement the selected post-quantum algorithms with FIPS 140-3 Level 3 certification. When HSMs with NIST-standardized post-quantum algorithm support become commercially available in quantities sufficient to support Tri-Cameral chamber key migration, this stream advances the score. The UK NCSC noted that hardware roots of trust implementing the NIST standards were expected to become available during 2025. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

**Intelligence Community Assessment Stream.** The parameter incorporates publicly available assessments from national cybersecurity agencies - CISA, NCSC, BSI, and equivalent bodies - regarding quantum computing progress and recommended migration timelines. When multiple agencies simultaneously advance their migration urgency recommendations, this stream contributes to parameter advancement.

### 11.4.2 Trigger Thresholds and Automatic Actions

**Threshold 1: Hybrid Protocol Activation.** When the Quantum Readiness Parameter crosses the first defined threshold, the Governance Lane automatically initiates the hybrid signature protocol. From this point forward, every Permission Token must carry both ECDSA and Dilithium signatures for Governance Lane acceptance. The Inference Lane's proposal generation pipeline receives automatic notification to begin including Dilithium signature capability in Decision Proposals. Chamber key migration ceremonies are scheduled by the Smart Contract Treasury's algorithmic agents. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

Institutions that have not prepared their technical infrastructure for Dilithium signature generation begin receiving Epistemic Hold on all proposals from this point forward until their systems are updated. This creates a strong economic incentive for advance preparation: institutions that delay Dilithium implementation past Threshold 1 experience immediate operational disruption. The Smart Contract Treasury's fee schedule includes a transition preparation incentive that reduces governance fees for institutions that complete Dilithium readiness certification before Threshold 1 is crossed.

**Threshold 2: ECDSA Retirement Preparation.** When the Quantum Readiness Parameter crosses the second defined threshold - corresponding to conditions where independent security analysis indicates that ECDSA may be broken within a five-year horizon - the Governance Lane automatically initiates the ECDSA retirement preparation sequence. The Technical Council receives mandatory notification to begin validation of all system components' post-quantum-only operation capability. The Stewardship Custodians receive mandatory scheduling of ECDSA retirement vote. Blockchain anchor transactions begin generating single-use post-quantum accounts for the Ethereum and Polygon anchor channels. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

**Threshold 3: Full Post-Quantum Mode.** When the Stewardship Custodians complete the supermajority ECDSA retirement vote, the Governance Lane transitions to full Dilithium-only operation. ECDSA signature verification is removed from the five-layer NL=NA enforcement architecture. ECDSA signing capability is retired from all chamber HSMs through the standard key destruction protocol. Anchor transactions on all three blockchains transition to Dilithium-only signing. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

The transition from Threshold 2 to Threshold 3 is the only step in the migration sequence that requires explicit Stewardship Custodian deliberation and supermajority vote. Thresholds 1 and 2 trigger automatically without requiring human authorization. This design ensures that even if the Stewardship Custodians face institutional pressure to delay migration, the hybrid protocol activates automatically and the transition preparation sequence begins regardless of institutional preferences.

### 11.4.3 Transition Window Management

The transition window - the period during which both ECDSA and Dilithium signatures are required simultaneously - should be long enough to ensure that all system components and counterparties have migrated to Dilithium capability, but short enough to minimize the period of dual-signature storage overhead and dual-verification processing. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

The minimum transition window is twelve months: sufficient time for Technical Council validation of all deployed system components, Stewardship Custodian chamber key migration ceremonies, counterparty system updates, and regulatory notification in all applicable jurisdictions. The maximum transition window is thirty-six months, beyond which the dual-signature storage overhead becomes an operationally significant burden and the extended ECDSA retention increases the harvest-now-decrypt-later exposure window unnecessarily.

The exact transition window duration is configured by the Stewardship Custodians in the constitutional schema following Threshold 1 activation, subject to the twelve-to-thirty-six-month constitutional bounds. The chosen duration is anchored to the Immutable Ledger with the full Tri-Cameral signatures and becomes binding on all participants in the governance network.

## 11.5 Multi-Chain Anchoring During Quantum Transition

TL anchors Merkle roots to Bitcoin, Ethereum, and Polygon simultaneously. Each chain has different quantum vulnerability characteristics that affect the transition strategy for its anchor transactions. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

### 11.5.1 Bitcoin Anchor Considerations

Bitcoin's UTXO model provides some degree of natural quantum protection for unspent outputs: the public key is not revealed on the blockchain until an output is spent, so unspent outputs are protected against Shor's algorithm because there is no public key to apply the algorithm to. However, TL's anchor mechanism requires periodic transactions that expose the signing public key with every anchor publication. Each anchor transaction reveals the key for that anchor account, making it permanently quantum-vulnerable to harvest-now-decrypt-later collection. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

Bitcoin does not yet support Dilithium signatures at the base protocol layer. The hybrid protocol handles Bitcoin anchors by maintaining dual-signature content in the OP_RETURN data payload of the anchor transaction, with the transaction itself signed under the existing ECDSA protocol. The Dilithium signature is embedded in the payload rather than in the transaction signature field. This preserves the Merkle chain's hybrid leaf structure for verifiability while accommodating Bitcoin's current signature protocol constraints. When Bitcoin protocol upgrades add native post-quantum signature support, the anchor transactions can migrate to native Dilithium signing.

### 11.5.2 Ethereum and Polygon Anchor Considerations

Ethereum and Polygon anchor transactions use single-use accounts: the signing private key is generated inside the DITL coprocessor's secure enclave and cryptographically shredded immediately after the transaction confirms. The public key is revealed by the transaction and is therefore permanently quantum-vulnerable to collection, but the destroyed private key means that an adversary who later derives the private key from the public key gains only the ability to spend the now-empty account. The anchor data and the Merkle root it records cannot be retroactively modified by possession of the private key. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

Both Ethereum and Polygon support account abstraction mechanisms that enable Dilithium signature verification through smart contracts even before native protocol-level post-quantum support is added. TL's anchor contract is deployed at Threshold 1 activation to begin accepting Dilithium-signed anchor submissions alongside ECDSA-signed ones, enabling the hybrid anchor structure during the transition window.

### 11.5.3 Multi-Chain Redundancy During Transition

The specification mandates that a valid external anchor requires confirmation on at least two of the three chains during and after the quantum transition. If one chain experiences a quantum-related security incident - such as a successful Shor's attack against its signature scheme that enables reorganization of historical blocks - the other chains maintain the Merkle root record. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

If the quantum incident corrupts a chain's historical record by enabling retroactive block reorganization, the TL governance records anchored to that chain become unverifiable through that chain's history alone. The surviving chains continue to provide valid anchors, and the hybrid Merkle leaves include Dilithium signatures that are independently verifiable without reference to the compromised chain's ECDSA anchor. The redundancy ensures that no single chain's quantum failure destroys the governance record's verifiability.

## 11.6 The Constitutional Agility Protocol

Post-quantum migration is the most predictable major cryptographic transition TL will face. Future cryptographic developments may require additional migrations: advances in lattice cryptography that weaken ML-DSA, new algebraic attacks on hash functions that reduce effective security margins, or the development of new quantum algorithms that threaten primitives currently considered quantum-resistant. The constitutional agility protocol ensures that the framework can accommodate any required cryptographic transition without architectural disruption. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

### 11.6.1 Algorithm Slot Architecture

The TL API Signature Block is structured as a fixed-width header containing algorithm type identifiers alongside variable-width signature fields. Algorithm type identifiers are four-byte values drawn from a registry maintained by the Technical Council and anchored to the Immutable Ledger. The current registry includes slots for ECDSA-P256, ECDSA-P384, ML-DSA-87, SLH-DSA-SHAKE-128s, and ML-KEM-1024, as well as reserved slots for future algorithm additions. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

Adding a new algorithm to the registry does not require a schema update. It requires Technical Council certification of the algorithm's security properties against both classical and quantum attacks, Stewardship Custodian approval of the algorithm's inclusion in the registry through the standard schema modification process, and anchoring of the new registry entry to the Immutable Ledger. Once the registry entry is anchored, the algorithm is available for use in the next hybrid transition without any changes to the API schema's structural definition.

### 11.6.2 Retroactive Re-Anchoring

For historical records that were anchored using an algorithm that is later found to be broken, the constitutional agility protocol specifies a retroactive re-anchoring procedure. The Governance Lane generates a re-anchoring record: a new Merkle leaf containing the original leaf hash, the original signatures, and a new Dilithium signature computed over the concatenation of the original content and the re-anchoring timestamp. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

The re-anchoring record is appended to the Immutable Ledger as a new leaf, not as a replacement for the original. The original record remains intact. The re-anchoring leaf creates a forward cryptographic link from the original content to the new post-quantum signature without modifying any historical record. External auditors verify both the original record and the re-anchoring leaf independently. The combination provides verification under both the original algorithm - available to auditors who have not yet transitioned their verification infrastructure - and the new algorithm - available to auditors who have. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

The exact byte-level serialization of the re-anchoring record and the concatenation format for the new signature computation are specified at a high level in the Governance Complete source document and are tracked as near-term entries in the Constitutional Uncertainty Register pending Technical Council finalization of the encoding standard.

## 11.7 Institutional Preparation Requirements

The post-quantum migration places operational demands on institutions that deploy TL. These demands are not optional: they are constitutional obligations that are enforced through the automatic trigger system and the Smart Contract Treasury's penalty structure. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

**Hardware Security Module Upgrades.** Institutions must upgrade or replace HSMs that do not support ML-DSA and ML-KEM-1024 operations. The Technical Council maintains a certified HSM compatibility registry that lists approved modules. Institutions operating on non-certified HSMs receive automatic Epistemic Hold on all proposals at Threshold 1 activation. The Smart Contract Treasury's Recovery Reserve includes a hardware upgrade allocation that institutions can access through constitutional request, reducing but not eliminating the financial burden of the upgrade cycle.

**Capital Budgeting.** The ten-to-fifteen-year cryptographic horizon defines the planning window for institutional capital allocation. Organizations deploying TL must budget for the current ECDSA-based deployment and the post-quantum migration as sequential capital obligations. The Smart Contract Treasury is constitutionally required to allocate reserves for both migrations as obligations, not as discretionary IT projects. An institution that fails to budget for post-quantum migration will be unable to afford the transition when the Quantum Readiness Parameter crosses Threshold 1, forcing it either into constitutional non-compliance or into emergency borrowing that compromises its governance independence. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

**Counterparty Coordination.** Cross-border regulatory cooperation through the EKR mechanism requires that both the accessing party and the institution holding the records support compatible post-quantum signature verification. Institutions must coordinate with their principal regulatory counterparties and institutional partners to align migration timelines. The Stewardship Custodians maintain a counterparty readiness registry in the Regulatory Nexus that institutions can query to determine which of their counterparties have completed Dilithium readiness certification. Transactions with counterparties who have not completed certification at Threshold 2 activation receive Epistemic Hold pending counterparty migration confirmation.

**Regulatory Notification.** The constitutional schema modification that activates the hybrid protocol must be disclosed to all applicable financial supervisors as a material change to the institution's automated governance infrastructure. The Stewardship Custodians maintain jurisdiction-specific notification templates in the Regulatory Nexus that institutions can use to satisfy notification requirements. The notification Decision Log is anchored to the Immutable Ledger, creating a permanent record of regulatory disclosure compliance.

## 11.8 Tri-Cameral Oversight of Migration

The post-quantum migration is the largest coordinated cryptographic operation in the framework's lifecycle. Each chamber holds specific migration responsibilities that are constitutional obligations, not optional governance functions. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

The **Technical Council** monitors the Quantum Readiness Parameter inputs, evaluates post-quantum algorithm candidates against the security requirements of TL's specific use cases, validates HSM implementations against NIST CAVP test vectors, certifies the hybrid Merkle leaf structure for correctness and chain continuity, and manages the algorithm slot registry. The Council is the constitutional authority on whether a proposed migration step is technically sound. Its certification is required before any migration threshold action takes effect. [[3]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Governance/Ternary_Logic_as_Constitutional_Triadic_Governance_Architecture_for_High-Stakes_Automated_Decision-Making.md)

The **Stewardship Custodians** approve the Quantum Readiness Parameter threshold values, manage the physical chamber key migration ceremonies for Dilithium key pair generation, hold the Shamir shares required for ECDSA key destruction at retirement, maintain the counterparty readiness registry, and execute the supermajority ECDSA retirement vote. The Custodians are the constitutional authority on whether a migration step is institutionally appropriate and legally compliant with applicable regulatory requirements across all jurisdictions. [[3]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Governance/Ternary_Logic_as_Constitutional_Triadic_Governance_Architecture_for_High-Stakes_Automated_Decision-Making.md)

The **Smart Contract Treasury** manages the capital reserves for HSM upgrades and chamber key migration ceremonies, administers the transition preparation incentive fee structure, imposes operational penalties on institutions that are not Dilithium-ready at Threshold 1 activation, and maintains the automatic destruction timers for retired ECDSA key material. The Treasury's algorithmic agents execute the transition preparation fee schedule and penalty structure without requiring human quorum authorization for individual transactions - but the parameter values governing the fee schedule and penalty magnitude are set by the Stewardship Custodians through the standard schema modification process. [[3]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Governance/Ternary_Logic_as_Constitutional_Triadic_Governance_Architecture_for_High-Stakes_Automated_Decision-Making.md)

The migration's constitutional success depends on all three chambers fulfilling their mandates on the timeline defined by the automatic trigger system. A Technical Council that delays algorithm certification, a Stewardship Custodian body that defers threshold parameter configuration, or a Smart Contract Treasury that fails to maintain migration reserves are each independently capable of causing the migration to miss its constitutional deadline. The Immutable Ledger records every chamber action and every deadline, making any chamber's failure to meet its migration obligations permanently visible to external auditors, counterparties, and regulatory authorities who have subscribed to the migration status feed through the Regulatory Nexus API. ## 11.9 Migration Residual Risks

Three residual risks remain after the full hybrid protocol activation and eventual ECDSA retirement. Each is tracked in the Constitutional Uncertainty Register. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

**Pre-Hybrid Anchor Exposure.** Every ECDSA-only anchor generated before Threshold 1 activation is permanently quantum-vulnerable. The hybrid protocol protects future anchors but cannot retroactively protect historical ones. The re-anchoring procedure of Section 11.6.2 provides partial mitigation by creating forward cryptographic links from historical content to post-quantum signatures, but the original ECDSA anchors remain in the blockchain record. An adversary who collects these anchors today can attempt forgery after quantum hardware becomes available. The fundamental exposure is irreducible: it is a function of how long the institution operated before Threshold 1 activation. Early activation minimizes this exposure; delay extends it indefinitely.

**Cryptanalytic Risk to ML-DSA.** The lattice hardness assumption underlying ML-DSA has been validated by the NIST evaluation process and eight years of international cryptanalytic scrutiny. However, lattice cryptography is a younger field than elliptic curve cryptography, and the community of researchers examining its security is still growing. A breakthrough in lattice cryptanalysis - analogous to the index calculus advances that weakened older discrete logarithm implementations - could reduce ML-DSA's effective security below acceptable levels before the next migration cycle is complete. The SLH-DSA-SHAKE-128s fallback provides a hedge against this risk for the highest-value governance operations because its security reduces to hash function collision resistance rather than lattice hardness. The algorithm slot architecture of Section 11.6.1 ensures that a new signature standard can be incorporated without architectural disruption if ML-DSA requires replacement. [[6]](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/FractonicMind/TernaryLogic/refs/heads/main/Adversarial_Survivability/TL_Constitutional_Survivability_Under_Adversarial_Pressure.md)

**Correlated Migration Failure.** If all three Tri-Cameral chambers simultaneously fail to meet their migration responsibilities - because all three have been captured, because a geopolitical event disrupts the institutions hosting chamber members, or because the required HSMs are unavailable due to supply chain disruption - the migration cannot proceed and the framework enters an extended ECDSA-only vulnerability window. The distributed chamber membership across multiple jurisdictions reduces the probability of correlated failure, but it cannot reduce it to zero. The constitutional agility protocol's reserve slot architecture means that the migration can be executed with substitute algorithms if ML-DSA becomes unavailable, but the migration still requires functional chamber operation to proceed. This represents a genuine residual risk that is tracked as a long-term entry in the Constitutional Uncertainty Register.
