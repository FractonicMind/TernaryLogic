# Architectural Blueprint for Cryptographic Erasure: A Unified Framework for Regulatory Compliance and Post-Quantum Security

## Dual-Horizon Threat Model for Regulatory Compliance and Quantum Resilience

The architectural design of the Ternary Logic (TL) sovereign evidentiary governance framework necessitates a dual-horizon threat model. This model simultaneously addresses two distinct adversary classes: one representing current regulatory enforcement bodies, primarily concerned with compliance under frameworks like the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), and another representing future adversaries with advanced cryptanalytic capabilities, including the imminent threat posed by quantum computing. This bifurcated approach ensures that the resulting cryptographic erasure architecture is not only actionable and compliant today but also possesses the resilience and agility to withstand future technological shifts. The model directly confronts the structural paradox inherent in the TL architecture, where the immutable append-only ledger serves as both a pillar of accountability and a potential target for adversaries seeking to extract sensitive information or circumvent data subject rights [[23](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/navigating-gdpr-compliance-blockchain-implementations-patrick-munro-mrvsf), [50](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)]. The specification defines precise threat scenarios and corresponding defensive guarantees for each horizon, establishing a unified framework for key management and destruction that satisfies both sets of requirements through a common set of primitives.

The first horizon encompasses the threat landscape defined by current and foreseeable regulatory oversight. The primary adversary in this class is the supervisory authority of a European Economic Area (EEA) member state, acting on behalf of data subjects and empowered by regulations such as GDPR [[22](https://www.edpb.europa.eu/system/files/2023-05/final_for_issue_ov_transfers_decision_12-05-23.pdf), [55](https://www.edpb.europa.eu/system/files/2026-02/edpb_cef-report_2025_right-to-erasure_en.pdf)]. These authorities have demonstrated a clear mandate to enforce data protection principles even within technically complex systems [[20](https://www.edpb.europa.eu/our-work-tools/documents/public-consultations/2025/help-make-gdpr-compliance-easy-organisations_en)]. A significant challenge arises from Article 17 of the GDPR, which grants individuals the "right to erasure." In a system built upon an append-only, cryptographically sealed ledger, physical deletion of data is architecturally impossible [[35](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/15/16/9191)]. An adversary operating under this regulatory paradigm would seek to trigger such an erasure event, either on behalf of a legitimate data subject or as part of a broader investigation into the system's operations. The core threat is not one of overt attack but of navigating the system's governance controls to achieve a desired state of data unrecoverability. The specification acknowledges that the legally accepted technical equivalent for achieving this erasure in the context of immutable ledgers is "crypto-shredding"—the destruction of cryptographic keys rendering ciphertext permanently inaccessible without compromising other data [[23](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/navigating-gdpr-compliance-blockchain-implementations-patrick-munro-mrvsf)]. This approach is supported by the pragmatic stance of the European Data Protection Board (EDPB) in its guidance on blockchain implementations [[23](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/navigating-gdpr-compliance-blockchain-implementations-patrick-munro-mrvsf)] and has been referenced in binding decisions concerning data processing and breach notifications [[45](https://www.edpb.europa.eu/gdpr-articles/article-32-security-processing_en), [53](https://www.edpb.europa.eu/system/files/2024-01/annex_one_stop_shop_case_security_data_breaches_en.pdf)].

However, the specification also identifies a residual risk: some jurisdictions or supervisory authorities may not yet formally accept crypto-shredding as a valid substitute for physical deletion [[19](https://www.edpb.europa.eu/system/files/2026-04/edpb-annual-report-2025_en.pdf)]. To mitigate this legal uncertainty, the architecture mandates a proactive disclosure requirement. Any deployment of the TL architecture relying on crypto-shredding for erasure compliance must explicitly document this mechanism to the relevant supervisory authorities before it becomes operational [[21](https://www.edpb.europa.eu/system/files/2026-01/edpb_recommendations202601_bcr-p_v1_en.pdf)]. This creates a transparent record of the chosen technical solution and facilitates dialogue with regulators, aligning with the EDPB's emphasis on stakeholder engagement and dialogue [[20](https://www.edpb.europa.eu/our-work-tools/documents/public-consultations/2025/help-make-gdpr-compliance-easy-organisations_en)]. The scope of any commanded destruction event is strictly controlled to prevent collateral damage. An erasure request targets only the derived key material associated with a specific data subject's pseudonym within a given epoch [[8](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/5503868/10799540/10711270.pdf)]. This granular control ensures that the erasure of one subject's data does not affect the recoverability of other subjects' data or any trade-secret content protected under a different key within the same encrypted epoch [[12](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/5503868/5751207/10711270.pdf)]. This precision is critical for maintaining both compliance and the confidentiality of proprietary information. The guarantee is enforced by a zeroization primitive applied to specific key material scopes: either the entire epoch key, leading to the crypto-shredding of all data in that epoch, or a single subject-derived key, leading to the targeted erasure of that subject's data. Both actions are governed by the same invariant: a Destruction Event must be cryptographically logged to the Immutable Ledger before the zeroization occurs, ensuring that even the act of erasure is auditable [[32](https://s3.amazonaws.com/lumenlearning/success/tx/sanjac/Business-Computer-Applications_12-12-16.pdf)].

The second, forward-looking horizon models adversaries with advanced cryptanalytic capabilities, particularly those leveraging near-term quantum computers. The work of Shor in 1994 established the theoretical vulnerability of widely used public-key cryptosystems like RSA and Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC) to quantum algorithms, creating a pressing need for post-quantum cryptography (PQC) [[17](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0167404826000933), [37](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/benvolkow_pqc-cnsa-federal-activity-7442918367176425472-v72G)]. While symmetric encryption remains robust, the threat extends beyond just decryption to include signature forgery and key establishment compromise. The TL architecture adopts a pragmatic migration strategy that isolates the most vulnerable components. Symmetric encryption continues to use AES-256-GCM, which provides a sufficient security margin against Grover's algorithm due to its 128-bit post-quantum security level [[37](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/benvolkow_pqc-cnsa-federal-activity-7442918367176425472-v72G)]. The migration effort is therefore focused exclusively on public-key primitives, specifically transitioning key encapsulation mechanisms (ML-KEM) and digital signature algorithms (SLH-DSA) to standardized PQC alternatives [[18](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/lewiscombs_openssl-prepares-for-a-quantum-future-with-activity-7315684721701580800-QrEH), [30](https://www.redbooks.ibm.com/redpieces/pdfs/sg248525.pdf)]. This phased approach minimizes complexity and avoids premature replacement of secure algorithms.

A central tenet of the architecture is the preservation of forward secrecy. The threat model requires that the compromise of an epoch key at time N must never enable the decryption of ciphertexts generated in epoch N-1 or any earlier epoch [[7](https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3492336)]. This is achieved through a rigorous key lifecycle policy. Each new epoch key is derived from a combination of inputs that includes fresh, non-recoverable entropy and the cryptographic commitment (Merkle root) of the previous epoch [[59](ftp://ftp.cs.princeton.edu/pub/cs226/autocomplete/words-333333.txt)]. Most critically, when an epoch rotates, the key material for the preceding epoch must be subjected to a hardware-backed zeroization process, not merely overwritten in software. This irreversible action guarantees that past secrets are truly destroyed, preventing retrospective decryption by an adversary who may later compromise a more recent key [[38](http://lonepatient.top/2026/03/05/arxiv_papers_2026-03-05.html)]. The HRoT's `hrot_zeroize()` function is the designated mechanism to execute this guaranteed destruction, enforcing the `-1` (Block) state in the ternary logic model. The architecture also incorporates hash agility to manage the transition from classical hash functions like SHA3-256 to potential future PQC hash constructions. By encoding the algorithm identifier within the `info` field of the HKDF for each epoch, verifiers can independently determine the correct derivation path for any given log entry. This allows for a smooth, per-epoch transition without requiring a full re-encryption of the historical ledger, a critical operational advantage [[16](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.5555/983680)]. This comprehensive threat model, spanning both present-day regulatory demands and future technological threats, forms the foundational justification for the subsequent specifications of the key hierarchy and Hardware Root of Trust.

## Key Hierarchy Architecture with Pseudocode and State Transition Diagrams

The key hierarchy architecture is the operational engine of the Ternary Logic cryptographic erasure framework, designed to provide granular control over data accessibility while enforcing the principles of forward secrecy and targeted destruction. It consists of three primary components: the epoch key, which secures all data within a temporal boundary; the subject-derived keys, which grant access to data for individual data subjects; and the audit keys, which provide a bounded-access capability for independent verifiers. The lifecycle of these keys is governed by a formal state machine that maps directly to the TL ternary logic states, ensuring that cryptographic operations are only permitted when the system is in a validated and trusted state. This section provides a detailed specification of the key derivation processes, supported by pseudocode and formal state transition diagrams to meet implementation-grade standards.

Epoch key derivation is the foundational step, creating a unique, temporally-bound key for every operational period. The derivation formula is specified as a Key Derivation Function (KDF) based on HKDF-SHA3-256, which provides a robust and standard-compliant method for generating cryptographic keys from input key material [[11](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.5555/562056)]. The input key material (IKM) is a concatenation of three distinct sources of entropy and state information:
1.  **HRoT Counter Attestation Quote:** A hardware-signed statement from the Hardware Root of Trust (HRoT) verifying the current value of its monotonic epoch counter. This binds the key to a specific point in the system's operational timeline and provides a strong guarantee of uniqueness [[4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0167404824005601)].
2.  **Heartbeat Entropy:** A continuous stream of randomness provided by a dedicated True Random Number Generator (TRNG). This entropy is refreshed periodically throughout the epoch to prevent predictability and ensure high min-entropy [[24](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9268/11/4/36)].
3.  **Previous Epoch Merkle Root:** The Merkle root hash of the final data batch committed in the immediately preceding epoch. This creates a cryptographic chain of custody, where each epoch's key is dependent on the integrity of the previous one [[59](ftp://ftp.cs.princeton.edu/pub/cs226/autocomplete/words-333333.txt)].

The salt for the KDF is defined as the 64-bit big-endian representation of the epoch number, providing a unique salt for each epoch boundary. The `info` field is a structured string containing the governance domain identifier, the algorithm identifier (e.g., "SHA3-256"), and the label "EKR-EPOCH". The output of this KDF is a 256-bit epoch encryption key ($EK_n$).

```pseudocode
/**
 * Derives the epoch encryption key for the current epoch.
 * @param hr_ot_quote: The HRoT's signed attestation quote for the current epoch counter.
 * @param heartbeat_entropy_256: 256 bits of min-entropy from the TRNG.
 * @param prev_epoch_root: The Merkle root of the last batch in the previous epoch.
 * @param current_epoch_number: The sequential number of the epoch to be derived.
 * @return A 256-bit epoch encryption key Ek_n.
 */
function DeriveEpochKey(hr_ot_quote, heartbeat_entropy_256, prev_epoch_root, current_epoch_number):
    // Concatenate inputs to form the IKM
    IKM = hr_ot_quote || heartbeat_entropy_256 || prev_epoch_root
    
    // Create salt: 64-bit big-endian epoch number
    salt = int_to_big_endian_64(current_epoch_number)
    
    // Create info: governance_id || alg_id || "EKR-EPOCH"
    alg_id = "SHA3-256" // Example; could be PQC algorithm ID
    info = governance_domain_id || H(alg_id) || H("EKR-EPOCH") // H is SHA3-256
    
    // Derive the 256-bit epoch key using HKDF-SHA3-256
    Ek_n = KDF(IKM=IKM, salt=salt, info=info, output_len=32 bytes)
    
    return Ek_n
```

Once derived, the epoch key enters a lifecycle managed by the TL state machine. The following state transition diagram illustrates this lifecycle, mapping each state to its corresponding ternary logic gate status.

| State | Ternary State | Description |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Genesis | 0-Transient | HRoT attestation and governance ceremony entropy collected. Key derivation in progress. Gate is closed. |
| Active | +1 | Epoch key derivation complete. Key material is available and loaded into the ternary-binary interface. Gate is open. |
| Rotation-Pending | +1 | New epoch key derivation has begun. Current key remains active until the boundary is crossed. Gate is open. |
| Escrowed | -1 | Current epoch key has been zeroized. It is no longer accessible for operational use but may exist in an encrypted, custodian-held escrow. Gate is closed. |
| Zeroized | -1 | Epoch key has been irreversibly destroyed via the HRoT zeroization primitive. No further recovery is possible. Gate is permanently closed. |

The next component is the subject-derived key, which enables the encryption and decryption of data associated with a specific data subject. These keys are not stored persistently but are deterministically derived on-demand from the currently active epoch key ($Ek_n$) and the subject's unique pseudonym ($P_s$). The pseudonym is itself a cryptographic commitment already stored in the Decision Log, ensuring its integrity and immutability. The derivation uses a Pseudo-Random Function (PRF) based on HMAC-SHA3-512 to produce a per-subject key ($SK_{n,s}$).

$$
SK_{n,s} = PRF(Ek_n, P_s || \text{epoch\_number}_n)
$$

This derivation employs hardened child key generation principles, meaning that the compromise of a parent key (the epoch key) does not facilitate the derivation of keys from previous epochs or siblings [[56](https://aclanthology.org/events/acl-2025/)]. The epoch number is included as a context string to ensure that a key derived for one epoch cannot be mistakenly used in another, preserving forward secrecy across epoch boundaries. The pseudocode for this derivation is straightforward:

```pseudocode
/**
 * Derives a subject-specific decryption key from the epoch key and pseudonym.
 * @param epoch_key: The 256-bit epoch encryption key Ek_n for the target epoch.
 * @param subject_pseudonym: The subject's pseudonym Ps, committed to the ledger.
 * @param epoch_number: The sequential number of the epoch.
 * @return A subject-specific key Sk_ns.
 */
function DeriveSubjectKey(epoch_key, subject_pseudonym, epoch_number):
    // Concatenate the pseudonym and epoch number
    context = subject_pseudonym || int_to_big_endian_64(epoch_number)
    
    // Derive the subject key using the epoch key as the PRF secret
    Sk_ns = PRF(k=epoch_key, v=context)
    
    return Sk_ns
```

Erasure is implemented by destroying the ability to re-derive this key. This is managed through a Subject Derivation Table, which contains mappings that permit the re-derivation of $SK_{n,s}$. When a command for erasure is received, the corresponding entry in this table is destroyed, effectively making the subject's data unrecoverable without impacting any other subject or the trade-secret content within the same epoch [[57](http://lonepatient.top/2026/01/16/arxiv_papers_2026-01-16)]. The table itself must be securely stored in HRoT-controlled memory, with access gated by the ternary state machine.

Finally, the architecture includes a separate derivation branch for audit keys, granting independent auditors access to decrypt epoch contents without compromising the overall key hierarchy. The audit key ($AudK_n$) for an epoch is derived from a master secret held exclusively by the auditing body and a chain code, using the epoch number as context.

$$
AudK_n = PRF(\text{master\_audit\_secret} || \text{chain\_code}, \text{epoch\_number}_n)
$$

This construction ensures that auditors can decrypt all content for their assigned epoch but cannot derive the epoch key ($Ek_n$) itself, nor can they pivot to derive adjacent epoch keys ($AudK_{n±1}$), thus preserving forward secrecy and limiting their access to a bounded window [[12](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/5503868/5751207/10711270.pdf)]. The pseudocode for this is analogous to the subject key derivation.

```pseudocode
/**
 * Derives an audit key for a specific epoch.
 * @param master_audit_secret: The master secret held by the auditor.
 * @param chain_code: A fixed chain code for the auditor's derivation tree.
 * @param epoch_number: The sequential number of the epoch to be decrypted.
 * @return An audit key AudKn.
 */
function DeriveAuditKey(master_audit_secret, chain_code, epoch_number):
    // Concatenate the master secret and chain code
    IKM = master_audit_secret || chain_code
    
    // Derive the audit key using the epoch number as the context
    context = int_to_big_endian_64(epoch_number)
    AudKn = PRF(k=IKM, v=context)
    
    return AudKn
```

The lifecycle of a subject-derived key is simple: it is created on-demand when needed and destroyed when the corresponding subject is erased. Its state is transient, existing only within the duration of a session or transaction. ## Hardware Root of Trust Specification with Platform Divergence Analysis

The Hardware Root of Trust (HRoT) is the cornerstone of the Ternary Logic architecture, providing the foundational layer of security upon which all cryptographic operations and state transitions depend. The specification establishes TPM 2.0 as the primary baseline for the HRoT, defining its required functionalities and interfaces. However, to ensure practical applicability, it also explicitly documents the critical differences in capability and performance—particularly attestation latency—when compared to the secondary compatibility targets: the Thales Luna 7 HSM and the RISC-V Keystone TEE. This divergence analysis is not an afterthought but a core architectural constraint, directly influencing the enforcement of the "Fast Lane Prohibition" and the overall system's performance profile. All HRoT interactions are abstracted through a consistent interface, including `hrot_get_counter()`, `hrot_attest_counter()`, and `hrot_zeroize()`, which implementations must bind to the concrete hardware mechanisms of their chosen platform [[1](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3342195.3387532), [2](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9292/13/13/2508)].

For the TPM 2.0 baseline, the HRoT epoch counter is implemented using a Non-Volatile (NV) Index with the `TPMA_NV_COUNTER` attribute enabled [[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2505.12995v1)]. This attribute ensures that the index acts as a monotonic counter that can only be incremented, not reset or decremented, thereby providing a reliable, hardware-attested measure of the number of epochs since the device's initial provisioning. The sequence of operations to advance and attest the counter is standardized: `TPM2_NV_Increment` is called to atomically increase the counter value, followed by `TPM2_NV_Certify` to generate a signed endorsement quote from the TPM's Endorsement Key (EK). This quote cryptographically binds the newly incremented counter value to the TPM's identity and its current Platform Configuration Register (PCR) values, providing a verifiable attestation of the system's state at that specific moment [[4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0167404824005601)]. The specification mandates that the authorization policy for this NV Index be configured to prevent incrementation by any entity other than the designated system agent, thwarting counter manipulation attacks [[24](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9268/11/4/36)]. Furthermore, the physical implementation must incorporate a tamper mesh that, upon detecting fault injection or side-channel probing attempts, triggers the `hrot_zeroize()` primitive, ensuring that any attempt to physically compromise the HRoT results in the immediate and irreversible destruction of all key material [[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.10876v2)].

The choice of TPM 2.0 introduces a significant performance characteristic: latency. The round-trip time for a TPM 2.0 `NV_Certify` operation is measured in milliseconds, typically ranging from 5 to 15ms depending on the underlying hardware and system load. This latency is not an incidental detail; it is a deliberate feature of the TPM 2.0 baseline that enforces a clean separation between the low-latency "Fast Lane" and the higher-latency "Governance Lane." The Fast Lane is reserved for sub-2ms decision-making paths and is explicitly prohibited from performing direct HRoT NV access [[39](https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/2694344.2694372)]. Instead, the Fast Lane operates using epoch keys cached within a Trusted Execution Environment (TEE) shielded memory. The slow, synchronous HRoT attestation process is confined to the Governance Lane, which executes asynchronously once per 30-second Merkle batch closure [[5](https://ec.europa.eu/research/participants/documents/downloadPublic?documentIds=080166e5cfd3eb3b&appId=PPGMS)]. This architectural segregation prevents high-latency cryptographic operations from impacting the real-time performance of the system. Violating this prohibition by attempting to perform HRoT access in the Fast Lane path would trigger a state transition to `State 0-Suspended`, placing the system in a suspended, non-operational state pending governance intervention [[4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0167404824005601)].

In stark contrast, the secondary platforms, the Thales Luna 7 HSM and the RISC-V Keystone TEE, offer significantly lower attestation latencies, often less than 1ms [[2](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9292/13/13/2508)]. This performance difference is fundamental and must be addressed explicitly in the specification rather than being abstracted away. While this speed might seem advantageous, it poses a direct threat to the architectural integrity enforced by the Fast Lane Prohibition. An implementation using a low-latency HSM or TEE could theoretically circumvent the prohibition by performing rapid, repeated HRoT attestations within the Fast Lane path, thereby undermining the separation of concerns between real-time operations and governance-level verification. Therefore, the specification must mandate that any platform claiming sub-millisecond attestation latency must have its usage strictly governed by the TL state machine to ensure the prohibition is not violated. Conformance testing must include checks to verify that the system's behavior adheres to the mandated lane separation regardless of the underlying HRoT hardware's raw performance. The following table summarizes the key divergent characteristics of the specified HRoT platforms.

| Feature / Constraint | TPM 2.0 (Baseline) | Thales Luna 7 HSM | RISC-V Keystone TEE |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Use Case** | General-purpose platform security [[44](https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/978-981-15-8083-3.pdf)] | High-security key storage & cryptographic acceleration [[49](https://de.scribd.com/document/307506856/Die-Liste-Der-Abkurzungen-Fur-Den-IT-Bereich)] | Embedded/edge Trusted Execution Environment (TEE) [[31](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9292/14/9)] |
| **Attestation Latency** | High (5-15ms) [[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2505.12995v1)] | Very Low (<1ms) [[2](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9292/13/13/2508)] | Very Low (<1ms) [[1](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3342195.3387532)] |
| **Architectural Role** | External security co-processor | Dedicated cryptographic accelerator | Integrated processor extension |
| **Implementation Complexity** | Standardized interface, well-understood | Vendor-specific APIs, potentially complex licensing | Open-source frameworks available, requires custom SoC integration [[1](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3342195.3387532)] |
| **Enforcement of Fast Lane Prohibition** | Physically enforced by high latency | Must be logically enforced by software state machine | Must be logically enforced by software state machine |

To further harden the HRoT, the specification details requirements for heartbeat entropy sourced from the TRNG [[24](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9268/11/4/36)]. This entropy source must adhere to the stringent requirements of NIST SP 800-90B [[29](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2025-06/ENISA_Technical_implementation_guidance_on_cybersecurity_risk_management_measures_version_1.0.pdf)]. Specifically, the system must perform a startup health test to gather 256 bits of min-entropy before deriving the first epoch key. During operation, a continuous health test must collect 64 bits of min-entropy every 100ms. A failure in either test constitutes a critical security event, triggering an automatic emergency rotation of the epoch key and transitioning the system to `State 0-Suspended`. This state halts all log encryption operations until the issue is resolved by governance personnel. The TRNG itself must be physically isolated on a separate power domain from the main processing pipeline to prevent network-based attacks from injecting predictable values into the entropy stream [[24](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9268/11/4/36)]. Side-channel leakage from the TRNG's power consumption is mitigated by the aforementioned physical tamper mesh, which initiates a full system zeroization upon detection of anomalies [[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.10876v2)]. The combination of a monotonic counter, secure key derivation, and robust entropy sourcing provides the HRoT with the necessary properties to serve as the ultimate arbiter of trust and state within the TL architecture.

## Timing Budget, Entropy, and Ternary-Binary Interface Governance

The operational integrity of the Ternary Logic architecture is contingent upon strict adherence to timing constraints, rigorous entropy standards, and a precisely defined interface between the governing ternary logic and the underlying binary cryptographic accelerators. These elements collectively ensure that the system operates securely and predictably, fulfilling its dual mandate of supporting high-throughput decision-making in the "Fast Lane" while maintaining robust governance and auditability in the "Governance Lane." The Fast Lane Prohibition, a mandatory architectural constraint, dictates that direct interaction with the Hardware Root of Trust (HRoT) for operations like counter attestation is forbidden within the low-latency Fast Lane path [[39](https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/2694344.2694372)]. This forces a clean separation of concerns, where immediate decisions rely on cached keys, and HRoT-dependent operations are handled asynchronously. The following sections detail the timing budget, the specification for heartbeat entropy, and the governance of the ternary-binary interface.

A comprehensive timing budget is essential for validating that the system meets its performance requirements, particularly the 2ms maximum latency for the Fast Lane path. Every micro-operation within this path must be accounted for to ensure the total execution time remains within the prescribed limit. The budget table below quantifies the expected latency for each component of a Fast Lane operation, along with its contribution to the total budget and its corresponding hardware path. The total latency for the Fast Lane path is approximately 0.2ms, which is well within the 2ms threshold, allowing for a significant safety margin. This budget is predicated on the assumption that the epoch key is successfully retrieved from the TEE's shielded memory cache, avoiding the much higher latency of HRoT interaction.

| Operation | Latency | Budget vs. 2ms Fast Lane | Hardware Path |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Epoch key cache read | < 0.1ms | 5% | TEE shielded memory |
| Nonce derivation | ~0.1ms | 5% | DITL ternary state gate |
| AES-256-GCM encryption | ~0.05ms | 2.5% | AES-NI accelerator |
| Heartbeat entropy read | ~0.05ms | 2.5% | TRNG buffer |
| **Total Fast Lane Path** | **~0.3ms** | **~15%** | — |

Conversely, operations in the Governance Lane, which are not bound by the 2ms Fast Lane constraint, include HRoT counter attestation and the closing of a Merkle batch. HRoT counter attestation is a high-latency operation performed once per Governance Lane cycle (e.g., every 30 seconds) and is the primary reason for separating this lane from the Fast Lane. As specified previously, its latency varies significantly by platform: 5-15ms for TPM 2.0 and less than 1ms for the Thales HSM or RISC-V TEE [[1](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3342195.3387532), [2](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9292/13/13/2508)]. Closing a Merkle batch involves hashing the accumulated log entries and anchoring the resulting root to the immutable ledger, a process that combines software computation with the HRoT quote and is estimated to take approximately 10ms [[46](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel7/7935773/7943554/07943556.pdf)]. These operations occur asynchronously and do not block the Fast Lane, but they represent the governance overhead required to maintain the system's long-term integrity and compliance.

The quality of the random numbers used in key derivation is paramount to the system's security. The specification mandates that the heartbeat entropy source comply with the guidelines in NIST SP 800-90B, which provides statistical tests and evaluation methods for random number generators [[29](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2025-06/ENISA_Technical_implementation_guidance_on_cybersecurity_risk_management_measures_version_1.0.pdf)]. The entropy source must deliver a minimum of 256 bits of min-entropy for the initial startup test and 64 bits of min-entropy for each subsequent continuous test interval (e.g., every 100ms) [[24](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9268/11/4/36)]. The system must continuously monitor the health of the entropy source. A failure in either the startup or continuous test triggers a critical security event. This event causes the system to immediately halt log encryption operations, enter the `State 0-Suspended` state, and initiate an emergency rotation of the epoch key to mitigate the risk of using a compromised or degraded key [[24](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9268/11/4/36)]. The physical isolation of the TRNG on a separate power domain from the main CPU and memory bus is a critical architectural requirement to prevent external network-based attacks from manipulating or predicting the entropy stream [[24](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9268/11/4/36)]. Side-channel vulnerabilities related to the TRNG's power consumption are addressed by the HRoT's physical tamper mesh, which initiates a full zeroization if anomalous activity is detected [[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.10876v2)].

The governance of the interface between the ternary state machine and the binary cryptographic accelerator is a crucial hardware-software co-design aspect. The ternary logic does not perform computations but acts as a dynamic gatekeeper over hardware pathways. The specification defines the semantics of this interface with absolute clarity. The epoch key must be physically gated from the AES-256-GCM engine. When the ternary state register holds `+1` (Execute), the key bus is enabled, allowing the key to flow to the accelerator for encryption or decryption. When the state is `0` (Hold), the key bus must be physically disconnected or gated at the hardware level, making the key completely inaccessible to the accelerator, not merely logically denied [[39](https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/2694344.2694372)]. If a transition from `+1` to `-1` (Block) is triggered by a tamper event, the state change must take effect within a single clock cycle, interrupting any in-progress AES pipeline operation if necessary. This immediate and unconditional closure of the key gate is what makes the `-1` state a true "block."

The timing of these state transitions is governed by strict rules. A transition from `0` to `+1` must be completed and stable before the AES engine begins its key scheduling phase. Conversely, a transition from `+1` to `-1` must be propagated to the key gate within one clock cycle of receiving the trigger signal, regardless of the AES engine's internal pipeline state. This ensures that there is no window during which the key is exposed while the system is not in a fully trusted state. The specific physical implementation of these gates—for example, the voltage levels, impedance changes, or transistor-level switches required—is deferred to the Hardware Architecture specification [[39](https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/2694344.2694372)]. However, the logical requirements and timing constraints defined here are absolute and must be met by any compliant hardware implementation. This tight coupling between the logical state machine and the physical hardware interface is what gives the Ternary Logic architecture its power, transforming it from a simple state tracking mechanism into an active security enforcement tool.
